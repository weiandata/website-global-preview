# A12 · EAP, MLE, WLE: What's the Difference Between These Three Ability Estimation Methods?

> IRT Advanced Series. The consequences of choosing the wrong ability estimation method are often underestimated. This article explains everything in one go.

## The Same Model, Three "Scores"

After the IRT model is calibrated, "estimating a θ for each person" seems to be just one step, but in reality, you face at least three mainstream methods: Maximum Likelihood (MLE), Expected A Posteriori (EAP), and Weighted Likelihood (WLE/Warm Estimation). **The same response can yield different scores from these three methods, with differences so significant that they can alter downstream conclusions.**

## The Mechanisms of the Three Methods

**MLE (Maximum Likelihood Estimation)**: Find the θ that maximizes the probability of "this person's response pattern."
Properties: Asymptotically unbiased (with more items); **no solution for all correct or all incorrect** (likelihood is monotonic, θ tends to infinity) — must be patched (truncated or use another method); high variance with few items, and biased estimates that are more extreme than the true value at the extremes.

**EAP (Expected A Posteriori)**: Bayesian approach — give θ a prior (usually N(0,1) or a conditional prior from latent regression), and take the mean of the posterior distribution.
Properties: Solutions exist for all correct or all incorrect, stable numerically, and typically has the smallest mean squared error (which is why it's the default for large-scale assessments and CAT). The cost is **shrinkage**: estimates are pulled toward the prior mean, and the more items there are, the less shrinkage. Consequences: At the individual level, extreme ability individuals are underestimated or overestimated toward the center; **at the group level, the variance of EAP scores is systematically smaller than the true ability variance** — using EAP scores to calculate variance, correlations, and group differences will be contaminated by this shrinkage (underestimation).

**WLE (Warm Weighted Likelihood Estimation)**: A bias correction for MLE — multiply the likelihood by a weight related to the information function to counteract the first-order bias of MLE.
Properties: Approximately unbiased (this is its biggest selling point), usually has finite solutions for all correct or all incorrect, and no shrinkage. **WLE is the commonly used choice for reporting individual scores in large-scale assessments** (e.g., PISA's early years used WLE to provide feedback to schools), because "unbiasedness" is a key property for individual reporting.

## A Quick Reference Table

| | MLE | EAP | WLE |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Bias | Biased toward extremes | Shrinkage toward prior (biased inward) | Approximately unbiased |
| All correct/all incorrect | No solution | Has a solution | Usually has a solution |
| Mean squared error | Relatively large | **Smallest** | Moderate |
| Score variance | Relatively large | **Smaller (shrinkage)** | Close to true value |
| Dependence on prior | No | Yes | No |
| Typical use | Traditional/theoretical analysis | CAT real-time estimation, general point estimation | Individual score reporting |

## Practical Decision: Choose Based on Purpose

**1. Reporting scores and generating reports for individuals** → Prioritize WLE (unbiasedness is key for individual fairness), EAP is a close second (but understand the direction of shrinkage and explain the meaning of the extremes to users).

**2. Real-time estimation for CAT** → Use EAP (stable numerically, performs well with few items); final test scores can be rechecked with WLE.

**3. Using scores for secondary statistical analysis (variance, correlation, regression, subgroup comparison)** → **Be cautious: all three point estimates are suboptimal.** MLE/WLE carry measurement error (attenuation effect), and EAP carries additional shrinkage. The correct approach increases in rigor:

- Most rigorous: Modeling latent variables in one step (SEM/latent regression, measurement error is handled within the model);
- Standard for large-scale assessments: True value PV + Rubin merging (see the latent regression article);
- A less rigorous alternative: Use WLE + reliability-corrected attenuation, and explain the limitations.

**"Using EAP as true scores for correlation" is the most common systematic error in secondary analysis of assessments**, and the direction of the error can be expected: all relationships are underestimated.

**4. Ranking/selection scenarios** → The rankings from the three methods are highly consistent (under a unidimensional model, EAP/WLE rankings are also close to total score rankings), so method choice is not sensitive — simply choose EAP, which is numerically stable.

## Two Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **The prior for EAP should be consistent with latent regression**: If latent regression was used during calibration (conditional prior), EAP should also be based on the same model — using the wrong prior will lead to incorrect shrinkage direction; EAP with background variables for individual scoring also raises fairness concerns (same response, different background → different scores), and individual reporting scenarios typically avoid this (another advantage of WLE);
2. **The definition of standard error**: The SE for MLE/WLE comes from the information function, while the SE for EAP is the posterior standard deviation — the meanings are different, and mixing them can distort the judgment of "who is measured inaccurately." Clearly specify which one is used in technical reports.

## One-Sentence Summary

Ability estimation methods are not just arbitrary dropdown options in software: **use WLE for individual reporting, EAP for real-time adaptive testing, and PV/latent variable modeling for statistical inference** — writing this principle into your technical plan can avoid an entire category of "results that cannot be interpreted" incidents.
