# 02 · From "How Many Questions Right" to "Seeing Ability": The Pitfalls of Traditional Total Score Method, and How IRT Fixes It

> Who doesn't know how to calculate a total score? Yet precisely because it's so convenient, we often forget how much information it quietly discards. This article is specifically about pointing out its flaws.

## Total Score Method: An Old Friend We've Used Since Childhood

From primary school to postgraduate exams, our scoring method has remained largely unchanged: one point for each correct answer, and then sum them up for the total score. Simple, transparent, and fair for everyone.

This method has a professional name: **Classical Test Theory** (CTT). It has dominated the measurement field for half a century and is still widely used today—classroom quizzes, company questionnaires, and quick surveys all rely on it.

So we need to clarify: **The total score method isn't wrong; it just has limitations.** Once you want to do a few "more refined" things, it will reveal its flaws. Below are four of the most critical pitfalls.

## Pitfall 1: Two "12 Points" Can Be Worlds Apart

On a 20-item test, both person A and person B answered 12 questions correctly, resulting in a total score of 12. In the eyes of the total score method, they are the same.

But let's look at the details:

- **Person A**: Got all 12 easy questions right and got all the difficult ones wrong.
- **Person B**: Mixed correct and incorrect answers, got two easy questions wrong, but answered two extremely difficult questions that only a few students could solve correctly.

You can feel intuitively that person B's level is likely higher. He was able to tackle the tough questions that others couldn't, but may have carelessly missed two easy ones. However, the total score method discards all this information about the "response pattern" and only leaves a dry "12."

**How does IRT fix this?** IRT doesn't just look at how many questions you got right; it also considers "which questions you got right and which you got wrong." Getting difficult questions right adds more points to your ability estimate. If someone gets all the easy questions wrong but answers all the difficult ones correctly, the model will raise an eyebrow, saying "this data seems suspicious"—this is precisely a clue to detect careless responding, cheating, or guessing (we'll discuss this in posts 49 and 46).

## Pitfall 2: Scores Don't Recognize People When the Test Changes

Xiaoming scored 85 on one test, but 70 on a more difficult test. Has he regressed?

You can't answer that because **the two tests have different difficulty levels, and the scores are not comparable.** The total score is a hybrid of "person × this test," so when the test changes, the score fluctuates.

This is a fatal flaw in projects that require "continuous tracking." National-level large-scale literacy assessments change the test items every year (old items leak or become outdated), but the conclusions are always about "how much improvement there was compared to last year." If scores fluctuate with the test, such year-to-year comparisons become like castles in the air.

**How does IRT fix this?** IRT separates a person's ability (θ) from the item difficulty (b), treating them independently. As long as a few identical "anchor items" are included in the two tests, all items can be aligned on the same scale—so "this year θ = 0.4" and "last year θ = 0.2" can be meaningfully compared. This skill of aligning different tests onto the same scale is called "equating."

## Pitfall 3: Item Difficulty Changes Depending on the Group

In the total score method, an item's difficulty is defined as its pass rate (how many people answered correctly). That sounds reasonable, but here's the problem:

The same item, when given to a group of top students, has a pass rate of 90%, looking "easy"; when given to a group with weak foundations, the pass rate is 30%, looking "difficult." **It's still the same item, but the difficulty label changes depending on who takes it.** Whether the item is difficult or not isn't up to you—it depends on who takes it.

**How does IRT fix this?** Under the assumption that the model holds, an item's parameters do not change with the group. Whether it's a group of top students or an average group, the estimated item difficulty b should remain stable. This property is called "parameter invariance"—it's not magic, and it only holds if the model fits well and the sample is large enough. However, it's the fundamental reason why an item bank can be built.

## Pitfall 4: It Pretends to Be Accurate for Everyone

The total score method gives you a reliability coefficient, assuming the same error for everyone. But in reality, a test can be much less precise for different levels of ability.

For example: A ruler that only marks whole centimeters works fine for measuring a desk, but it's a disaster for measuring the diameter of a hair. You can't expect it to be equally accurate for all sizes.

**How does IRT fix this?** IRT uses an "information function" to draw a curve that clearly tells you "where I measure accurately and where I measure imprecisely." If you need to make critical decisions around the passing score, you can specifically check whether the curve is accurate enough in that range and even add more items in that difficulty range.

## Do We Still Need the Total Score Method? — Yes, and We Can't Do Without It

By now, you might think the total score method is completely useless. Quite the opposite. It has zero barriers to entry, can be calculated with just dozens of people, and even Excel can do it. It's so intuitive that it doesn't need explanation. In mature projects, the two methods are actually in a **relational** role: first use the total score method as a simple indicator to quickly screen out obviously bad items, and then move on to IRT for fine-tuning.

To put it in a hospital analogy: **The total score method is the stethoscope, and IRT is the MRI.** Hospitals need both—neither replaces the other, but they serve different purposes depending on what you're looking for.

## One-Phrase Conclusion

> **The total score method answers "how many questions did you get right," while IRT answers "what is your actual level, what are the qualities of each item, and how to fairly compare different tests." Use the former if it's sufficient, and the latter is the standard for big projects.**
