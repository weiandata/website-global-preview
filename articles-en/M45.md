# 45 · How to Present IRT Results to Non-Technical Bosses and Clients

> Can you calculate is one thing; can you explain is another. This article is the final soft skill in our application series — how to turn a pile of θ, information curves, and DIF into something bosses and clients can understand and use.

## First, Set the Right Mindset

**Your audience doesn't care how you calculated it — they care what it means for them.**

The biggest mistake technical people make is turning a report into a "show-off session" — outputting formulas, terminology, and curves, while the audience nods along, but actually understands nothing. In the end, they only remember "this person is smart, but I don't know what they said."

A good report does the opposite: **hide the technical details and present the conclusions and value.** Clients want decision-making evidence, not a statistics class.

## Three-Step Reporting Method: Conclusion → Evidence → Recommendation

No matter how complex the analysis, you can always structure it this way:

**Step 1: Start with the conclusion (one sentence).**
Speak plainly and directly. Instead of "we estimated discrimination and difficulty using the 2PL model," say:

> "This questionnaire has good overall quality, but 5 items should be removed, and it doesn't measure high-level individuals accurately."

One sentence, and the boss immediately gets the point.

**Step 2: Provide supporting evidence (pick the key points and explain in plain language).**
Explain where the conclusion comes from in a way that the audience can understand. For example:

> "These 5 items, regardless of the test-taker's level, are mostly answered correctly or incorrectly by chance, so they don't help distinguish between individuals (low discrimination). For the high-level group, since the items are generally not difficult, most of them score perfectly, and we can't tell who is stronger (insufficient information)."

**Don't mention** a, b, or information functions — but explain their **meaning** thoroughly.

**Step 3: Provide actionable recommendations.**
The end goal of a report is "what to do next":

> "Recommendations: 1. Remove these 5 items; 2. Add a few more difficult items to measure high-level individuals accurately; 3. Retest after the modifications to verify."

**Make the conclusion clear, the evidence convincing, and the recommendations actionable** — that's what makes a successful report.

## Use the "Translation Toolkit" Effectively

Translating technical terms into plain language has a few handy tools:

- **Metaphors** (the main strength of this manual): "IRT is like an MRI, while ordinary scoring is like a stethoscope" (Chapter 2); "information curves are like hourly weather forecasts, telling you where the measurement is accurate and where it isn't" (Chapter 13). A good metaphor beats ten terms.
- **Wright Map** (Chapter 15): This "chart where people and items are on the same scale" requires almost no explanation. Laypeople can see for themselves "there are no items in this range, and high-level individuals aren't measured accurately." **A chart that lets the audience see the problem on their own is a reporting superpower.**
- **Percentiles and levels** (Chapter 42): "Better than 78% of peers" or "at the proficient level" — more intuitive than any raw score.
- **Comparison charts / trend charts**: "This year's score is 8 points higher than last year's" or "this school is above the district average" — managers are most sensitive to trends and rankings.

## Three "Don'ts" Red Lines

1. **Don't use raw technical terms.** "θ = 0.6, SE = 0.2" or "3PL model fits RMSEA = 0.05" — unless the audience is a peer, these terms only create distance. Translate into plain language (Chapter 42).
2. **Don't pretend to be 100% certain.** When you should say "this case is close to the threshold, and we recommend combining with other information for judgment," say it (Chapters 21 and 42). **Honesty about uncertainty actually builds trust**; presenting estimates as ironclad rules leads to a complete loss of credibility once challenged.
3. **Don't overload in one go.** Stick to 2–3 core conclusions in one report — don't list all 50 item parameters. **Focus on the key points, not everything.**

## Tailor Your Message to Different Audiences

The same results can be presented differently depending on the audience:

- **For bosses / managers**: Focus on value, cost, risk, and decision-making. "This method can reduce the questionnaire from 60 to 40 items, saving one-third of the testing cost, with almost no loss in accuracy" (Chapter 43) — they care about the return on investment.
- **For client stakeholders**: Focus on delivery, reliability, and compliance. "We conducted fairness checks to ensure that the items treat urban and rural students equally" (Chapter 23) — they care about "whether this work is reliable and can withstand scrutiny."
- **For peers / technical contacts**: You can then use technical terms and details, since they understand them and need them for verification.

## A Report Template You Can Use Directly

> "**Conclusion**": [One sentence that clearly states the strengths and issues].
> "**Reason**": [Explain the key findings in plain language or with a metaphor, and include a Wright Map or trend chart].
> "**Recommendation**": [1, 2, 3 actionable next steps].
> "**Note**": [Honestly explain limitations and uncertainties, such as where the measurement is still not accurate enough].

Memorize this template, and you can present any IRT results in a professional and understandable way.

## One Final Word

> **The golden structure for presenting results is "Conclusion → Evidence → Recommendation": one sentence to explain the good and bad, use metaphors and Wright Maps to explain the reasons thoroughly, and provide actionable next steps. Avoid jargon, don't pretend to be certain, don't overload in one go, and tailor your message to bosses, clients, and peers. Being able to calculate is your ticket in, but being able to explain is your moat.**
