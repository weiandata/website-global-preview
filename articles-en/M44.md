# 44 · Establishing an Item Bank: How IRT Makes Questions from Different Batches "Comparable"

> Many organizations want to have their own "item bank" — questions that can be reused, customized as needed, and accumulated over time. This article explains why an item bank cannot be built without IRT, and how "equating" is the key technique that makes everything comparable.

## First, Clarify What an "Item Bank" Is

An item bank is not simply "a folder containing many questions." A real item bank is a collection of questions that each have **stable "identity parameters" (difficulty, discrimination, fairness, etc.) and these parameters are measured on the same scale, making them comparable with each other.**

With such an item bank, you can:

- **Customize exams as needed**: Want a moderately difficult exam? Pick questions of the right difficulty from the bank.
- **Prevent cheating with multiple versions**: Create several different exams and ensure fairness (because all questions are measured on the same scale).
- **Track results over time**: Use a set of questions this year and a different set next year, and still have comparable results.
- **Support adaptive testing**: The CAT ammunition is exactly this (see Chapter 28).

**Without IRT, you cannot build an item bank** — because under ordinary scoring, question difficulty changes with the population (Chapter 2), and there is no such thing as "stable, comparable identity parameters."

## Core Challenge: How to Put Questions from Different Batches on the "Same Scale"

An item bank is built gradually. This year, you calibrate 100 questions, and next year, you calibrate another 100, using **different people**.

The problem arises:

- This year, the calibration is done by Group A, and next year by Group B.
- If Group A is generally more capable than Group B, the same difficulty concept will be "anchored" at different positions in the two calibration batches.
- Result: **The parameters of the two batches, although both called "difficulty b," are not on the same scale — mixing them directly would lead to confusion.**

This is like two countries printing their own currency, both called "yuan," but with different exchange rates. Without conversion, they cannot be used interchangeably.

## Solution: Equating (equating / linking)

The technique that puts questions from different batches on the same scale is called **equating** (also known as linking). Its core mechanism is — **anchor items** (mentioned in Chapter 2).

**In practical terms**, the approach is: have **some overlapping questions** between the two batches (these shared questions are the "anchor items").

- These anchor items have been taken by both Group A and Group B.
- Since they are the same questions, they should theoretically have the same set of parameters.
- Therefore, we can calculate the "exchange rate" (offset and scaling) between the two scales by comparing the performance of the anchor items in Group A and Group B.
- Use this exchange rate to **convert** the parameters of the questions from the next batch to the current scale.

In this way, all batches of questions are unified on the same scale, and the item bank becomes truly "universal." **Anchor items are the bridge connecting different batches; equating is the procedure of crossing the bridge.**

## Anchor Items Are Not Chosen Arbitrarily

The success of equating largely depends on how well the anchor items are selected. A few principles (just for understanding):

- **Quantity matters**: Too few anchor items make the bridge unstable, and the "exchange rate" cannot be accurately calculated.
- **Quality is essential**: Anchor items themselves should be good questions (high discrimination, good fit). Poor anchor items can skew the entire bridge.
- **Representativeness is key**: The difficulty of the anchor items should cover a wide range; avoid having too many easy or too many difficult questions.
- **Stability is important**: Anchor items should perform consistently across different batches. If an anchor item "drifts" (possibly due to leakage or being guessed), it becomes unreliable and should be removed from the anchor set (see parameter drift in Chapter 27).

**In one sentence**: Anchor items are the "calm in the storm" of an item bank. If they are not selected well, the comparability of the entire item bank collapses.

## An Item Bank Is "Living" and Needs Ongoing Maintenance

Building an item bank is not the end — it's the beginning. An item bank requires long-term maintenance:

- **Add new questions**: Questions can become outdated or leaked, so new questions must be continuously added (and equated into the bank).
- **Retire old questions**: Questions that are overexposed or have drifted should be retired.
- **Regular check-ups**: Periodically review whether the parameters are still stable and whether new DIFs have emerged.

Therefore, a good item bank is a **long-term asset that accumulates year by year and becomes more valuable with maintenance**. This is also the long-term value of such services (see Chapters 7 and 26) — it is not a one-time transaction, but rather an infrastructure that can accompany customers for many years.

## Final Summary

> **A real item bank is a collection of questions with stable parameters that are comparable on the same scale. Only IRT can build such a bank. The technique that unifies questions from different batches onto the same scale is called "equating," which uses "anchor items" as a bridge to calculate the "exchange rate" between batches. Anchor items must be numerous, high-quality, representative, and stable. An item bank is a living asset that becomes more valuable with ongoing maintenance.**
