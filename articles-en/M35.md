# 35 · Common R Packages Spotlight: What mirt, ltm, and TAM Each Specialize In

> The previous article explained that R's capabilities are housed in "packages." This article gives a "spotlight introduction" to the most commonly used IRT packages, so you won't be confused when you hear these names. You don't need to remember details—just recognize them.

## First, Understand What a "Package" Is

R itself is like an "empty kitchen," and a "package" (package) is the **professional kitchen set** you add to it. If you want to make bread, get a bread maker; if you want to make juice, get a juicer.

Doing IRT means adding a few "IRT kitchen sets" to R. They are free, open-source, and can be installed with just one command. The following are the most commonly used ones in the community, each with its own specialty.

## mirt — The Most Versatile "Mainstay Chef"

**Name Meaning**: Multidimensional Item Response Theory (Multidimensional Item Response Theory).

**Specialties**: Almost anything can be done. Single-dimensional, multidimensional, dichotomous (right/wrong), polytomous (Likert, partial scoring), 1PL to complex models, parameter estimation, model fit checks, information functions, DIF, and plotting—all covered.

**Status**: If you can only remember one IRT package name, remember **mirt**. It is the default choice for many IRT practitioners, a Swiss Army knife of sorts. It is comprehensive, frequently updated, well-documented, and has a large community.

**One Sentence Summary**: **A versatile mainstay for both beginners and experienced users; use it when you're unsure what to use.**

## ltm — The Classic "Entry-Level Veteran"

**Name Meaning**: Latent Trait Models (Latent Trait Models).

**Specialties**: Single-dimensional classical models (1PL/Rasch, 2PL, 3PL, and GRM for Likert items), dichotomous and ordered polytomous items.

**Status**: It has been around for a long time and is well-known. Many textbooks and tutorials use it as an example. It is not as comprehensive as mirt (e.g., it's not the best for multidimensional models), but it is clear, sufficient, and easy to use for **beginners doing single-dimensional analysis**.

**One Sentence Summary**: **A classic teaching favorite, ideal for beginners practicing single-dimensional models.**

## TAM — The "Professional Competitor" for Large-Scale Educational Assessments

**Name Meaning**: Test Analysis Modules (Test Analysis Modules).

**Specialties**: Designed for **complex needs in large-scale educational assessments/literacy monitoring**—latent regression (incorporating background variables into the model), plausible values (Plausible Values, a specialized technique for group distribution in large-scale survey reports), complex sampling weights, and various extensions of the Rasch family.

**Status**: TAM can handle the analytical approaches used in large international assessments like PISA and TIMSS. It is more "professional-oriented" and is a common tool for national-level large-scale surveys (e.g., scientific literacy, student science literacy, digital literacy).

**One Sentence Summary**: **A top-tier competitor for large-scale educational assessments, a staple for census-level projects.**

## One More from Our Own: IRTC

In addition to the three community packages mentioned above, we have developed our own open-source IRT estimation engine called **IRTC** (publicly available on GitHub). It specializes in solving a tough problem—**speed and memory issues in IRT estimation for large-scale scenarios** (mentioned in Article 32, where standard tools struggle with millions of participants). When standard packages can't handle ultra-large data, this is where IRTC comes in.

**One Sentence Summary**: **A self-developed estimation engine designed specifically for "running big data smoothly."**

## A Quick Reference Table

| Package | One-Sentence Summary | When to Use It |
| -------- | ---------------------- | ---------------- |
| **mirt** | All-in-one mainstay, Swiss Army knife | Most situations, especially when unsure |
| **ltm** | Classic entry-level veteran | Beginners' practice, simple single-dimensional models |
| **TAM** | Professional competitor for large-scale assessments | Literacy monitoring, plausible values, latent regression, complex sampling |
| **IRTC** (Self-developed) | Big data estimation engine | Ultra-large data, when standard packages can't handle it |

(There are also other packages like eRm and sirt that specialize in specific needs, but you don't need to worry about them in the early stages. Check them out when you encounter specific needs.)

## Practical Suggestions for Regular Users

1. **Don't install and learn everything at once.** Starting with just the **mirt** package is enough, as it can cover your journey from beginner to intermediate for a long time.
2. **Choose a package based on your needs, not its reputation.** For plausible values in large-scale assessments, mirt can do it, but TAM is more efficient; for practicing single-dimensional models, ltm is more straightforward. Tools should serve your needs.
3. **The results from the same data should be consistent across different packages** (within the same model). If the results differ significantly when using different packages, it's likely due to a mistake in your settings or understanding. It's worth pausing to check—this is also a form of cross-validation.

## One-Sentence Conclusion

> **Common R packages for IRT: mirt is the all-in-one mainstay (use it when unsure), ltm is the classic entry-level veteran (practice single-dimensional models), TAM is the professional competitor for large-scale assessments (plausible values, latent regression, complex sampling), and our self-developed IRTC specializes in "running big data smoothly." Beginners can start with mirt alone, and choose packages based on needs, not reputation.**
