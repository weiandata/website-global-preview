# A14 · DIF Detection: Insuring the "Group Difference" Conclusion

> A deeper version accompanying Q17. Fairness is a mandatory question for all formal assessments.

## Starting from a Question

Your monitoring report states: "Female reading scores are significantly higher than male scores." A review expert asks: **"How do you know this is a difference in ability, rather than item bias?"**

If the item context systematically aligns more with the life experiences of one gender (e.g., math problems with a lot of football-related scenarios), then the "group score difference" includes the component of "item bias." DIF (Differential Item Functioning, Item Functioning Difference) detection is the quality control process that separates these two elements—**without it, all group comparison conclusions carry the risk of unreviewed bias**.

## Precise Definition of the Concept

The definition of DIF includes a key conditional clause: **under the condition of controlling ability equivalence**, the probability of answering a particular item correctly still differs between groups.

- A 65% pass rate for males and 55% for females on a particular item: this is called impact, which may simply reflect different ability distributions, **not DIF**;
- Males and females with the same θ (0.5) have pass probabilities of 0.7 vs. 0.5: this is DIF—this item measures something gender-related beyond ability.

DIF is divided into two types: **consistency** (one group consistently performs worse across the entire ability range) and **non-consistency** (low scorers in group A perform worse, but the trend reverses at high scores, with ICC crossing). Detection methods must be able to capture both types.

## Method Toolkit

**1. Mantel-Haenszel (MH)**: Stratify by total score, compare pass rates between groups within each stratum, and aggregate into a common odds ratio.
Advantages: Simple, robust, usable with small samples, and has a mature effect size grading system—ETS's A/B/C levels (|ΔMH| < 1 is A, negligible; > 1.5 is C, requiring action), which is very convenient for practical communication. Limitations: Not sensitive to non-consistent DIF.

**2. Logistic Regression**: Model P(correct) ~ matched score + group + matched score × group. A significant main effect = consistent DIF, a significant interaction = non-consistent DIF. Use Nagelkerke ΔR² for effect size. **This method handles both consistent and non-consistent DIF, is easy to implement, and is recommended as the default method**.

**3. IRT Approach**:

- Likelihood Ratio Test: Compare two models—"item parameters are equal across groups vs. freely estimated";
- Lord Chi-square / Raju Area: Directly test differences in item parameters between groups or the area between ICCs.

Theoretical cleanest (matching variable is a latent variable, not a total score with measurement error), but requires a large sample and model fit assumptions.

R Implementation: `difR` package (MH, logistic, Lord, Raju, etc., all in one interface), `DIF()` in `mirt` and multi-group models.

## Operational Process (Including the Most Common Mistake)

1. **Define Groups**: Focus group/reference group (gender, urban/rural, language group... each pair is tested separately);
2. **Purify Matching Variable (Most Common Mistake)**: Using "total score with DIF items" as a proxy for ability will contaminate the detection—standard practice is **iterative purification**: first detection → remove detected items from the matching score → re-detect → repeat until the set stabilizes;
3. **Dual Thresholds of Statistical Test + Effect Size**: In large samples, p-values are always significant, **only effect sizes reaching C level (or a predetermined threshold) enter the action list**;
4. **Content Review**: Statistics can only raise alarms, not make judgments. Send C-level items to content experts and target group representatives: why is this item biased? Is it a reasonable part of the construct (e.g., female advantage in "reading literary texts" may be a part of the construct), or is it a deviation unrelated to the construct (contextual experience, dialect vocabulary)? **Keep the former, revise or delete the latter**—this judgment cannot be replaced by an algorithm;
5. **Documentation**: Record detection methods, thresholds, detected items, and decisions—everything goes into the technical documentation.

## Three Deep Water Area Warnings

1. **DIF Cannot Detect "Entire Test is Biased"**: When all items are biased in the same direction, the matching variable itself is contaminated, and DIF methods collectively fail (this is the logical boundary of the method). The defense can only rely on fairness review during item development and a diverse item development team;
2. **Multiple Comparisons with Multiple Groups and Items**: Hundreds of items × multiple group pairs, managing false positives is essential (graded effect size is itself a defense, and FDR correction can also be added);
3. **DIF is the Item-Level Version of Measurement Invariance**: The corresponding concept in factor analysis tradition is scalar invariance testing, with the same logic—before cross-group comparisons, first prove that the measuring tool is the same for all groups.

**Conclusion in One Sentence**: Group differences are the most attention-grabbing and easily questioned output of an assessment. Every day spent on DIF detection is an investment in the credibility of the report.
