# A05 · CTT vs IRT Comprehensive Comparison: When You Must Switch Weapons

## What Are the Two Theories Fighting Over

Classical Test Theory (CTT) has dominated the measurement field for nearly half a century, with its core equation being simple: observed score = true score + error (X = T + E). Item Response Theory (IRT), on the other hand, shifts the modeling focus from "scores" down to "every interaction between individuals and items." This is not a battle between old and new, but rather a **battle over applicability**—this article breaks down the two theories for comparison and provides a decision table at the end.

## Comparison Across Five Dimensions

**1. Modeling Unit: Test vs Item**

All CTT indicators are attributes of the "whole test": reliability is for the whole test, standard error is for the whole test. Changing three items requires recalculating all indicators, and they are not comparable to the previous version. IRT parameterizes each item (difficulty b, discrimination a), and the test is just a collection of items—thus, "changing items without changing the measuring scale" becomes possible, which is the theoretical foundation for item banks, multiple test versions, and adaptive assessments.

**2. Sample Dependency**

CTT defines item difficulty as pass rate, which is essentially a mixed measure of "item × this group of people." A strong group makes all items "easier." IRT, when the model holds, has parameter invariance: item parameters do not drift with the population, and ability estimates do not drift with item combinations. **Note that this is a property of the model, not magic**—its validity depends on good model fit and sufficient calibration samples. In practice, we need to use fit checks and drift detection to protect it, not treat it as an infallible doctrine.

**3. Characterization of Measurement Error**

CTT: one reliability coefficient + one measurement standard error that is the same for everyone. IRT: information function—the complete curve of measurement precision varying with ability level, with each person's ability estimate having its own standard error. Practical implication: when making high-stakes decisions on a particular score line (pass/fail), IRT can tell you **how accurate the measurement is near that line**, while CTT can only provide the average score for the whole test.

**4. Definition of "Score"**

CTT scores are bounded total scores, with true differences compressed at the ceiling or floor, and no guarantee of equal spacing between score points. IRT's θ is defined on an unbounded latent variable scale (logit), with better spacing properties and the ability to be freely linearly transformed into any reported scale (e.g., 500 ± 100).

**5. Cost**

CTT: nearly zero threshold, can be calculated with dozens of people, and Excel can do it. IRT: model assumptions need to be tested (unidimensionality, local independence, fit), parameter estimation requires samples (Rasch hundreds, 2PL/GPCM thousands), and calculation and interpretation require professional investment. **The cost difference is an order of magnitude, which is the fundamental reason why CTT has remained popular so far.**

## Clarification of Common Misconceptions

- **"IRT is more accurate than CTT"**—this is not true. In scenarios with a single administration, single test, and group ranking, the ability rankings from both are highly consistent (correlation often above 0.95). IRT's advantage lies in "comparability, item management, and precision characterization," which CTT cannot achieve structurally, not in making a single test "more accurate";
- **"CTT is outdated"**—CTT is the correct tool for classroom tests, small-sample research, and initial screening of scales. Whether to use IRT depends on demand, not on advancement;
- **"IRT does not need reliability"**—IRT has its own reliability counterparts (marginal reliability, information function), and formal reports still require providing them.

## Decision Table

| Your Scenario | Recommendation |
| --- | --- |
| Class/Classroom Test, Dozens of People | CTT |
| Single Administration Survey, Scale Analysis | CTT (+Factor Analysis) |
| Item Selection for Scale Development | CTT Initial Screening + IRT Refinement (if sample is sufficient) |
| Cross-Year/Cross-Test Comparison | **Must Use IRT** (Equating) |
| Item Bank Construction and Maintenance | **Must Use IRT** |
| Adaptive Testing | **Must Use IRT** |
| Fairness Check (DIF) | IRT or MH Method Based on Total Score |
| Large-Scale Literacy Monitoring | IRT Full Suite (Calibration/Equating/PV) |

## The Right Approach in Practice: Relay, Not Replacement

In mature projects, the two are in a pipeline relationship: CTT indicators (pass rate, item-total correlation, alpha after item deletion) perform the **first round of item screening**, which is fast, intuitive, and has low sample requirements; items that pass the screening enter IRT modeling for **fine calibration** (parameters, fit, DIF); the final report is completed on the IRT scale for equating and transformation. CTT is like a stethoscope, and IRT is like an MRI—both are available in a hospital, used as needed.
