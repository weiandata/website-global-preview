# A27 · Guide for Clients: How to Efficiently Collaborate with External Data Analysis Teams

> A deeper version of Q26. Business conversion line, building trust from the perspective of "helping clients avoid pitfalls."

## Honest Words for the Requesting Party

This article is written from your perspective (research institutions, government departments, evaluation agencies): how to make external data analysis collaboration successful, rather than a disaster. The content comes from the real-life breakdowns seen from the perspective of a service provider — **most failed collaborations have problems planted before they even start**.

## Before Starting: Three Essential Questions to Ask Yourself

**1. What exactly are you buying?**  
"Finding someone to do data analysis" can mean at least four different types of procurement, and misalignment is the number one cause of failure:

- **Buying results**: Completing the analysis and producing a report in one go → project-based;
- **Buying methods**: Scheme design, technical oversight, and review response → consultant-based (by day or by phase);
- **Buying tools**: Repeating analysis processes every year → **software delivery** (the provider delivers software assets that you can run yourself);
- **Buying capability**: Teaching the team to do it themselves → training + accompaniment.

Special note on the third: **Whenever you have an analysis that needs to be done every year, buying software is more cost-effective than buying labor** — the first year's cost is similar, and from the second year onward, the marginal cost approaches zero, with the capability being retained within your organization. Many clients are unaware that this option exists on the market.

**2. Can the data leave the premises?**  
For data involving personal information or sensitive domains, this question must be clarified **before** contacting any supplier. Data not leaving the premises ≠ no collaboration — mature isolation models (software delivery, on-site work, secure environment) can fully support professional collaboration (I have written a piece titled "Data Never Leaves the Premises" specifically about this model). However, it must be written into the procurement requirements, because **suppliers capable of delivering isolated solutions are few**, and this condition will directly eliminate half of the candidates, making it more efficient to screen early.

**3. Who will be the internal point of contact?**  
The most underestimated cost of external collaboration is the internal effort from the client side. A **data-savvy internal contact person** must be designated (not necessarily a statistician, but someone who can answer "how was this variable collected originally"), and their time must be reserved. Without this role, any service provider will waste half their time guessing the meaning of the data.

## Selection: Five Questions to Gauge Depth

1. "My sampling is stratified cluster sampling. How will you handle it?" — If they don't ask for your sampling plan before quoting, they are out;
2. "Will the deliverables include runnable code? Can we reproduce it?" — If they only deliver a report and not the code, you're buying a one-time firework;
3. "Why is this method suitable for my problem?" — The depth of the explanation matters. A professional can explain the basis and limitations, while a workshop-style provider will just say, "That's how it's always done";
4. "Do you have any publicly accessible work?" — Papers, open-source projects, and technical documentation. Code quality on GitHub cannot be faked, and claims of "having worked on large projects" cannot be verified;
5. "What if the data doesn't support the expected conclusion?" — **This is the litmus test**. The correct answer is "report it truthfully"; any suggestion that "you can tweak the results to match your expectations" is an invitation to collude, and when the scandal breaks, your organization's reputation will suffer as well.

## Contract: Turn Professional Requirements into Clauses

Recommended key clauses (each corresponds to a real dispute):

- **Deliverables list**: Report + full analysis code + running instructions + data dictionary/codebook (plus automated testing in the case of software delivery);
- **Acceptance criteria**: "The client personnel can reproduce the main results in their own environment according to the instructions" — this ensures both code quality and knowledge transfer;
- **Data security**: Contact boundaries (who, in what form, in what environment), storage and destruction, and breach responsibilities; the isolation delivery model should explicitly state "the service provider does not access the raw data";
- **Midpoint check points**: At least one mid-project delivery (e.g., cleaning report + preliminary description), don't put all the risk on the final stage;
- **Intellectual property**: Code ownership or usage rights, to avoid situations where "you'll have to pay again for a parameter change next year."

## Collaboration Period: Let Professionals Make Professional Judgments, But Stay Informed

- Start with a **data briefing meeting**: Collection background, known pitfalls, and changes in reporting definitions — the more you explain, the fewer detours the project will take;
- Respect methodological suggestions, but require **all method choices to have written justification** (technical memo), which is both a quality control measure and an asset for you;
- Give a reward response to "bad news": When the service provider reports that "this data can't support a provincial-level conclusion," it's a professional and responsible action; forcing them to "think of a way" is forcing them to fabricate;
- At the end, besides accepting the results, conduct a **handover exercise**: Your team runs the entire process from scratch. If it doesn't run, the acceptance isn't complete.
