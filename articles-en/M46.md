# 46 · Is Discrimination Negative? —— It's Usually Due to These Reasons

> The first case in the emergency series. A negative discrimination is one of the most common and alarming "warning lights." This article teaches you how to calmly diagnose and treat the issue.

## First, Understand How Unusual Negative Discrimination Is

Discrimination (discussed in posts 3 and 10) measures "the higher the level, the more likely the item is answered correctly." For a normal item, discrimination is positive — the probability of a high-ability person answering correctly is higher than that of a low-ability person.

**Negative discrimination means: higher-ability individuals are more likely to answer incorrectly.** High-achievers collectively fail, while low-achievers get it right.

This violates the most basic common sense. So when you see negative discrimination, don't panic — first, treat it as a clear signal: **this item (or data) is almost certainly wrong somewhere.** It's not "the world is strange," but rather "there's a bug to fix." Below are the most common culprits, ranked by probability from highest to lowest.

## Culprit One: Reverse Items Were Not Flipped (Top Suspect)

This is **the most common** cause, without any doubt.

In scales, there are often reverse-scored items (post 37). For example, when measuring "happiness," most items are scored higher with more agreement, but one item might be "I often feel painful" — the more agreement, the **lower** the happiness.

If you encode this reverse item without flipping it, its direction will be opposite to the entire scale: high-happiness individuals will choose low scores on this item, leading to "higher ability individuals are less likely to get high scores," and discrimination naturally becomes negative.

**Treatment:** Check whether this item is a reverse item. If it is, flip it (swap 1↔5, 2↔4 on a five-point scale) and rerun. **In most cases, flipping will fix the issue.** When you see negative discrimination, check this first — it can save you a lot of effort.

## Culprit Two: The Answer Key Was Incorrect (Key Error)

The "correct answer" for a multiple-choice item was incorrectly entered or set — for example, B was marked as correct, but the correct answer is actually C.

Result: Those who actually know the answer (chose C) are marked as "wrong," while those who guessed (randomly chose B) are marked as "correct." Thus, high-ability individuals "answer incorrectly," and low-ability individuals "answer correctly," leading to negative discrimination.

**Treatment:** Verify the standard answer for this item. Correct it and rerun — the item will be restored. **This is a good item that was wrongly marked, and it shouldn't be deleted — it should be cleared of the error.**

## Culprit Three: The Item Itself Has Serious Ambiguity or Flaws

Unclear item wording, multiple plausible answers, or the "correct answer" being controversial — this leads to higher-ability individuals, who think more carefully, being more likely to be "trapped" into selecting an option outside the "standard answer," while those who don't think deeply might randomly select the correct one.

**Treatment:** Have content experts review the item. If there are indeed ambiguities or flaws, either revise it or delete it (post 41).

## Culprit Four: Data or Sample Issues

- **Too small or too skewed a sample:** When data is insufficient, parameter estimates are inherently unstable (post 30), and occasionally negative values may appear, which may not indicate an item error but rather unstable estimation.
- **A lot of careless responding:** If many high-ability individuals randomly answer this item (e.g., it's at the end of the test and everyone is tired and just guesses), it may also result in negative discrimination.

**Treatment:** Check the standard error (is it too large to be reliable), whether the number of respondents is sufficient, and whether this item is a major area of careless responding (post 38).

## A "Negative Discrimination" Emergency Flowchart

When you see negative discrimination, follow this order to diagnose efficiently:

1. **Is it a reverse item?** → If yes, flip it and rerun (most frequent, check first).
2. **Is the answer key correct?** → Verify the key, correct it if wrong, and rerun.
3. **Does the item have ambiguity or flaws?** → Have experts review the item, revise or delete it.
4. **Is the sample sufficient, and are there issues with careless responding?** → Check the standard error, number of respondents, and response quality.

Following these four steps, most negative discrimination issues will be resolved.

## An Important Reminder: Don't Delete It Immediately

Although negative discrimination is alarming, it often points to **correctable errors** (reverse items, incorrect answer keys), rather than "the item is inherently bad."

**The most common mistake for beginners is to delete the item immediately upon seeing negative discrimination.** This may delete a good item that was wrongly marked. The correct approach is to **first diagnose the cause and then decide on the treatment** — flip if possible, correct the key if needed, fix it if possible, and only delete it if it's a fundamental flaw in the item itself (the "statistical accusation + manual judgment" approach in post 41).

## One-Paragraph Summary

> **Negative discrimination = "higher ability individuals are more likely to answer incorrectly," which almost certainly indicates a bug. Follow the frequency order to check: reverse items not flipped (top culprit, check first), incorrect answer keys, ambiguous items, sample/careless responding issues. Most are correctable errors — don't delete immediately; first diagnose the cause, then treat it appropriately.**
