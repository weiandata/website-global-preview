# 11 · The "Can't Lift Its Head" Curve —— What Is the Guessing Parameter c?

> You might have noticed that some ICCs don't start at the ground level but instead lift slightly off the ground. This article explains what this "small step" is and what it is trying to tell you.

## Starting with a Common Sense: Multiple Choice Questions Can Be Guessed

A multiple-choice question with four options, even if you don't know any of the words, has a 25% chance of being guessed correctly by randomly selecting an option.

This "guaranteed correct by guessing" probability is described by a specific parameter in IRT, called the **guessing parameter**, denoted as **c** (also known as the lower asymptote).

The 1PL and 2PL models previously discussed pretend that guessing doesn't exist (the curve starts at 0). However, for multiple-choice questions that allow guessing, this assumption is invalid — hence the introduction of the 3PL model, which specifically includes this c.

## What Does It Look Like on the Graph

Recall the standard ICC: the left end (for low-ability individuals) has a probability of answering correctly that starts at 0, because "not knowing is not knowing."

Now, add guessing. Although low-ability individuals don't know the answer, they can guess, so their probability of answering correctly doesn't drop to 0 — instead, it **stops at a small step**. The height of this step is the value of c.

Thus, the entire curve's left end "lifts its head": it no longer starts from the ground but from a small platform in the air, then rises to the right. For a four-option question, this step is approximately around 0.25 (though in practice, there may be variations; good distractors can lower it, while overly attractive distractors might even make it lower).

**Imagery**: A standard ICC is like an athlete starting from the ground; an ICC with guessing is like an athlete starting from a small platform — the starting point is elevated.

## Why We Must Acknowledge "Guessing"

Because ignoring it will **overestimate the ability of low-ability individuals**.

Imagine a very difficult multiple-choice question. A low-ability person guesses correctly. If the model doesn't consider guessing, it will take it seriously and think, "He got a very difficult question right — his ability must be high!" It will then overestimate his ability. This is unfair — it's just luck.

After the 3PL model introduces c, it learns to "discount" — when a low-ability person answers a difficult question correctly, it will think, "This might be a guess," and thus not easily overestimate the ability estimate. **The role of c is to equip the model with a 'guessing filter'**.

## But c Is a "Difficult-to-Handle" Parameter

To be honest, c is the most unstable and hardest to estimate of the three parameters.

The reason is straightforward: **estimating c relies on the performance of low-ability individuals on this item**. However, if your sample has few low-ability individuals or if the item is not too difficult for everyone, the data on how low-ability individuals guess on this item is severely insufficient, and the machine can only guess, leading to inconsistent and sometimes absurd estimates.

In practice, several common approaches are used: when the sample is insufficient, simply avoid using the 3PL model (reverting to the 2PL); or set a reasonable prior for c (effectively telling the machine, "For a four-option question, c is likely around 0.2 — don't guess randomly"). These techniques will be revisited in posts 30 and 49.

## A Frequently Asked Question: Does the Likert Scale Have a c?

Usually not.

The guessing parameter is the **exclusive property of multiple-choice questions (especially true-false or multiple-choice items)**, because there is an action of "guessing an answer." In contrast, a Likert scale such as "Very Agree / Agree / Neutral / Disagree / Very Disagree" is not about "guessing a correct answer," but rather expressing a degree, and there is no "standard answer to guess." Therefore, models like GRM used for Likert scales do not include a guessing parameter.

**In One Sentence**: Only items with a "standard answer that can be guessed" require considering c.

## How to Use c When Interpreting a Graph

If you see an ICC with its left end lifted very high (e.g., the step is at 0.4), you should start to suspect:

- Is it because the **distractors are too poor**, allowing two to be easily eliminated, effectively turning it into a "two-choice" question, thus naturally increasing the guessing rate?
- Is it because the item has **obvious flaws**, allowing it to be guessed using common sense or elimination?
- Or is it purely due to **insufficient sample size and an inaccurate estimate of c**?

Conversely, if c is close to 0, it indicates that the item is almost impossible to guess — it is either a fill-in-the-blank or subjective question, or the distractors are exceptionally well-designed.

## One-Sentence Conclusion

> **That "Can't Lift Its Head" Curve, the small step at its left end is the guessing parameter c — it describes the guaranteed correct probability of guessing even if you don't know the answer. It equips the model with a 'guessing filter' to avoid overestimating low-ability individuals; however, it is the hardest to estimate, and when the sample is insufficient, it's better to avoid using it.**
