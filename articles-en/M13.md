# 13 · Test Information Curve: What Kind of People Does This Questionnaire "Specialize" In?

> The previous article explained the "information mountain" of a single item. This article stacks the information mountains of all items in the test to see the "health report" of the entire questionnaire — it tells you where the test measures accurately and where it measures poorly.

## Stack the Small Mountains into One Big Mountain

Each item has its own "information mountain" (the sweet zone is around its difficulty). A questionnaire with dozens of items has dozens of small mountains.

By **adding them point by point** at each level, we get a new curve called the **Test Information Function** (Test Information Function, abbreviated as **TIF**). It answers the question:

> **How accurate can this entire questionnaire measure people at different levels?**

- **High points** on the curve: This group of people is measured accurately by the test.
- **Low points** on the curve: This group of people is measured poorly, with large errors.

Information and precision are two sides of the same coin: **the higher the information, the smaller the measurement error, and the more reliable the estimated level** (the relationship between information and standard error will be explained in Chapter 21). Therefore, the TIF can also be read as an "error curve" — where information is high, error is small, and where information is low, error is large.

## This Curve is the "Health Report" of the Questionnaire

Once you have the TIF, you can immediately see the "constitution" of the questionnaire:

**Case 1: The peak is in the middle.**
Information is concentrated around θ = 0, and it tapers off at both ends. This indicates that the test measures **middle-level individuals** most accurately, and it is less accurate for those at the extremes. Most standard questionnaires look like this — because most items have moderate difficulty, and the sweet zone is concentrated in the middle.

**Case 2: The peak is skewed to one side.**
Information is concentrated on the right (high-level segment), indicating that this test is designed for high achievers — it can differentiate the top performers well, but it is almost useless for low-level individuals. This is common in selection exams. Conversely, if the peak is on the left, it is intended for screening individuals with weak foundations.

**Case 3: Bimodal.**
Information has two peaks at two different levels, with a dip in between. This often indicates a test that **wants to screen low scorers and select high scorers**. A well-designed bimodal curve is intentional, but a poorly designed one suggests extreme difficulty on both ends and a gap in the middle.

**Case 4: Flat and low.**
The entire curve is flat and low, with no clear peak. This indicates that the test measures poorly across all levels — it is likely due to low discrimination and poor item quality, requiring a complete overhaul.

## Key Step: Align "Information" with "Target"

The most important use of the TIF is to **compare it with your measurement target**:

> **Wherever you want to measure accurately, the information peak should be there.**

- If your target is **making judgments around the passing line** (around θ = 0), the TIF peak should be at θ = 0. If you plot it and find the peak at θ = 1.5, with a dip near the passing line, the test is most inaccurate where it should be most accurate — judgments can be easily overturned.
- If your target is **a census of scientific literacy**, aiming to measure all individuals from low to high, you need not a sharp peak, but a **high and wide information platform** that covers a broad range of levels, ensuring sufficient information for each segment.
- If your target is **selecting top performers**, it is acceptable to have the peak skewed toward the high-level segment, even if the low-level segment is not measured accurately.

This idea of "target determines the shape of information" is the guiding principle for test construction, item selection, and item supplementation. **Without first asking "who do you want to measure accurately," you can't judge whether a test is good or not.**

## A Practical Scenario

Imagine a large-scale literacy monitoring project that aims to truthfully reflect the distribution of levels across the entire population, from the weakest to the strongest. When you plot the TIF of the existing item bank, you find it is a sharp peak in the middle, with low information at both ends — middle-level individuals are measured accurately, but the weakest and strongest groups have large measurement errors.

Once the problem is diagnosed, the solution is clear: **add items at both ends.** Specifically, add a batch of items with low difficulty and high discrimination to raise the information at the left end, and another batch with high difficulty and high discrimination to raise the information at the right end. After adding these items, plot the TIF again. Ideally, the curve will transform from a sharp peak into a wide and flat high plateau, covering the entire population.

This is the everyday use of the TIF: **plot the curve → identify the low areas by comparing with the target → add items specifically to those areas → replot the curve to verify.**

## Don't Forget: Information is "Enough is Enough"

Information is not always better — it is **enough where you care about it**. Accumulating more information means more items, longer response times, higher costs, and test taker fatigue. The professional approach is to set an "information target line" (e.g., the information at key levels should reach a certain value, corresponding to acceptable error levels). Once the target is met, it is sufficient — there is no need to pile up endlessly. **Just the right amount is the real skill.**

## Final Summary

> **The Test Information Curve (TIF) stacks the information mountains of the entire test to tell you where this questionnaire measures accurately and where it measures poorly. The key is to align the target: where you want to measure accurately, the information peak should be there; where there are low areas, add items there.**
