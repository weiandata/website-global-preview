# 36 · What Does a Standard IRT Analysis Process Look Like (Without Code)

> In the previous sections, we discussed components (curves, parameters, assumptions, tools). This article ties them together into a complete workflow, giving you a full picture of "what a complete IRT project entails from start to finish." The entire process does not involve code, focusing instead on "steps" and "why."

## First, a Full-View Process Diagram

A standard IRT analysis generally follows these nine steps:

1. Clarify what you are measuring (define the construct)
2. Prepare and clean the data
3. Check assumptions (dimensionality, local independence)
4. Choose a model
5. Estimate parameters
6. Conduct fit and quality checks
7. Address problematic items, and if necessary, restart
8. Estimate ability, and transform the scale
9. Output results and write a report

Below, we break each step down, explaining clearly what to do and why.

## Step 1: Clarify What You Are Measuring

**What to do**: Clearly define the latent variable you are measuring (e.g., scientific literacy, reading ability, a certain attitude), and ensure all items point to it.

**Why**: This is the foundation of the foundation (Sections 16 and 17). A vague construct definition leads to everything else going wrong. **Don't skip this step; it's essential.**

## Step 2: Prepare and Clean the Data

**What to do**: Format the data into a form that IRT can process (Section 37 discusses "what data looks like"), handle missing values, and remove clearly invalid responses or careless answers (Section 38 discusses this).

**Why**: Garbage in, garbage out. Dirty data can ruin everything that follows. **Data cleaning is often the most time-consuming, least noticeable, yet most important step in the entire process.**

## Step 3: Check Assumptions

**What to do**: Use factor analysis and other methods to confirm unidimensionality and check for local dependence (e.g., chain items, item groups).

**Why**: These two are the foundation of classical IRT (Sections 17, 18, and 29). Without a stable foundation, modeling is like building on sand. **Establish the foundation first, then proceed to modeling.**

## Step 4: Choose a Model

**What to do**: Select a 1PL/2PL/3PL or GRM/GPCM model based on item type (right/wrong or multi-point), sample size, and requirements (Section 6).

**Why**: The model must match the items and data. Use a simple model for small samples, include a guessing parameter for multiple-choice items, and use GRM for Likert-type items. **Don't use one model for everything** (Sections 30 and 29).

## Step 5: Estimate Parameters

**What to do**: Use software (e.g., mirt) to estimate parameters for each item (the "turning the knobs" process in Section 19).

**Why**: This is the core computational step, yielding each item's "identity." Pay attention to warnings about non-convergence or extreme values (Sections 30 and 49).

## Step 6: Conduct Fit and Quality Checks

**What to do**: Check item fit, person fit, DIF, and parameter standard errors (Sections 22, 23, and 21).

**Why**: This is the "mirror" step, identifying items that don't fit, careless respondents, and unfair items. **Skipping this step and using parameters directly is the most dangerous amateur approach.**

## Step 7: Address Problematic Items, and if Necessary, Restart

**What to do**: Remove or revise problematic items (low discrimination, poor fit, harmful DIF), and if needed, rerun the estimation (back to Step 5).

**Why**: Good results are "iteratively" achieved, not all at once. **Modeling often requires several rounds**: estimation → checking → deleting or revising items → re-estimation, until item quality and fit are satisfactory.

## Step 8: Estimate Ability and Transform the Scale

**What to do**: Use the refined item parameters to estimate each person's ability θ (Sections 4 and 20), and then convert θ into a more understandable report score (Sections 14 and 42).

**Why**: θ is too abstract for clients to understand; it needs to be translated into a human-readable scale like 500±100. Cross-year projects also require equating at this step (Section 44).

## Step 9: Output Results and Write a Report

**What to do**: Organize parameters, information curves, DIF conclusions, ability distribution, and reliability into a report that clients can understand (Sections 42 and 45).

**Why**: The end goal of analysis is "conclusions that are understandable and usable for decision-making," not just a pile of numbers. **Explaining clearly is harder and more valuable than just calculating.**

## Three Principles That Run Through the Entire Process

**1. Reproducibility.**
Record each step with code (Section 33) so the entire process can be rerun exactly as is. This is the credibility of professional analysis.

**2. Iteration, Not a Straight Line.**
The nine-step diagram is for clarity, but in practice, the process often involves back-and-forth (especially between Steps 6 and 7). **Don't expect to go through everything in one go.**

**3. Every Step May Be Paused.**
If any step reveals a foundational problem (wrong dimension, poor data, insufficient sample), stop and address it, even going back to a simpler method (Section 31), rather than pushing forward blindly.

## One-Sentence Summary

> A standard IRT analysis follows nine steps: define the construct → clean the data → check assumptions → choose a model → estimate parameters → conduct checks → address problematic items (often requiring restarting) → estimate ability and transform the scale → output the report. Throughout the process, three principles are followed: reproducibility, iteration rather than a straight line, and the possibility of pausing at any step. Data cleaning is the most labor-intensive, quality checks are the most critical, and explaining clearly is the most valuable.
