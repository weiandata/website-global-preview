# 28 · Computer Adaptive Testing (CAT): How IRT Makes Exams "More Understandable as You Take Them"

> You may have taken some online exams that get "harder or easier as you go" (such as GRE, IELTS computer-based tests, or many online ability assessments). This is exactly what CAT is. This article will explain its principles and why it is so efficient in terms of question usage.

## First, Feel What It's Like to Take a CAT

**Standard Exam:** Everyone gets the **same test paper**, and you work through all the questions in order, with fixed items.

**Adaptive Exam (CAT, Computer Adaptive Testing):** The questions are **served one by one by the computer**, and —

- If you get the previous question **correct**, the next one will be **more difficult**;
- If you get the previous question **incorrect**, the next one will be **easier**.

As a result, everyone gets a different set of questions, like a patient teacher testing your level continuously. As you progress, the difficulty of the questions converges to the level where you are "right about half the time" — that is exactly where the measurement is most accurate.

## Its Principle: A "Guess the Number" Game

The logic of CAT is exactly the same as the childhood game of "guess the number."

You think of a number between 1 and 100, and I try to guess it:

- I guess 50, and you say "too high" → I know the answer is between 1–49, and I guess 25 next;
- You say "too low" → the answer is between 26–49, and I guess 37 next...
- Each guess halves the range, and it quickly narrows down.

CAT is the computer "guessing your ability θ":

1. **Start with a medium-difficulty item** (equivalent to guessing 50).
2. If you get it right → your ability **may be higher** → give you a harder item next (go up).
3. If you get it wrong → your ability **may be lower** → give you an easier item next (go down).
4. After each item, the computer updates its estimate of your θ and **chooses an item where the information peak is exactly at your current level** (the sweet spot from the 12th article) — every item hits your weakness.
5. Stop when the estimate of your θ is **sufficiently accurate (standard error is small enough)**, or when you've used up all the items.

**The core idea is: not waste any item, and every item is targeted precisely at your level.**

## Why It Is So Efficient and Accurate

The problem with a standard fixed test is that for a top student, the first few questions are just a waste of time (he can answer them all correctly with his eyes closed, and these questions provide no information about "how strong he really is"). For a student with weak foundations, the later difficult questions are also useless (he doesn't know any of them, so they also provide no information). **A large number of questions is ineffective for a large number of people.**

CAT eliminates all this waste. It only gives you questions that are "matched to your level," each one in your sweet spot, with high information efficiency. The result is: **using far fewer questions than a fixed test, you can achieve the same or even higher measurement accuracy.** In some scenarios, CAT can use half or even fewer questions to achieve the same accuracy as a long test.

## Why Can It Work? — All Thanks to IRT's "Separation of Person and Item"

Here's the key point: **CAT only works within the framework of IRT.**

Because everyone gets a different set of questions, a standard scoring system would be lost — "You got 5 out of 8 questions right, and he got 7 out of 12 — how can we compare them?" It's impossible to compare.

But IRT's parameter invariance (from the 27th article) saves the day: ability estimates **do not change with the item set**, and item parameters are all on the same scale. So even if you two have completely different sets of questions, the final estimated θ can still be fairly compared. **The seemingly abstract property of "separation of person and item" becomes a real-world "each person gets a different set of questions yet they are still comparable" in CAT.**

## CAT Is Not Without Its Requirements (Stay Alert)

It's cool, but it comes with real costs — not something you can just jump into:

1. **Need a large and high-quality item bank**: You need enough items with a wide range of difficulty and well-calibrated parameters, so the computer has options to choose from. Building such an item bank is a long-term and expensive project (from the 44th article).
2. **Need to prevent "exposure" and cheating**: Popular items being repeatedly selected can leak information, so you need an "exposure control" mechanism to ensure items are evenly distributed.
3. **Need software and hardware support**: Real-time estimation and real-time item selection require the engineering capabilities of the testing system.
4. **Need to handle special cases**: Some people may start off guessing randomly, or their performance may fluctuate, and the algorithm must be robust to avoid being misled by the first few questions.

Therefore, CAT is typically used in **large-scale, long-term, and worthwhile projects**. Applying CAT to small projects is like using a sledgehammer to crack a nut.

## Final Summary

> **CAT is the computer playing a "guess the number" game with you: give you harder questions if you get the previous one right, and easier ones if you get it wrong. Every question is targeted precisely at your level, so you can measure accurately with very few questions. It only works because of IRT's "separation of person and item" (each person gets a different set of questions yet they are still comparable). But it requires a large item bank, exposure control, and engineering support — it's a feature reserved for large-scale projects.**
