# 30 · What Happens When Sample Size Is Insufficient? —— The "Appetite" of IRT

> The previous article listed sample size as one of the foundations. This article focuses specifically on: how much data does IRT really need, what problems arise when the sample is insufficient, and what to do if there aren't enough people.

## First, Build an Intuition: The More "Greedy" the Model, the Larger Its Appetite

In Article 19, it was mentioned that parameter estimation is like "turning knobs to find the best fit," and the more knobs there are, the more data is needed to fix each one.

The "knobs" in an IRT model are the parameters. Therefore, the rule is simple: **the more parameters the model considers, the greater its appetite, and the more data it needs.**

- **1PL / Rasch** (only one parameter: difficulty): the smallest appetite. Empirically, **hundreds of people** are enough (often starting from 200–500).
- **2PL** (difficulty + discrimination): moderate appetite. It is recommended to have **thousands of people** (starting from around 1,000 is more stable).
- **3PL** (adding a guessing parameter c): the largest appetite, and the most difficult to manage. Often, **thousands of people** are needed, because the guessing parameter is particularly "data-hungry."
- **Polytomous scoring models (GRM/GPCM)**: the more categories (five-point scale is more complex than two-point), the more parameters need to be estimated for each item, and the greater the appetite.

(These numbers are empirical ranges, not strict rules. The actual required sample size depends on the number of items, item quality, and distribution of ability levels, among other factors. Professionals will conduct proper sample size planning, and there is a detailed explanation in content library A17.)

## What Problems Arise When the Sample Is Insufficient?

An IRT model that is starved of data will "throw a tantrum" in various ways:

**1. Parameter estimates are unstable and unreliable.**
With too little data, the machine "can't fix the knobs," and the estimated difficulty and discrimination fluctuate wildly. A different sample can lead to completely different results. You might think you've obtained the "ID card" of the item, but in reality, it's like a blurry photo that you can't even recognize.

**2. Standard errors are too large to be useful.**
Fewer samples → less information → larger standard errors (as discussed in Article 21). For example, if you estimate θ = 1.2, but the standard error is 0.8, this "1.2" spans a large range and is basically unusable for any fine-grained judgment.

**3. Estimates can produce absurd extreme values.**
Especially for the guessing parameter c in 3PL models, insufficient samples often result in values close to 0 or 1 (this situation is discussed in Article 49). Discrimination parameters can also be estimated as extremely high.

**4. The model simply "fails to converge."**
The machine can't stabilize the knob-turning process, and the algorithm can't produce results, leading to a complete breakdown. This is often the clearest signal that "the data can't support the model."

**In short**: Insufficient sample size doesn't just give you "slightly worse results"—it often gives you "results that look plausible but are actually unreliable." This is more misleading than a direct error message.

## What to Do When There Are Not Enough People? —— Four Practical Solutions

In reality, you don't always have thousands of people. Don't force it—there are solutions:

**Solution 1: Use a more "diet-friendly" model.**
With limited samples, go back to simpler models. Hundreds of people? Use 1PL / Rasch, don't force a 3PL. **Using a simple model to explain things clearly is the greatest respect for small samples.** This also aligns with the idea from Article 6: "Don't blindly pursue complexity."

**Solution 2: Add "prior" information to the model.**
When data is insufficient, use reasonable priors to constrain parameters (e.g., tell the machine "the guessing parameter c for a four-choice question is around 0.2, don't go wild"), as mentioned in Articles 11 and 19. This is like giving the model a "nutritional supplement" when food is scarce, preventing estimates from going out of control.

**Solution 3: Go back to CTT honestly.**
If the sample is too small (e.g., a class of only a few dozen students), don't use IRT—use traditional methods like pass rates, item-total correlations, and Cronbach's α, which are fast, stable, and sufficient (as discussed in Articles 2 and 5). **Not all scenarios are suitable for IRT, and not all scenarios need it.**

**Solution 4: Collect data in stages.**
If this is a long-term project, you can start with a small-scale pilot (using CTT for initial screening), and gradually move to IRT as data accumulates. Many item banks are built this way, one batch at a time.

## A Common Misconception: Can More Items Make Up for Fewer People?

No, you can't directly substitute. Sample size (number of people) and item count are two separate dimensions:

- **Number of people** mainly supports the estimation of **item parameters** (you need many people to have taken an item to accurately estimate its difficulty).
- **Number of items** mainly supports the estimation of **individual ability θ** (you need many items to accurately estimate a person's level).

Therefore, a configuration like "100 people taking 200 items" may be acceptable for estimating individual θ, but it still struggles with estimating item parameters (each item has only 100 people behind it). **Few people are few people, and more items can't fill the gap in item parameter estimation.**

## Final Summary

> **The appetite of IRT increases with model complexity: Rasch needs hundreds, 2PL needs thousands, and 3PL needs even more. The consequences of insufficient data are not just "slightly worse results"—they are often "unstable parameters, huge errors, extreme values, or even failure to converge," all while pretending to be normal results. When the sample is insufficient, reduce the model, add priors, go back to CTT, or collect data in stages—don't force it.**
