## Three Parameters for Items/Persons (Greek and Lowercase Letters)

| Symbol | Pronunciation | One-Sentence Explanation | Memory Hook | See Also |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **θ** | theta | A person's **ability/level**, ranging from -3 to +3 | θ looks like a barred 0, representing "a person's position on the scale" | 04, 14 |
| **b** | difficulty | How **difficult** a question is (the level at which half-right and half-wrong points align) | b = difficult (bǐ nán, homophonic memory), the larger the b, the harder the question | 03, 09 |
| **a** | discrimination | How **good** a question is at distinguishing people (how steep the curve is) | a starts first, most important, steep = high a | 03, 10 |
| **c** | guessing | The **minimum probability** of getting it right even if you don't know | c = cāi (guess), a small step on the left end of the curve | 11 |

**One Sentence Summary**: The probability of a person with ability **θ** answering a question with difficulty **b**, discrimination **a**, and guessing rate **c** correctly is determined by these four factors.

## Three Common Curves (Capitalized Abbreviations)

| Abbreviation | Full Name | One-Sentence Explanation | What It Looks Like | See Also |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **ICC** | Item Characteristic Curve (Item Characteristic Curve) | The curve showing "the higher the level, the easier it is to answer correctly" for a single item | S-shaped | 08 |
| **IIF** | Item Information Function (Item Information Function) | The "sweet spot" curve for a single item, indicating where people are measured accurately | Small hill | 12 |
| **TIF** | Test Information Function (Test Information Function) | The combined information of the entire test, indicating where the test is most effective | Big mountain (may be multi-peaked) | 13 |

**Relationship**: ICC transforms into IIF (single-item sweet spot), and all IIFs are summed to get TIF (whole test capability). ICC is the root, and the latter two are the branches.

## Several Quality/Statistical Concepts

| Term | One-Sentence Explanation | See Also |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Latent Variable** | An invisible but indirectly measurable thing (θ is an example) | 16 |
| **Unidimensionality** | A set of items measures only one thing (a prerequisite for classical IRT) | 17 |
| **Local Independence** | Items are not additionally connected when levels are the same (no collusion allowed) | 18 |
| **Parameter Estimation** | Deriving parameters from right/wrong data (turning knobs to find the best fit) | 19 |
| **Maximum Likelihood** | Choosing the set of parameters that best explains the data (detective selects the script) | 20 |
| **Standard Error (SE)** | The error range of an estimate (smaller with more information) | 21 |
| **Goodness of Fit** | How well the model fits the data (mirror that reveals truths) | 22 |
| **DIF** | Whether a question is biased toward a particular group after leveling the ability (fairness) | 23 |
| **Reliability** | How stable and reliable the measurement is (a curve in IRT) | 24 |
| **Validity** | Whether the measurement is accurate and measures what it's supposed to | 24 |
| **Equating** | Putting different tests on the same scale to make scores comparable | 02, 44 |

## Three Golden Rules for Interpreting Results (Review)

1. **When seeing any estimate, first ask about its standard error** — numbers without an error range are cheating (Chapter 21).
2. **Before doing any calculations, confirm unidimensionality + local independence** — unstable foundation, the higher the building, the more dangerous it is (Chapters 17, 18).
3. **Do not skip goodness-of-fit checks** — it's the mirror that reveals problems; skipping it is truly dangerous (Chapter 22).
