# 43 · How IRT Helps Us Shorten Questionnaires Without Losing Precision

> "The questionnaire is too long; respondents get bored and start filling it in randomly halfway through. Can we shorten it without affecting the quality?" This is one of the most common requests from clients. IRT is precisely the tool that solves this issue. This article explains the principle clearly.

## First, Understand Why "Long" Is a Problem

Long questionnaires have real drawbacks:

- **Respondent Fatigue**: As respondents progress, their attention wanes, leading to more random answers and a decline in data quality (see Chapter 38).
- **High Cost**: Time is money, especially in large-scale surveys. Each additional question multiplied by tens of thousands of respondents creates a significant testing cost.
- **Low Response Rate**: Very long questionnaires often lead to many respondents abandoning the survey altogether.

Therefore, "using fewer questions to measure just as well" is a widespread need. But intuitively, fewer questions shouldn't mean less precision, right? IRT says: **Not necessarily; the key is which questions you cut.**

## Core Insight: Not All Questions Are Equivalent—Some "Outweigh" Several

In a standard scoring system, each question contributes only "one point," so cutting any question results in similar losses. However, IRT reveals a counterintuitive fact (see Chapter 12):

> **Different questions provide vastly different amounts of "information." A high-quality question can be worth as much as several mediocre ones.**

- A question with high discrimination and difficulty that aligns well with the target level → **High Information Contributor**, cutting it would be a significant loss.
- A question with low discrimination or difficulty that deviates from the target level → **Low Information Contributor**, it occupies space but contributes little, so cutting it causes minimal loss.

The secret to shortening a questionnaire is not to "randomly cut half" but to **precisely remove the "low information contributors" and retain the "high information contributors."** This way, the number of questions is significantly reduced, yet the total information (measurement precision) remains almost unchanged.

## An Intuitive Analogy

Imagine you're moving and your luggage space is limited (questionnaire length budget), and you want to pack the most useful items (information).

- **The naive approach**: Just fill it up randomly—possibly packing a lot of useless old newspapers (low information questions), while the important items have no space.
- **The IRT approach**: Sort by "usefulness" and prioritize packing the most valuable items first, discarding the old newspapers—**the same luggage box can now carry much more valuable items.**

Shortening a questionnaire is essentially this "packaging luggage by information value" process.

## How IRT Works Specifically (Without Code)

The general approach is:

1. **First, calculate the information function for each question in a longer item bank or initial questionnaire** (see Chapter 12), to understand how much information each question contributes to different groups.
2. **Clarify your measurement goal**: Which group do you want to measure accurately? (e.g., near the cut score or across the entire population? See Chapter 13)
3. **Select questions based on the target**: Prioritize retaining questions that provide high information and strong discrimination in the target group, assembling a shorter version that maintains the test information curve (TIF) at the target level.
4. **Validate the shorter version**: Calculate the TIF and reliability of the shorter version to confirm that the precision is still sufficient in the key levels. If it meets the standard, it's done; if not, add one or two key questions.

The result is often: **The number of questions is reduced by 30% to 50%, yet the measurement precision in the key levels drops only slightly, or remains almost unchanged.** This is a significant win in terms of respondent experience, cost, and response rate.

## Going Further: Adaptive Testing Is the Ultimate Form of "Shortening"

If you remember Chapter 28 on CAT (Computerized Adaptive Testing), it is essentially the ultimate form of "shortening a questionnaire": instead of giving everyone a fixed shorter version, **CAT customizes the shortest question sequence for each individual**, with each question hitting their personal sweet spot. Therefore, CAT can save even more questions than any fixed shorter version. Shortening questionnaires and CAT are two different implementations of the same "information efficiency" principle.

## A Necessary Reminder: Don't Shorten Just for the Sake of It

Shortening has its limits:

- **The precision in the key levels of the shorter version must still meet the standard** (see Chapters 13 and 24), you cannot cut to the point of losing accuracy.
- **You cannot remove content representativeness**: If a questionnaire is meant to cover multiple aspects of a construct, simply cutting based on information may remove all questions on a particular aspect, leading to "precise but biased" measurement (validity loss, see Chapter 24). **Information efficiency must serve content completeness.**
- **The shorter version should be validated again**, rather than assuming that cutting low-information questions will automatically solve the problem.

**In one sentence**: Shortening is "precision fat loss," not "mindless cutting in half"; you need to remove the fat while keeping the muscle and skeleton.

## Final Summary

> **IRT can shorten questionnaires without losing precision by leveraging the insight that "questions are not equivalent": remove the low-information contributors (low discrimination, difficulty deviating from the target) and retain the high-information contributors. This results in a significant reduction in the number of questions while maintaining almost no change in precision in the key segments. It's like packing luggage by value. CAT is its ultimate form. But we must stay within the limits: precision in the key segments must meet the standard, content representativeness cannot be lost, and the shorter version must be re-validated.**
