# 39 · When You Get a Parameter Table, Which Columns Should You Look At First?

> After running IRT, software will output a dense table. This article teaches you how to quickly identify the most important columns like an expert, avoiding being overwhelmed by the sea of numbers.

## What a Parameter Table Looks Like

After running the model, you will get a table with one row per item. The columns are roughly like this (the names may vary slightly across different software):

| Item | a (Discrimination) | b (Difficulty) | c (Guessing) | Standard Error | Fit Statistics |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Item 1 | 1.20 | -0.5 | 0.18 | … | … |
| Item 2 | 0.35 | 1.8 | 0.22 | … | … |
| Item 3 | 1.85 | 0.2 | 0.05 | … | … |
| … | | | | | |

With too many numbers, it can be overwhelming. Don't panic — experienced professionals have a fixed "scanning order" when looking at this table.

## Step 1: First Look at Discrimination (a)

**Why Look at It First**: Discrimination directly determines whether an item is useful (see Chapter 10). It is the first gate for judging the quality of an item.

**How to Look at It**:

- If a is too small (e.g., below 0.3 or 0.4) → This item barely distinguishes between individuals, **a key suspect**, consider eliminating it.
- If a is **negative** → Alarm! The item is likely flawed (e.g., answer key reversed, reverse-scored items not flipped), must check (see Chapter 46).
- If a is **moderate to high** (e.g., around 1.0 or higher) → This is a good item, with strong discrimination power.

**In One Sentence**: **First scan the a column, circle the small and negative values — they are high-risk items.**

## Step 2: Next Look at Difficulty (b)

**Why Look at It Second**: Difficulty determines who this item is suitable for (see Chapter 9), and it relates to the coverage of the test's difficulty.

**How to Look at It**:

- Scan all b values to see if they are **evenly distributed**. If they are all clustered at one end (either very hard or very easy) → The test's difficulty coverage is problematic (see Chapters 9 and 15).
- Check if any b values are **extremely high or low** (e.g., beyond ±4) → These could be extreme items or indicate estimation issues (see Chapter 49).
- Compare with your **measurement goal**: If you want to measure people near the passing line, check if there are enough items with b values near the passing line.

**In One Sentence**: **Scan the b column, check if the difficulty distribution is spread out and whether it aligns with your target population.**

## Step 3: Glance at Guessing (c) (If Available)

This column is only present in the 3PL model. Quickly scan it:

- If c is **significantly high** (e.g., above 0.4) → This item has a high chance of being guessed correctly, possibly due to poor distractors or flaws (see Chapter 11).
- If c is **extremely high or low** (e.g., near 0 or 1) → This is likely due to insufficient sample size or unstable estimation (see Chapters 30 and 49), don't take it seriously.

## Step 4: Don't Forget the Standard Error

This step is often skipped, but it is extremely important (see Chapter 21).

Each parameter has a standard error associated with it. **A large standard error indicates that the parameter estimate is not reliable** — even if it looks like a good value, you shouldn't take it too seriously.

- If the standard errors for a and b of an item are both large → This item is poorly calibrated (possibly due to insufficient responses), and its "identity" is unclear.
- When reporting or making decisions, prioritize parameters with **small standard errors and stable estimates**.

**In One Sentence**: **Don't just look at the parameter itself — also look at its standard error. Large standard errors make even good-looking numbers unreliable.**

## Step 5: Scan the Fit Statistics for Outliers

Finally, quickly scan the fit column (see Chapter 22), and mark items with poor fit. These are "non-conforming" items that require special attention (they may violate unidimensionality, have dependencies, or have inherent issues).

## Summary of the "Three-Second Scan Method"

When you get a parameter table, quickly scan through it in this order:

1. **a Column**: Circle small and negative values (high-risk items).
2. **b Column**: Check if the difficulty distribution is spread out and whether it aligns with your target population.
3. **c Column** (if available): Check for unusually high or extreme values.
4. **Standard Error**: Apply a "credibility discount" to the previous judgments.
5. **Fit Column**: Mark non-conforming items.

These marked items are the ones you should focus on next (see Chapters 40 and 41 for how to judge and decide which items to keep or remove).

## Two Common Mistakes for Beginners

1. **Focusing Only on Difficulty (b)**: Many beginners think difficulty is the most important, but **discrimination (a) is actually the first indicator of item quality**. Difficulty only determines suitability, not quality; low discrimination is a clear weakness.
2. **Treating Parameters as Absolute Truths**: Forgetting to look at standard errors and treating a highly uncertain estimate as an exact truth for interpretation or comparison.

## Final One-Sentence Summary

> **The experienced way to read a parameter table: First look at discrimination (a), circle small and negative values (high-risk items), then look at difficulty (b), check if the distribution is spread out and whether it aligns with your target population, glance at guessing (c), then apply a credibility discount to all previous judgments using standard errors, and finally scan the fit column to mark non-conforming items. Remember: a determines quality, b determines suitability, and standard error determines credibility.**
