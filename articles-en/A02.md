## Why Cleaning Warrants a Long Article

In the field of large-scale surveys, there's a saying that **the upper limit of analysis is set the moment the data is handed over to you; cleaning determines whether you can reach that limit.**

In a dataset of hundreds of thousands of questionnaires, there are answers filled in carelessly, partially completed, logically inconsistent, incorrectly coded by the data collection system, and those affected by mid-survey revisions. Without cleaning these issues, the more refined the modeling, the more polished the errors will appear. This article provides a six-step process, repeatedly validated in national-level surveys with hundreds of thousands of samples, along with real-life pitfalls encountered at each step.

## Step 0: Principles First

**Raw data is like an artifact: read-only, archived, never modified.**

All cleaning actions should be written as scripts, generating an analysis dataset from the raw data. This brings three life-saving properties: every step is traceable; rules can be reviewed; and when cleaning errors are found, changing one line of code and rerunning is sufficient, rather than digging through an Excel file that has been edited eight times.

Manually deleting rows and changing values directly in Excel is the biggest taboo in survey data processing — three months later, no one (including yourself) will be able to say what this data has gone through.

## Step 1: Structural Validation — First Confirm That "The Data Looks Right"

Before looking at the content, first perform structural assertions:

- Does the sample size match the reported recovery rate?
- Are the IDs unique? (Duplicate IDs often result from data collection system retransmissions, with two records possibly one complete and one incomplete)
- Do the number, type, and range of variables match the questionnaire design? (Does a 5-point scale having a 6 represent a real data error or a "no answer" code?)
- **Is the skip logic valid?** Someone who answers "never used the internet" should not have "daily internet usage time" afterward.

All of this should be written as automated check scripts, with each rule outputting pass/fail. **Prototype of a crash case**: The questionnaire was revised midway, and the number of options for a question changed from 4 to 5, with two versions of data mixed in the same column — without value range validation, this issue can lurk all the way to the report release.

## Step 2: Response Quality Screening — Clean Up "Human" Issues

In electronic surveys, low-quality responses account for 5%–15% as confirmed repeatedly in literature. Detection metrics are ranked by cost-effectiveness:

1. **Response Duration**: Total duration + duration on key pages. Thresholds can be set using "one-third of the median" or an absolute minimum (e.g., less than 2 seconds per question);
2. **Long Sequences**: The maximum length of consecutive selection of the same option (straight-line answering);
3. **Logical Contradictions**: Pairs of questions in the questionnaire with semantically conflicting answers (it's best to intentionally embed these during design);
4. **Patterned Responses**: ABABAB, 12345 cycles;
5. **Internal Consistency**: Correlation between questions in the same dimension, serious respondents should have high correlation;
6. If IRT modeling is done, **person fit indices** (e.g., lz) can identify more response pattern anomalies.

**Key Discipline**: A single indicator cannot be used to accuse. Each indicator can mislead (a thoughtful respondent may take longer, and an extreme attitude may look like straight-line answering). A mature approach is multi-indicator marking + rule combinations (e.g., if two or more rules are hit, enter review or exclusion), and **rules should be set before looking at the data and written into the plan** — otherwise, "use the deletion method that makes the results significant," and you're engaging in p-hacking.

## Step 3: Missing Data Diagnosis — Three Types of "Empty" Are Three Different Problems

- **Legitimate Skip**: Empty values determined by questionnaire logic, not missing, strictly distinguished from real missing values during coding;
- **Refusal/Non-response**: Missing at the item level, handled according to the missing mechanism (low proportion near random can be deleted; otherwise, multiple imputation or FIML);
- **Not Reached**: Questions not completed due to time constraints in ability assessments, different from the "skip" mechanism, and in large-scale assessments, it's customary to code them separately and handle them differently in the model — scoring all as 0 will systematically wrongfully penalize slow respondents.

**Prototype of a Crash Case**: Refusal code 99 was not converted to NA, directly entered into mean calculations, resulting in a region with "average weekly exercise time of 11 hours" — all extreme aggregated values are worth suspecting the missing code first.

## Step 4: Coding and Derived Variables

Reverse scoring (only once! rename the column after transformation to prevent repeated execution), open-ended question coding, dimension score calculation, and age group variable generation. **Each derived variable should record three things in the codebook: source variables, generation rules, and script location.** The codebook is the most underestimated deliverable in large projects — it is the data's manual, and without it, data becomes a black box after personnel turnover.

## Step 5: External Consistency Check

After cleaning, the data should be compared with external benchmarks: how much does the gender and age structure of the sample deviate from the sampling frame (to prepare for weighting)? Do key indicators align with the previous survey or authoritative statistics? A threefold jump in values should first raise suspicion about the data, not the interpretation of "social upheaval."

## Step 6: Cleaning Report — Make Every Data Point's Journey Traceable

A flowchart: how many entered → how many were deleted by each rule (proportion) → how many final analysis samples. Append definitions and threshold justifications for each rule. In formal projects, this is a mandatory section of the technical report; in thesis writing, it's a shield for external review and defense. For a more refined approach, add a sensitivity analysis: are the core conclusions stable before and after exclusion?

## Engineering Issues When Scale Increases

With over 100,000 samples, cleaning itself becomes a computational task: Excel is out; in R, data.table/arrow can handle millions of rows without difficulty, but you must follow the "push filtering and aggregation down, and clean up intermediate objects promptly" memory discipline. More importantly, **full automation of the entire process** — large projects often receive data in batches and are frequently updated, and manual processes can crash after three batches, while script-based processes are just a rerun.

## Final Touch: Treat Cleaning as a Formal Stage

Allocate budget for the timeline (experience: 30%–50% of the entire analysis period), deliverables (cleaning scripts + codebook + cleaning report), and acceptance criteria (all structural assertions passed, exclusion rules are defensible). Data quality — one minute of rigor upfront beats ten minutes of remediation later.
