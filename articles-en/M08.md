# 08 · Getting to Know IRT's Iconic Curve —— Item Characteristic Curve (ICC)

> If IRT had a "signature poster," it would definitely be this S-shaped curve. Understanding it means you've got the key to deciphering all IRT charts.

## First, Draw a Curve in Your Mind

Close your eyes and imagine a coordinate system:

- **X-axis**: Ability θ, ranging from -3 on the left (low level) to +3 on the right (high level).
- **Y-axis**: The probability of answering the question correctly, ranging from 0 at the bottom (definitely wrong) to 1 at the top (definitely correct).

Now, plot the "probability of answering this question correctly for people at different levels" one by one and connect them into a line. What shape will you get?

An **S-shaped curve that slowly rises from the bottom-left to the top-right**. This is the **Item Characteristic Curve** (Item Characteristic Curve, abbreviated as **ICC**). In IRT, each question corresponds to such a curve.

## This Curve Tells a Very Simple Story

Reading this S-shaped curve from left to right, it conveys a simple and straightforward idea:

> **The higher the level, the higher the probability of answering the question correctly.**

- **Leftmost (low-level individuals)**: The curve is close to the bottom, with a probability of answering correctly near 0 — this question is too difficult for them, and they can't solve it.
- **Middle (moderate-level individuals)**: The curve rises sharply, with the probability of answering correctly jumping from low to high — this is the most "sensitive" interval for this question, where a slight change in level leads to a different outcome.
- **Rightmost (high-level individuals)**: The curve is close to the top, with a probability of answering correctly near 1 — for them, this question is a piece of cake.

That's it. Don't be intimidated by the five characters "Item Characteristic Curve." It's simply drawing a common sense you already know: "The higher the level, the easier it is to answer correctly."

## The Curve Hides the "Personality" of the Question

The magic lies in the fact that this curve is not randomly drawn — **the personality of the question is written in the shape of the curve**:

- **Whether the curve is overall to the left or right** reveals the **difficulty b** of the question. Being to the left (low-level individuals can answer correctly) indicates the question is easy; being to the right (only high-level individuals can answer it) indicates the question is difficult. (Details will be discussed in Chapter 9.)
- **Whether the middle part of the curve is steep or flat** reveals the **discrimination a** of the question. A steep curve (a slight change in level leads to a significant change in probability) indicates high discrimination, meaning the question can effectively differentiate between individuals. A flat curve (a change in level has little effect on the probability) indicates low discrimination. (Details will be discussed in Chapter 10.)
- **Whether the left end of the curve is touching 0 or has a small step up** reveals whether there is a **guessing parameter c**. If it steps up, it means even the worst individuals have a minimum chance of guessing correctly. (Details will be discussed in Chapter 11.)

Therefore, an experienced person can roughly determine whether the question is "difficult, discriminative, or guessable" by just looking at the shape of the ICC without needing to see any numbers. This is the skill of reading charts.

## The Critical Point of "Half Right, Half Wrong"

There is a particularly important point on the ICC: **the point where the probability of answering correctly is exactly 0.5**.

The corresponding x-coordinate (θ value) is the **difficulty b** of the question.

This means: a person whose level is exactly equal to b has a 50% chance of answering the question correctly, making them the "critical group" for this question. Those with a level higher than b have a probability of answering correctly over 50%; those with a level lower than b have a probability of answering correctly under 50%. So, when you look at an ICC, to determine how difficult it is, find the point where the curve crosses the vertical axis at 0.5, then look at the x-coordinate below it — that is the difficulty.

(Tip: If this question has a guessing step, the definition of the "half right, half wrong point" will shift slightly. Chapter 11 will explain this in detail. For now, just remember this main idea.)

## A Life-like Chart Reading Exercise

Imagine you have three ICCs, each belonging to a different question:

- **Question A** has a curve that is overall to the left and very steep: simple and highly discriminative — a beautiful "screening low scorers" question.
- **Question B** has a curve that is overall to the right and very steep: difficult and highly discriminative — a beautiful "screening high scorers" question.
- **Question C** has a flat and slow curve, almost like a horizontal line: extremely low discrimination — regardless of the level, the probability of answering correctly is almost the same. This question is basically not contributing anything, and it should be considered for elimination.

You see, just by looking at the shape, you can roughly determine the quality of the question. Once you finish reading Chapters 9, 10, and 11, your ability to read these charts will be well established.

## Why It's Worth Spending Time to Master ICC

Because almost all charts that come after IRT are "derivatives" of this curve: transforming the ICC of a single question into its information curve (Chapter 12); summing up the information of all questions to get the test information curve (Chapter 13). **ICC is the root, and the rest are the branches.** Once the root is firmly established, the branches will naturally become easy to understand.

## One Sentence to Wrap Up

> **ICC is an S-shaped curve that reflects the idea that the higher the level, the easier it is to answer correctly. Its position from left to right indicates difficulty, the steepness in the middle indicates discrimination, and whether there is a step up on the left end indicates guessing. Learning to read it is like holding the key to understanding all IRT charts.**
