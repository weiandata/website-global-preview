# 06 · IRT Family: 1PL, 2PL, 3PL, and GRM – Who Are They?

> IRT is not a single model, but a family of models. This article introduces the main members of the family, so you just need to recognize their faces, not memorize their family tree.

## Why There Are "Family" Models

Earlier, we mentioned that items have several characteristics: difficulty b, discrimination a, and guessing c.

The members of the IRT family differ in **how many of these characteristics they consider**. Some are lazy and only consider difficulty; others are more meticulous and consider both difficulty and discrimination; some even include guessing. The more parameters a model considers, the closer it is to reality, but it also requires more data and is harder to manage.

The "PL" in their names stands for Parameter Logistic (Parameter Logistic Model), and the number before it indicates **how many parameters are considered**. Therefore:

- **1PL = One parameter**
- **2PL = Two parameters**
- **3PL = Three parameters**

Doesn't that make them less intimidating? Let's meet them one by one.

## The Eldest: 1PL / Rasch Model – Only Difficulty Matters

The 1PL model considers only one parameter: **difficulty b**. It assumes that all items have the same discrimination (everyone has the same ability to distinguish between people) and does not consider guessing.

It is more famously known as the **Rasch model** (named after the Danish statistician Rasch). Strictly speaking, Rasch and 1PL have some subtle philosophical differences, but for beginners, they can be treated as the same for now.

**Personality**: Simple, steady, and requires the smallest sample size (hundreds of people are enough), with the cleanest and most elegant interpretation. Many educational measurement schools (especially in Europe) recognize it, treating "equal item discrimination" as an ideal pursuit in measurement.

**Weakness**: In reality, item discrimination varies, and forcing all items to have the same discrimination can lead to poor model fit for some items.

## The Second: 2PL – Difficulty + Discrimination

The 2PL model considers two parameters: **difficulty b and discrimination a**. It acknowledges that "items differ in their ability to distinguish between people," which is very realistic in psychological scales and attitude questionnaires.

**Personality**: More realistic than 1PL, it is a common choice in social sciences and psychological measurement. The example used earlier when discussing ability estimation (getting more points for answering difficult questions correctly) is based on the 2PL model.

**Cost**: Estimating an additional parameter requires more data, and a sample size of over a thousand is recommended.

## The Third: 3PL – Adding "Guessing"

The 3PL model considers three parameters: difficulty b, discrimination a, and **guessing c**.

The guessing parameter c is specifically for multiple-choice questions: for a four-choice question, even someone who knows nothing has a 25% chance of guessing correctly. The 3PL model acknowledges that "even people with very low ability have a minimum chance of guessing correctly," so its curve does not touch zero on the left end but instead lifts slightly (this "curve that can't lift its head" is discussed in detail in Chapter 11).

**Personality**: It is most suitable for ability assessments with guessing space, such as various standardized tests.

**Cost**: Estimating three parameters together requires the most data and is the least stable. When the sample size is insufficient, the guessing parameter c is often estimated incorrectly, requiring experience and skill to manage.

## Another Branch in the Family: Models for "Multi-Point Scoring"

The above three models are for **two-point scored items** (correct = 1, incorrect = 0). However, in reality, many items are not simply correct or incorrect:

- "Very agree / Agree / Neutral / Disagree / Very disagree" (five-point Likert scale)
- A question that is scored step-by-step (e.g., 0, 1, 2, 3 points for different steps)

For these **multi-point scored items**, another branch of the family is needed. Representative members include:

- **GRM** (Generalized Rating Model): Suitable for Likert-type "ordered scale" attitude scales, which are very common in questionnaires.
- **PCM / GPCM** (Partial Credit Model): Suitable for items scored step-by-step, such as giving half the points for answering half correctly.
- **RSM** (Rating Scale Model): Suitable for items that all use the same scale (e.g., all items in a test use the same five-point scale).

You don't need to distinguish their subtle differences now. Just remember this rule: **if the item is "two-point scored," use 1PL/2PL/3PL; if it is "multi-point scored," use the GRM branch.** For more details, see A10 in our existing content library.

## So, Which One Should You Choose? – A Quick Reference

| Your Item Looks Like This | Prioritize |
| --- | --- |
| Multiple-choice items, want the simplest and most stable, small sample size | 1PL / Rasch |
| Multiple-choice items, item discrimination varies significantly, large sample size | 2PL |
| Multiple-choice items, with clear guessing space, sufficient sample size | 3PL |
| Five-point or seven-point Likert attitude scale | GRM |
| Subjective items scored step-by-step | GPCM / PCM |

Choosing a model is not about complexity. The more parameters a model considers, the closer it is to reality, but it also requires more data and is more prone to errors. **If a simple model can explain it clearly, don't force a complex one** – this is the restraint of an experienced practitioner.

## One-Sentence Conclusion

> **1PL only considers difficulty, 2PL adds discrimination, 3PL adds guessing; multi-point items like Likert scales are handled by the GRM branch. The number in the name indicates how many parameters are considered.**
