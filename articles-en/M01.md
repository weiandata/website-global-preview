# 01 · What is IRT? —— Explaining with Two Metaphors: "An Exam" and "A Scale"

> This is the first article of this series. The goal is only one: after reading this 2000-word article, you should be able to confidently say to someone, "IRT is basically like that," without feeling uncertain.

## Don't Get Turned Off by the Name

IRT stands for Item Response Theory, which is called "Item Response Theory" in Chinese.

This name is not very friendly. "Item" sounds like a construction site, "response" sounds like a chemistry class, and the word "theory" directly turns off half the people. So let's first take off this scary sign and replace it with a simple phrase:

**IRT is a set of methods for "more intelligently scoring answers and simultaneously examining each question."**

That's it. It does two things: first, it translates a person's performance into a more reliable "level score"; second, it thoroughly understands the nature of each question (how hard it is, how useful it is). All the curves, formulas, and Greek letters are just tools to serve these two purposes.

## Metaphor 1: An Exam with Different Difficulty Levels

Imagine two students taking a math test. Xiao Ming scores 85, and Xiao Hong scores 78. Who is better at math?

You will instinctively ask: **Did they take the same test?**

Exactly, the problem is here. If Xiao Ming's test was easy as giving away points, and Xiao Hong's test was hard as an Olympiad, then 85 might not be better than 78. Scores are inherently "attached" to the test—you can't talk about score differences without the test.

Traditional scoring methods (adding one point for each correct answer and counting the total) have an unavoidable flaw: **scores always follow the test.** Change the test, and the same person's score changes; with the same test, change the group of people, and the "difficulty" of the questions seems to change. People and questions are mixed together, like a pot of unmixable porridge.

The first major thing IRT does is **separate this pot of porridge**: one side is "what level is this person" (regardless of which test they took), and the other side is "how difficult is this question" (regardless of who answers it). This way, even if Xiao Ming and Xiao Hong took different tests, as long as the tests have some "common questions" as a bridge, their levels can be measured on the same scale and truly compared.

This seems simple, but it is the foundation of modern large-scale assessments. International assessments like PISA and TIMSS, and domestic large-scale surveys on scientific literacy, student scientific literacy, and digital literacy, all rely on IRT's ability to "separate people and questions" to draw conclusions like "has this year improved compared to last year" or "are urban children stronger than rural children."

## Metaphor 2: A Scale That Reports Error

Now imagine you bought a scale.

A regular scale gives you a number: 65.0 kg. But it doesn't tell you that it measures accurately only within the 60 to 70 kg range, and it's wildly inaccurate when measuring a 20 kg child or a 150 kg adult. It pretends to be accurate for everyone, but in reality, it has no confidence.

Traditional scoring methods are a bit like this scale: it gives the whole test an "reliability coefficient," as if it measures everyone accurately. But reality is not that perfect.

Where is the brilliance of IRT's "scale"? It **clearly tells you what level of people it measures accurately and what level it measures poorly.** It has something called an "information function" (which will be discussed in detail in Articles 12 and 13), which is a curve that shows clearly: this test measures people of medium ability very accurately, but it's vague for those who are very strong or very weak.

This has huge value in practice. For example, if you need to make a high-stakes decision at the "passing score" — issuing a certificate if you pass, and requiring a retake if you don't — you should most care about **how accurate the scale is near the passing score.** IRT can directly answer this, while traditional methods can only give you a vague "overall average is okay."

## What Are θ, a, b, and ICC?

You will eventually come across these symbols. Here's a brief introduction to get familiar with them, as each will be covered in its own dedicated article:

- **θ (theta)**: A person's "level value." Note that it is not "how many questions you got right," but rather an abstract, test-independent ability scale.
- **b**: The "difficulty" of a question. The larger the b, the harder the question, and the higher the ability required to answer it.
- **a**: The "discrimination" of a question, or its ability to differentiate between strong and weak performers. A good question can clearly separate high performers from novices.
- **ICC**: The Item Characteristic Curve, an S-shaped curve, which is the iconic image of IRT. It shows how the probability of answering a question correctly changes as a person's level increases.

It's fine if you can't remember all of this now. Just leave an impression: **IRT gives each person a level value, each question several parameters, and then connects them with an S-shaped curve.**

## One Sentence to Wrap Up

If you can only take one sentence away, take this one:

> **Traditional scoring counts how many questions you got right; IRT figures out "what level you are" and "what kind of question each one is," and then allows different people, different tests, and different years to be fairly compared.**
