# 03 · Items Also Have "Personality": What Do Difficulty and Discrimination Really Mean

> In IRT, every item has its own personality. In this article, we'll conduct a "personality test" for items, and get to know its two most important traits: difficulty and discrimination.

## Items Are Not Just Tools; They Have "Personas"

When we look at items, we usually only care about whether they are correct or not. But in IRT, each item is a character with a file and an ID card. Its file at least includes two key attributes:

- **Difficulty** (symbol b): How difficult is this item?
- **Discrimination** (symbol a): How good is this item at distinguishing between high performers and low performers?

Understanding these two aspects will give you a good grasp of an item's personality. Let's go through them one by one.

## Difficulty b: How Selective Is This Item?

Difficulty is easy to understand — it's the level of ability needed to have a 50% chance of answering the item correctly.

Note this definition: **In IRT, difficulty is defined as the ability level at which the probability of answering correctly is exactly 50%**.

Let's take an example. An item with difficulty b = 0 means that a person with average ability (θ = 0) has a 50% chance of getting it right. People with higher ability have more than a 50% chance of getting it right, while people with lower ability have less than a 50% chance.

Another item with b = 2 is a tough one — only people with high ability (θ = 2) have a 50% chance of getting it right. Ordinary people are basically guessing.

On the other hand, b = -2 is an easy item. People with low ability (θ = -2) already have a 50% chance of getting it right, and people with average or higher ability can almost always get it right.

**Remember this in one sentence: the higher the b, the harder the item, and the more selective it is.** There's an intuitive but important point here — difficulty and ability are measured on the same scale (θ). So we can directly say, "This item's difficulty matches the ability level of this person," just like using the same ruler to measure both height and door height to determine whether the door is high enough.

## Discrimination a: Does This Item "Tailor Its Questions to the Person"?

Discrimination is a concept many people hear for the first time, but it actually aligns well with intuition.

**A good item should do one thing: make it easier for high-ability people to get it right and easier for low-ability people to get it wrong, clearly distinguishing them.** This ability to clearly distinguish is what we call discrimination.

Imagine two items with similar difficulty:

- **Item A**: High-ability people get it right almost all the time, low-ability people get it wrong almost all the time, and people with intermediate ability have a smooth transition in their probability of getting it right. This is high discrimination — like a clear dividing line, separating people by ability in one test.
- **Item B**: High-ability and low-ability people have similar probabilities of getting it right, and it's mostly a matter of luck. This is low discrimination — it has little to say about who is stronger or weaker, and it's like a fake expert.

On a graph (which we'll discuss in detail in the next article), high discrimination items are represented by a "steep" S-shaped curve, where a slight change in ability leads to a significant change in the probability of getting it right. Low discrimination items are "flat" like a lazy slope, where ability changes a lot but the probability remains almost the same.

**Why is discrimination so valuable?** Because the "discriminating power" of a test is almost entirely supported by items with high discrimination. Low discrimination items take up space, consume test-takers' time, but provide almost no useful information. When managing an item bank, identifying and eliminating low discrimination items is the most basic and effective step (we'll explain how to do this step-by-step in Articles 40 and 41).

## A Life-like Analogy: Interview Questions

Think of a test as an interview, and items as the interviewer's questions.

- **Difficulty** is the "threshold" of this question. "1 + 1 equals what?" has a very low threshold (b is very small), while "Please prove the Riemann Hypothesis" has a very high threshold (b is very large).
- **Discrimination** is how accurate this question is at "screening people." "What is your name?" is a question everyone can answer, and it screens out no one (discrimination ≈ 0, which is just a waste of time). "Given this business scenario, how would you design a solution?" — high performers speak fluently, while average performers stumble, and this question clearly separates people into different levels (high discrimination).

A good interviewer uses a bunch of **questions with difficulty covering various levels and high discrimination**. A good test is the same. The value of IRT is that it quantifies each item's "threshold" and "screening ability," allowing you to optimize your questionnaire like an HR optimizing an item bank.

## Are There Any Other Traits?

Yes, but there's just one more: **the guessing parameter c** — which specifically describes the possibility of getting an item right by pure guess, typical in multiple-choice questions (e.g., four options, guessing blindly gives a 25% chance of being correct). We'll discuss this in detail in Article 11.

Difficulty b, discrimination a, and guessing c are the three most common item parameters in IRT, and together they form the "three-parameter model" we'll discuss later. Right now, just firmly grasp the first two most important ones:

> **Difficulty b refers to "how selective this item is," and discrimination a refers to "how good this item is at distinguishing people." The former determines the position, and the latter determines the steepness.**
