# 15 · Wright Map (Wright Map): Putting "People" and "Items" on the Same Scale

> This is the most intuitive and suitable diagram for outsiders to understand in IRT. Understanding it will truly allow you to experience the elegance of "separating people and items, and comparing them on the same scale."

## One Diagram, Two Things, One Scale

Earlier, we repeatedly emphasized IRT's core strength: **placing people's ability (θ) and item difficulty (b) on the same scale.**

The Wright Map (also called the Item-Person Map or Item-Response Distribution Map) is a visual representation of this concept. It looks like this:

- In the middle is a **vertical scale** (the θ/b scale, which goes upward from low to high).
- On the **left side** of the scale: people are arranged by their ability level — those with higher ability are placed above, and those with lower ability are placed below. Usually, this is drawn as a histogram or a series of small dots.
- On the **right side** of the scale: items are arranged by difficulty — difficult items are placed above, and easy items are placed below. Each item is marked with a point.

Thus, **people and items share the same vertical axis**, and who is higher or lower, and who matches with whom, is immediately clear. This is the "panoramic view" that other diagrams cannot provide.

## How to Read This Diagram

The core idea is one sentence: **At the same height, the people on the left and the items on the right are "well-matched."**

Because difficulty and ability are measured on the same scale, we can say:

- A person placed at a certain height, the items at the same height on the right are the ones that are "half-right, half-wrong" — the most challenging items for that person.
- Items below that height (lower difficulty) are likely to be answered correctly by the person.
- Items above that height (higher difficulty) are likely to be answered incorrectly.

This is like using the same scale to measure both "people's height" and "doorframe height" — placing them together immediately reveals who can walk through which door without bending.

## The Problems It Is Best at Revealing: Misalignment Between People and Items

The greatest value of the Wright Map is its ability to immediately reveal whether **the difficulty of the items matches the ability level of the population**. Several typical misalignments include:

**Misalignment 1: People are at the top, items are at the bottom.**
People on the left are generally placed at a high level, while items on the right are all clustered at the bottom. This means: **the ability level of this group is generally higher than the difficulty of your items.** The items are too easy, and everyone answers them correctly, so you cannot distinguish the stronger individuals among them (ceiling effect). Solution: Add more difficult items.

**Misalignment 2: People are at the bottom, items are at the top.**
The opposite is true — people are all at the bottom, and items are all at the top. **The items are too difficult** — most people cannot answer them, resulting in a lot of blank responses, and you cannot distinguish the slightly stronger individuals among the weaker ones (floor effect). Solution: Add more simple items.

**Misalignment 3: There is a gap in the middle.**
Items on the right are missing in a certain ability range (there are no items at that height), but there are many people exactly at that ability level. This means: **this group of people needs items that match their ability level, but you don't have them.** The measurement of this group will be weak. Solution: Add items in that difficulty range.

**Ideal State:** The distribution of people on the left and the difficulty range of items on the right should **roughly overlap and cover the full range** without obvious misalignment or gaps. Only then can you say, "My items are suitable for the people I want to measure."

## Why It Is Especially Suitable for Outsiders

Earlier, we discussed ICC and information functions, which are somewhat abstract. But the Wright Map requires almost no explanation — just place the diagram in front of your client:

> "Look, the people on the left are the participants in the assessment — the higher up, the stronger they are. The items on the right are the questions — the higher up, the more difficult they are. They are measured on the same scale. Now you can immediately see: do our items (on the right) cover the ability range of this group (on the left)?"

Even a leader who doesn't understand statistics can look at the diagram and nod: "Oh, there are no items at that upper level — does that mean the high-ability group wasn't measured accurately?" — **This is a good diagram because it allows outsiders to see the problem themselves.** It is a powerful tool for reporting, proposals, and explaining methods to stakeholders (it will be used again in Chapter 45).

## A Small Reminder

The most classic and beautiful form of the Wright Map comes from the Rasch / 1PL model (because there, "item difficulty" is a clean single point). When using the 2PL or 3PL models, since items also have discrimination and guessing parameters, each item is no longer just a single point on the map — the drawing becomes more refined. However, the core interpretation of "people and items on the same scale, seeing whether they match" remains unchanged.

## One Sentence to Conclude

> **The Wright Map places people and items on the same vertical scale: people on the left (the higher, the stronger), items on the right (the higher, the more difficult). People and items at the same height are the best match. It is especially good at immediately revealing whether the items are too hard, too easy, or have gaps, and it is understandable even to outsiders — it is a reporting tool.**
