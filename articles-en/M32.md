# 32 · How Good of a Computer Do You Need to Run IRT?

> Many people think that "statistical modeling" requires a supercomputer or a top-tier workstation. This article dispels that misconception: most IRT analyses can be run on your current computer.

## First, a conclusion that will ease your mind

**Most routine IRT analyses can be run on a regular modern laptop — no special hardware is needed.**

You don't need a graphics card (GPU), a server, or a "deep learning furnace." A computer that can smoothly open web pages, play videos, and run Office is sufficient for daily IRT modeling. IRT is not deep learning; it has much more moderate hardware requirements.

## What resources does IRT consume?

IRT analysis mainly consumes two things:

**1. Memory (RAM) — the most important one to pay attention to.**
During parameter estimation, the program needs to load data and intermediate results into memory for repeated calculations. The larger the data (more people, more items), the more memory it consumes.

- **Routine projects (thousands of people, dozens of items):** **8GB of memory** is more than enough.
- **Larger projects (tens of thousands of people, hundreds of items, or polytomous/multidimensional):** **16GB** will be more comfortable.
- **Symptoms of insufficient memory:** The program becomes extremely slow, or it directly reports "out of memory" and crashes.

**2. CPU (processor) — affects how long it takes to run.**
Estimation is an iterative process (see post 19), so the faster and more cores the CPU has, the faster the computation. However, this only affects "how many seconds or minutes you wait" — it does not affect "whether it can run at all." A slower CPU is manageable by taking a cup of tea and waiting.

**Conclusion:** **Memory is the ceiling (determines whether it can run), CPU is the speed (determines how fast it runs).** Prioritize memory.

## How long does it take for different project sizes?

Here's a rough sense of how long it takes (actual speed depends on the model, software, and computer, for reference):

| Project Size | Sense on a Regular Laptop |
| --- | --- |
| Hundreds of people × dozens of items, Rasch/2PL | Seconds to tens of seconds — a blink of an eye |
| Thousands of people × dozens of items, 2PL/GRM | Tens of seconds to minutes |
| Tens of thousands of people × hundreds of items, or multidimensional | Minutes to tens of minutes, possibly a bit challenging |
| Millions of respondents, or complex multidimensional models | Regular computers start to struggle, optimization or stronger machines are needed |

Do you see the pattern? **Most routine projects with a few thousand people are in the "take a sip of water and it's done" range.** The real hardware-heavy projects are those with millions of respondents or complex multidimensional models — "national team level" big projects.

## What if you really can't run it?

If you really encounter ultra-large data (millions of respondents) or complex models, and your regular computer can't handle it, here are a few options:

1. **Switch to a more resource-efficient model or algorithm:** Simpler models use less memory; some estimation algorithms are specifically optimized for big data.
2. **Use better software/tools:** The efficiency of the same data can vary dramatically between different software. Choosing an estimation engine optimized for large-scale scenarios can turn "can't run" into "run easily." This is exactly one of the problems our self-developed open-source estimation engine **IRTC** aims to solve — making IRT estimation for millions of respondents both fast and memory-efficient (open on GitHub, feel free to search if interested).
3. **Upgrade to a stronger machine / cloud server:** Temporarily rent a large-memory cloud server, run it, and shut it down — cost-effective.
4. **Chunking / sampling:** In some scenarios, you can first calibrate on a representative subsample and then generalize.

**But remember:** These are all considerations for "big projects." As an beginner, 99% of your practice and routine projects can be handled by your current computer. **Don't be discouraged by the imagined hardware threshold of "my computer isn't good enough."**

## A quick note on software "size"

Besides hardware, some people worry about whether "software is hard to install and takes up a lot of space." Don't worry:

- The most commonly used software for IRT, **R** (which we'll discuss in the next post), is **free**, has a small installation package, and can be installed on a regular computer in a few minutes.
- Most commonly used IRT analysis packages are also free and open-source, and take up very little space.
- You can have a full set of professional-level IRT analysis capabilities without spending any money or taking up much space — this is the charm of the open-source ecosystem.

## Final summary in one sentence

> **Most IRT analyses can be run on a regular modern laptop: memory is the ceiling (8–16GB is sufficient), CPU determines speed. Routine projects with a few thousand people take seconds to minutes to run. Only projects with millions of respondents or complex multidimensional models require optimization, stronger machines, or specialized estimation engines. Don't be discouraged by the imagined hardware threshold.**
