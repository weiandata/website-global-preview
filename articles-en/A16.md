# A16 · Detecting Careless Responding: Method Checklist, Strategy Combinations, and Ethical Boundaries

> A deeper version of Q10. Can be split into a Xiaohongshu "Careless Responding Checklist" card.

## This Is Not a Minor Issue

Meade & Craig (2012) and other researchers repeatedly provide the same magnitude of estimates: 5%–15% of responses in online questionnaires are of low quality. Don't underestimate this adult — simulation studies show that 10% of random responding is enough to dilute real group differences to non-significance, allow "garbage factors" to appear in clean factor structures, and make scale reliability appear artificially low. **Detecting careless responding is not about being overly picky; it is a battle for validity.**

## Two Lines of Defense: Design Embedding + Statistical Detection

### Line One: Design Embedding (Low Cost, Strong Evidence)

1. **Instruction Check Item (IMC)**: "Please select 'Somewhat Disagree' for this item." Directly tests attention; 1–2 items are sufficient, hidden in the middle of a long item set;
2. **Self-Reported Quality Item**: At the end of the questionnaire, ask, "Did you answer this questionnaire seriously? Is your data suitable for research? (Honest answers do not affect your compensation)" — seemingly naive, but empirical evidence consistently shows it is effective and complementary to other indicators;
3. **Non-Existence Item**: Ask respondents to evaluate fictional items ("I am familiar with the 'Quantum Reading Method' course"), and mark those who claim familiarity;
4. **Contradictory Pairs**: Pairs of items with opposite semantic meanings, and mark those who answer them consistently.

The advantage of design embedding is **evidence interpretability** — "the respondent failed the instruction check item" is much easier to explain in a report than "Mahalanobis distance exceeds the threshold."

### Line Two: Statistical Detection (Can Be Done Without Embedding)

Sorted by cost-effectiveness:

1. **Response Time**: Total time + page-level time. The first indicator for electronic surveys. Threshold experience: 2 seconds per item is the physical minimum for "reading the question"; or use one-third of the median. Note that long time is not a problem (may have taken a phone call in between), **short time is**;
2. **Long String**: The maximum length of consecutive identical responses. `careless::longstring()`. A sequence of 15 "Somewhat Agree" responses in a Likert matrix item is unlikely to be explained by a genuine attitude;
3. **Individual Response Variability (IRV)**: Mark respondents with low overall standard deviation (complementary to long string);
4. **Odd-Even Consistency / Repeated Consistency**: Within-individual correlation of split-half items on the same dimension; serious respondents should have higher consistency;
5. **Mahalanobis Distance**: Multivariate outlier, capturing "pattern anomalies";
6. **Person Fit**: IRT-based lz/lz* indicators, capturing patterns like "answering difficult items correctly but simple items incorrectly." A standard procedure for test data;
7. **Psychometric Antonyms/Synonyms**: Within-individual correlation of semantically similar item pairs, advanced users can use this.

The R package `careless` covers items 2–5 and 7, with one metric per line.

## Strategy Combinations: How to Use These Indicators Without Causing Issues

**Principle One: Use Multiple Indicators, Intersect Strictly, Union Broadly.** Each indicator has a false positive rate (extreme attitudes may appear as linear responding; thoughtful respondents may have atypical response times). Recommended two-tier handling: hit ≥2 items → remove; hit 1 item → sensitivity analysis (run twice, once with and without removal, to check stability of conclusions).

**Principle Two: Rules Before Results.** The criteria for removal (which indicators to use, what thresholds, and what combination rules) **must be written into the analysis plan before seeing the results.** Post-hoc selection based on "significance of results" is standard p-hacking. Registered studies should directly register this set of rules.

**Principle Three: Transparent Reporting.** The number of hits per rule, total removal rate, and demographic differences between removed and retained respondents (is removal systematically biased toward a particular group? This relates to sample representativeness).

**Principle Four: Removal Is Not the Only Option.** In low-stakes studies, full questionnaire removal is acceptable; in formal surveys, some scenarios are better suited for "downweighting" or "marking and comparing in robustness analysis" — especially when removal rates are high and the hit group is concentrated in a specific population, as deleting them outright may introduce new biases.

## Ethical and Governance Boundaries

- In paid surveys, the rule "no compensation for poor data quality" must be **communicated in advance**;
- Quality judgments of individual respondents should not be fed back as any form of criticism or label;
- In large-scale formal surveys, quality screening rules are part of the technical solution and should be approved through the project governance process, not the analyst's on-the-spot decision — in our large projects, we typically write this as an independent section, reviewed at the same level as sampling and weighting schemes.

## Final Reminder on Order

The position of careless responding detection in the cleaning process: after structural validation, before missing data handling (the "missing" of careless respondents and the "missing" of serious respondents are two different things).
