# A06 · A Complete Guide to Sampling Weights: From Design Weights to Calibration

> A deeper version accompanying Q11. A core long article in the survey statistics business line.

## Weights are the "Translator" of Survey Data

A sample is never a scaled-down copy of the population: some groups are intentionally oversampled (to produce separate results), some are naturally hard to reach (high non-response rates), and others are simply the result of sampling design probabilities. **The role of weights is to translate "what is seen in the sample" back to "what holds true in the population."** Skipping this step and proceeding to analysis will only report the story of this particular sample.

A proper survey's weights are composed of three segments, each addressing a different deviation.

## First Segment: Design Weights — Reconstructing the Sampling Blueprint

The principle in one sentence: **Each person's weight equals the inverse of their probability of being selected.** A rural elderly person with a 1/2000 probability of being selected represents 2000 similar individuals.

In complex designs, probabilities are multiplied step by step. For example, in a typical three-stage design: first, districts are sampled (probability p1, often proportional to population size, i.e., PPS), then schools or communities within districts are sampled (p2), and finally individuals are sampled (p3) — the final probability is p1 × p2 × p3, and the design weight is its inverse.

**Practical Points**: The probability at each stage of sampling must be **recorded on-site**. Remembering "how this county was sampled" during analysis is the real reason why countless weight calculations fail. The sampling plan and the weight plan should be two chapters of the same document.

## Second Segment: Non-Response Adjustment — Correcting "Who Didn't Come"

Sampling 1000 people, with 700 completing the survey. If the 300 who didn't respond were randomly missing, it's just a smaller sample; but in reality, non-response is highly selective — young professionals are hard to reach, and high-income individuals may refuse to answer sensitive surveys. Without adjustment, the data will systematically favor those who are easier to reach.

Main approach: Use known information from the sampling frame (region, housing type, gender, age, etc.) to build a **response propensity model** (logistic regression or stratified cells), and divide the completed respondents' weights by their estimated response rate. Intuitively, completed young professionals need to "speak for" those who were not reached, so their weights are increased.

**Honesty at the Boundary**: Non-response adjustment can only correct the absence that is explained by observable variables. If non-response is directly driven by the survey content itself (e.g., extreme views leading to refusal in a views survey), this is considered non-negligible non-response, and any weighting cannot fix it — only a sensitivity discussion in the report is possible.

## Third Segment: Calibration — Aligning with the Known Population Structure

After the first two segments, the weighted sample structure still has residuals compared to authoritative population data (e.g., census). Calibration is the final step: **adjust the weights so that the weighted marginal distribution of the sample precisely matches the known population marginal distribution.**

The methodology spectrum ranges from simple to general:

- **Post-stratification**: Directly scale the weight proportions to the population shares by cell (gender × age group), but this can become fragmented when there are many cells;
- **Raking (Iterative Proportional Fitting)**: Only match the marginal distributions of each dimension (gender marginal, age marginal, urban-rural marginal), without requiring the joint distribution, which is the most commonly used;
- **General Calibration (Deville & Särndal, 1992)**: Incorporate all the above into a unified framework — find new weights that satisfy the marginal equations under the constraint of being "closest to the initial weights," and can include lower bounds to prevent extreme weights. R's `survey` and `sampling` packages have implementations.

Additional benefit of calibration: Variance of estimates related to calibration variables will decrease (similar to the efficiency gain of regression estimation).

## Weight Quality Control

1. **Diagnosing Extreme Weights**: An individual weight that is disproportionately large means that a few people are "capturing" the estimate. Monitoring indicators: weight coefficient of variation, **effective sample size ESS = (Σw)² / Σw²** — when ESS drops from 10,000 to 3,000, it indicates that weighting has "lost" 70% of the information, and trimming should be considered;
2. **Awareness of the Cost of Trimming**: Trimming reduces variance but introduces bias. Operations must be documented and compared before and after;
3. **Variance Estimation Must Be Accompanied**: Standard errors after weighting cannot be calculated as if it were simple random sampling; instead, use linearization or resampling (Jackknife/BRR/bootstrap). In R, the `survey` package's `svydesign()` function declares the design once, and all subsequent analyses are automatically correct — **the mean with weighting and the standard error as if it were SRS is the most common error in published work**;
4. **Documentation**: Record the inputs, methods, and parameters for each step of the three segments of weights in the technical report. Weights are the first thing that will be pulled out when questioned.

## Three Frequently Asked Questions

**"Does a large sample mean you don't need weighting?"** — Bias does not disappear with n; it only becomes more precisely estimated. A crooked ruler still measures a million times as crooked.

**"Do you need to weight in regression analysis?"** — Descriptive inference (population mean/proportion/distribution) must be weighted; analytical inference (mechanism, coefficients) is debated in academia. A cautious approach is to run both weighted and unweighted analyses — large differences are themselves important diagnostics (indicating that the model is missing structure related to sampling).

**"Can a convenience sample be rescued by weighting?"** — Calibration can align the gender and age structure of a social media survey with the census, but it cannot fix the fundamental selection bias that "those willing to fill out are inherently special." Weighting is a bonus for probability samples, not a resurrection for convenience samples.

## Conclusion

Weighting work is tedious, boring, and unremarkable, but it is the foundation of the legitimacy of survey conclusions — national-level survey weighting schemes often span dozens of pages of technical documentation, not out of bureaucracy, but because this step truly warrants it. Our experience in processing multiple large-scale survey datasets: **The rigor of the weighting scheme is the fastest way to distinguish between "professional surveys" and "big survey questionnaires."**
