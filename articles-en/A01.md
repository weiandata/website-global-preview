# A01 · What is IRT: When "Summing Scores" Is No Longer Enough, How Does Measurement Science Respond?

## Starting with a Paradox

Two students take math tests from different schools. Student A scores 85, and Student B scores 78. Who has better math skills?

You instinctively realize: it's impossible to compare — the two tests have different difficulty levels. Student A's test might have been much easier.

Now scale up the problem: a national survey wants to know whether citizens' scientific literacy is improving year by year, but the questions can't be exactly the same every year (questions may leak or become outdated); a province wants to compare reading ability between urban and rural students, but the two groups take slightly different difficulty levels of test books. **As long as "scores" depend on "which questions are encountered," cross-test, cross-year, and cross-group comparisons are all just castles in the air.**

This is the fundamental limitation of classical scoring methods (adding one point for each correct answer, and then summing up). Item Response Theory (IRT) was developed precisely to break through this limitation — it is the most significant advancement in measurement science over the past half-century, and the methodological foundation for international assessments like PISA and TIMSS, as well as various literacy monitoring systems in China.

## Core Idea: Separating "People" and "Items"

In Classical Test Theory (CTT), the only thing that matters is the score: total score, pass rate, and reliability coefficient are all mixed products of "this group of people × this test." When people change, the indicators change; when the test changes, the scores change — they are entangled together.

IRT performs a conceptual "decoupling":

- Each **person** has a latent ability value, denoted as θ (theta) — it is not "how many questions were answered correctly," but an abstract, test-independent quantity;
- Each **item** has its own parameters: at least difficulty b (how difficult the item is for people of a certain level), and possibly discrimination a (the item's ability to differentiate between strong and weak performers);
- The probability of a person answering an item correctly is determined by both. For example, in the classic two-parameter model (2PL):

$$P(\text{Correct}) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-a(\theta - b)}}$$

Don't worry about this formula — it says something very straightforward: **the higher your ability θ is compared to the item difficulty b, the higher the probability of answering correctly**; a controls how quickly this probability increases with ability. Plotting this probability against θ results in an S-shaped curve, called the item characteristic curve (ICC) — everything in IRT is built on this curve.

## What Did This Decoupling Buy Us

**1. Parameter Invariance: Cross-test Comparisons Are Now Possible.**
When the model assumptions hold, item parameters do not change with the examinee group, and ability estimates do not change with item combinations. As long as this year's test and last year's test share some common items (anchor items), all items from both years can be placed on the same "ruler," making "this year's θ = 0.4" and "last year's θ = 0.2" truly comparable. All conclusions about "trends over time" in continuous monitoring projects are technically based on this property.

**2. Measurement Precision Is No Longer a Single Number, but a Curve.**
CTT provides a single reliability coefficient for the entire test, as if it measures everyone equally accurately. IRT's information function tells you: this test measures people with moderate ability accurately, but poorly measures those at the extremes. This has huge practical value — if your test is used for high-stakes decisions near the cut score, you should check whether the information is sufficient near the cut score, and even consider adding more items in that difficulty range.

**3. Each Item Now Has an "ID Card."**
Difficulty, discrimination, fit, and whether it favors specific groups (DIF) all become testable item properties. Item bank construction, adaptive testing (giving harder items if correct, easier ones if incorrect), and refined management of missing items all stem from this.

## A Concrete Feeling: Two "60s" Are Not the Same

Imagine two people who both answered 12 out of 20 questions correctly. Student A answered 12 easy questions and got all the hard ones wrong; Student B's answers are mixed, and he also got two very hard questions right. In total score terms, they are the same; in IRT terms, Student B's response pattern points to a higher ability estimate (of course, if Student B also got many easy questions wrong, the model will "raise a flag" — such abnormal patterns can be detected using person fit indices, which are also tools for identifying careless responding and cheating).

**Total scores discard the information in the response pattern, while IRT recovers it.**

## IRT Is Not a Free Lunch

After discussing the benefits, we must also mention the costs — this is the basis for deciding whether to use IRT:

1. **It is a statistical model with assumptions.** The most basic are unidimensionality (the set of items measures the same construct) and local independence (items are independent once ability is controlled). If the assumptions break, the output is just garbage;
2. **Parameter estimation requires sample size.** The Rasch model starts with hundreds of people, and 2PL/multidimensional models recommend over a thousand. Using IRT for a class of thirty students is like performance art;
3. **Computation and interpretation are more complex.** θ = 0.83 cannot be directly explained to parents, and in practice, you need to perform scale transformation (e.g., converting to a reported score of 500 ± 100); estimation itself is a significant computational task in large samples and multiple dimensions — estimating millions of examinees requires performance optimization (which is what our self-developed open-source estimation engine IRTC solves, though we won't go into detail here; interested readers can search for it on GitHub).

**Simple criterion:** One-time, small-sample, and no cross-test comparisons — stick with CTT, and it's completely sufficient; large-scale, cross-year, cross-test, and needing to build an item bank or perform fairness checks — IRT is not an option, it's a must.

## If You Want to Get Started

Conceptually, explain the terms "ICC, difficulty, discrimination, θ" well enough to teach someone else; operationally, install the mirt package in R, run through the built-in examples, and then read the textbook (in Chinese, see Dai Haiqi's *Psychological and Educational Measurement*; in English, the first recommendation is *Embretson & Reise*). Run through the examples first, then read the book — don't reverse the order.
