# 42 · Score Report: Translating θ into Language Customers Understand

> You've calculated θ for each person. But you can't just tell parents, "Your child's θ is 0.83." This article explains how to translate abstract ability values into score reports that everyone can understand. This is the final step from "analysis" to "delivery."

## Problem: θ is for machines, not for people

The θ scale (from Article 14) has many features that are not user-friendly for ordinary people:

- It has **negative numbers** (e.g., -1.5? My child got a negative score?)
- It is a **decimal** (what is 0.83?)
- It has **no unit or reference** (how do we know if it's high or low?)

θ is an internal "production scale" used for precise and convenient work, but directly handing it to customers will only result in confusion or even panic. Therefore, a "translation" is necessary before delivery. The translation has three levels, each adding more depth.

## Translation Level 1: Convert θ into a Understandable Score

The first and most basic step is to convert θ into a more understandable score through a simple linear transformation (multiplying by a number and adding another number). The most classic example is "**average 500, standard deviation 100**" (the approach used by large-scale assessments like PISA):

- θ = 0 (average) → **Report Score 500**
- θ = 1 → **600**
- θ = -1 → **400**

After conversion, the numbers are all positive, whole numbers, and have clear references (500 is the average line). Customers can immediately understand, "My child scored 560, which is above the average line of 500."

**Key Point**: This is just "changing the outfit," θ and the report score are one-to-one, the information remains the same, but it becomes more understandable. The choice of scale (e.g., 500 ± 100 or something else) depends on project habits. The key is to ensure **consistency, reference, and positive whole numbers**.

## Translation Level 2: Give the Score a "Reference Frame"

Having just numbers is not enough; people need to know "**who they are comparing to**" to feel the meaning. Common reference methods include:

- **Percentile**: "Your child outperformed 78% of peers of the same age" — this phrase is more intuitive than any score. Percentiles are almost universally understood.
- **Level / Tier**: Divide continuous scores into several tiers (e.g., "Developing / Basic / Proficient / Excellent") and tell customers where they fall. Managers and parents often care more about the **tier** than the exact score.
- **Group Comparison**: "The average score of this school is 540, higher than the district average of 510" — this is for institutional clients to understand trends and relative positions.

**In One Sentence**: **Scores need a reference frame.** "600 points" is less meaningful than "outperformed 78% of people."

## Translation Level 3: Include "Error," but in Human Language

As emphasized in Article 21, θ is an estimate with error. Professional reports should reflect this, but **you shouldn't use "standard error 0.2" as black language to scare customers**. Instead, translate it into human language:

- ❌ "θ = 1.0, standard error 0.2"
- ✅ "Your child's level is roughly equivalent to a report score of 600. Considering normal measurement fluctuations, the reliable range is 580 to 620."

This third layer is especially important in **cut-score judgments**. If a person's score is exactly on the passing line, and the error range spans both sides, a responsible report should explain, "This case is close to the threshold; it is recommended to make a judgment based on other information," rather than making a hard decision of "pass" or "fail." **Honestly presenting uncertainty is a demonstration of professionalism and trust, not a sign of weakness.**

## A Complete "Translation" Demonstration

Combining the three layers, a friendly expression for parents can be:

> "In this science literacy assessment, your child's score is **560** (the average score is 500), **exceeding about 72% of peers of the same age**, and is at the **proficient** level. Considering normal measurement error, the true level is reliably between **540 to 580**, consistently in the mid-upper range."

Compare this with the original "θ = 0.6, SE = 0.2" — the same information, one that is reassuring and clear, and one that is confusing. **This is the value of translation.**

## A Few Translation Rules

1. **Don't lose or fabricate information**: Translation is about changing the expression, not exaggerating or embellishing. The score is what it is.
2. **Align with the audience**: Explain percentiles and levels to parents, retain more technical details for researchers, and discuss group trends for managers. The same result should be **translated differently for different audiences**.
3. **Retain traceability**: The report score is easy to understand, but internally, keep θ and the conversion relationship for easy verification and cross-year comparisons (equating is done on the θ scale, as discussed in Article 44).

## Final Summary in One Sentence

> **θ is for machines, and before delivery, it needs three layers of translation: convert it into an understandable report score (e.g., 500 ± 100), pair it with a reference frame (percentile, level, or group comparison), and express the error range in human language (especially when near a cut score). Translation is about changing the expression, not fabricating information. It also requires aligning with the audience and retaining traceability. Making customers feel secure and informed is the value of this final step.**
