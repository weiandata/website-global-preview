# 05 · How IRT Differs from the "Scoring" We Usually Talk About

> In the previous few articles, we've mentioned the differences in a scattered way. This article will lay them out in a comparison table, so you can understand them all at once. After reading this, you'll be able to explain this to outsiders at a dinner table.

## One-Sentence Introduction

**Ordinary scoring focuses on "how many points you got"; IRT focuses on "what level you're at and whether this test is suitable for making that judgment."**

The difference sounds subtle, but when you look at it closely, it's different in many ways. We'll compare them from six different angles.

## Angle One: Scoring Object — Entire Test vs. Each Item

Ordinary scoring focuses only on the "entire test": total score, pass rate, average score — all are attributes of the whole test. You can't tell whether a particular item is good or not just from the total score.

IRT focuses on **each item** individually: each item has its own difficulty, discrimination, and fit. The test is just a collection of these items. Because IRT focuses on individual items, it's possible to "eliminate poor items, retain good ones, and build tests as needed."

**Analogy**: Ordinary scoring is like giving a whole meal a total score of "this meal is 80 points"; IRT is like a Michelin reviewer, who gives each dish a separate review and score, and then tells you which dish should be replaced.

## Angle Two: Meaning of the Score — Counting Items vs. Locating Ability

Ordinary total scores are essentially "how many items you got right," which is a count.

IRT's θ is a **location** on a scale of ability. It doesn't ask "how many items you got right," but rather "where you are on this scale." So two people with the same total score can have different θ values (because the difficulty of the items they got right may differ).

## Angle Three: Cross-Test Comparability — Not Possible vs. Possible

Ordinary total scores are tied to the test. If you switch to a different test, the scores are not comparable. For example, Xiao Ming scored 85 on one test and 70 on another — you can't say he has regressed.

IRT relies on "person-item separation + anchor items" to connect different tests onto the same scale (equating). Thus, scores from different years or different test sets can be fairly compared. **This is the fundamental reason why all continuous tracking large-scale surveys rely on IRT.**

## Angle Four: Precision — One Number vs. A Curve

Ordinary scoring gives you one reliability coefficient, pretending to be accurate for everyone.

IRT gives you an "information curve," which clearly states where it is accurate and where it is not. When making critical judgments near the passing line, it can tell you whether that line is reliable.

**Analogy**: Ordinary scoring is like a weather forecast that only says "today is good"; IRT is like an hourly forecast, which is accurate in the morning but uncertain in the evening, and it tells you both.

## Angle Five: Item Management — Can't Manage vs. Fine-Grained Management

Under ordinary scoring, an item's "difficulty" is just the pass rate, which changes with the population, and it's hard to turn into a stable asset.

IRT gives each item an "ID card" (stable difficulty, discrimination, fairness, etc.), allowing items to be managed in an "item bank" for long-term use, repeated use, and cross-project reuse. Computerized adaptive testing (giving harder items if you get the current one right, and easier ones if you get it wrong, which is discussed in post 28) only works within the IRT framework.

## Angle Six: Cost — Almost Zero vs. High Investment

This point must be honestly addressed, otherwise you might think IRT is a pure profit-making business.

Ordinary scoring: low threshold, just a few people and Excel can handle it, and everyone can understand it.

IRT: you need to test model assumptions (whether this set of items measures the same construct, whether items are colluding), sufficient sample size (starting from hundreds to thousands), and more complex calculations and interpretation. θ also needs to be translated into human language for clients to understand. **The cost is higher by an order of magnitude — this is why ordinary scoring hasn't been replaced yet.**

| Comparison Item | Ordinary Scoring (CTT) | IRT |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Focus | Entire test | Each item |
| What is the score | Count of correct items | Locate you on an ability scale |
| Can scores be compared across tests | No | Yes (equating) |
| How to express precision | One reliability coefficient | One information curve |
| Can items be accumulated | Difficulty varies with population | Stable parameters, can build an item bank |
| Threshold and cost | Very low | Higher (sample, assumptions, computing power, interpretation) |
| Suitable scenarios | Classroom tests, small samples, one-time use | Large-scale, cross-year and cross-test, item bank, fairness |

## Don't Misunderstand: It's Not One Replacing the Other

After reading the table, don't rush to "discard scoring and embrace IRT." In mature projects, the two are **complementary**: first use ordinary indicators to quickly assess items (cheap and intuitive), and then pass the items to IRT for fine-tuning (accurate and in-depth).

In one sentence — **Ordinary scoring is like a stethoscope, IRT is like an MRI. Hospitals keep both, using them according to the condition.**

## One-Sentence Conclusion

> **Ordinary scoring counts items, IRT locates ability; ordinary scoring sticks to the test, IRT can compare across tests; ordinary scoring pretends to be accurate, IRT clearly states where it is accurate and where it is not. The cost is that IRT is more expensive and more demanding.**
