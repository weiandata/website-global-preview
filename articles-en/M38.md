# 38 · Missing Values, Blank Papers, and Careless Responses: How to Handle Dirty Data

> The previous article discussed the pitfalls of dirty data, and this one addresses how to handle them directly. In reality, data is never clean, and how you handle dirty data directly determines the trustworthiness of your analysis. This is the least glamorous, yet most skillful part of the process.

## Establish a Fundamental Rule

**Garbage In, Garbage Out (GIGO).** Even the most advanced IRT model will output gold-plated garbage if fed with dirty data — and because it looks professional, it can easily mislead people.

Therefore, data cleaning is not just a preliminary step that can be skipped or done or not, but a **mandatory checkpoint** to ensure the reliability of your conclusions. Below, I'll discuss three types of dirty data.

## One: Missing Values: What to Do with Unanswered Items

**First, distinguish why the data is missing**, as the handling methods differ significantly:

- **Not done / Not enough time** (e.g., the test is too long, and the later questions are left blank): This type of missingness is often related to ability (weaker individuals take longer and leave more questions blank).
- **Intentionally skipped / Refused to answer** (e.g., sensitive questions): This may be related to the respondent's characteristics.
- **Random technical missingness** (e.g., printing errors, system failures): This is unrelated to ability or characteristics and is the "cleanest" type.

**Core Principle: Never fill missing values with 0!** (This was emphasized in Article 37). In right/wrong scoring, "not answered" ≠ "answered wrong." Filling missing values with 0 is equivalent to arbitrarily deducting points, systematically lowering ability estimates.

**Common Correct Handling** (understand this, but let professionals handle the analysis):

- **Treat missing values as "not answered" and mark them specially**, so that IRT can skip them during estimation rather than treating them as errors.
- **More sophisticated methods**, such as **multiple imputation**, use other information to reasonably "guess" the missing values (see Article A15 in the current content library for more details).
- **Directly remove test papers with excessive missing values** (see the section on "blank papers" below).

**In One Sentence:** Missing values should be "treated according to the person and the reason," and the only thing you must never do is simply and crudely fill them with 0.

## Two: Blank Papers: How to Discard Entire Papers

Some test papers are so poor in quality that keeping them can be harmful. Common signals of "blank papers" include:

- **High missing rate**: A test paper with more than half of the questions unanswered, leaving insufficient information for reliable estimation, and forcing the inclusion of such papers would contaminate item parameters.
- **Abnormal response time**: Completing hundreds of questions in just a few seconds, which clearly indicates careless responding (if response time is recorded).
- **Patterned random responses**: Entire columns selecting the same option ("one column standing tall") or regular patterns like ABCDABCD ("Christmas tree").

For such test papers, the usual approach is to **set a clear criterion for removal and discard the entire paper**, and **record how many were removed and why** (this is important, and it should be reported, not secretly deleted).

## Three: Careless Responses: Hidden in "Normal" Papers

More subtle than blank papers are those test papers that appear completed but were not answered seriously. They do not boldly fill all items with the same option, but their response patterns are questionable. How to detect them?

- **Person-fit statistics** (Article 22): A person who gets easy items wrong and difficult items right by chance, or whose pattern of right and wrong answers is completely random, will have a poor person-fit — this is a signal that "this person's responses do not align with any reasonable ability level."
- **Response consistency checks**: Contradictory responses to reverse-scored items (e.g., both "strongly agree I am happy" and "strongly agree I am unhappy").
- **Combined judgment of time and pattern**: Responses that are fast and patterned are the most suspicious.

When detecting careless responses, the handling is similar to blank papers: **remove those that are valid and leave a trace of the reason**. (Articles A16 and Q10 in the current content library focus specifically on detecting careless responses.)

## A Key Mindset: Cleaning Should Be "Transparent, Traced, and Reproducible"

Data cleaning is most dangerous when it involves "secretly deleting data until the results look good" — that is not cleaning, but **data manipulation**, which is a red line in both academic and professional contexts.

The correct approach is:

1. **Set standards first, then proceed with cleaning**: Cleaning rules (how to handle missing values, which test papers to remove) should be established before looking at the results to avoid selective deletion based on the outcome.
2. **Keep a full record**: Document what was deleted, how many were deleted, and why — ideally in code (see Article 33 for reproducibility).
3. **Report honestly**: Report the original number of test papers, the number removed, and the number remaining for analysis. **Only by openly sharing the cleaning process can your analysis be trusted.**

## Don't Forget: Cleaning Should Not Be "Overdone"

Overcorrecting is equally dangerous. Removing all individuals whose responses do not match expectations may also eliminate genuine low-ability individuals or valuable outliers, making the sample appear artificially "clean and perfect."

**The goal of cleaning is to remove "obviously invalid" data, not to shape the data into your desired form.** Navigating this balance is the dividing line between professionals and amateurs.

## Final Summary in One Sentence

> **Three types of dirty data: missing values (treat according to the reason, never fill with 0), blank papers (remove entire papers if they meet the criteria, and leave a trace), and careless responses (detect using person-fit statistics, and remove with a trace). Cleaning should be transparent, set standards first, keep full records, and report honestly — secretly deleting data to make results look good is data manipulation. But also avoid over-cleaning, as it may mistakenly remove valid data. This step is not glamorous, but it reveals the most skill.**
