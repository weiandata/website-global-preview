# 09 · What Does It Mean When the Curve Shifts Left or Right?

> In the previous article, we got to know the ICC curve, which is an S-shaped curve. This article will focus on one action: shifting the entire curve to the left or right, and what changes occur in the item.

## Remember This One Sentence First

**ICC shifting to the right = the item has become harder; shifting to the left = the item has become easier.**

That's the only rule. The rest is about helping you remember and apply it correctly.

## Why "Right Shift = Harder"

Recall the horizontal axis: the left side represents low ability (θ = -3), and the right side represents high ability (θ = +3).

Also recall the key point: the position where the ICC crosses the horizontal line for "50% correct" is the **difficulty parameter b**.

Now, if we shift the entire curve to the right, the "50% correct" point also moves to the right, landing on a higher θ. What does this mean? **People with higher ability are now needed to reach the "50% correct" threshold.** In other words, this item has become more selective — it has become harder.

Conversely, if we shift the curve to the left, the "50% correct" point falls on a lower θ. People with lower ability can now reach the "50% correct" threshold, meaning the item has become easier.

**Imagery**: Imagine the curve as a threshold. A threshold shifting to the right = it's raised, requiring more ability to pass; a threshold shifting to the left = it's lowered, allowing ordinary people to pass.

## Three Items Line Up, Easy to Understand

Assume three items have the same curve shape (same steepness), but different positions:

- **The leftmost curve** (difficulty b = -1.5): People with low ability can already answer correctly — it's an **easy item**.
- **The middle curve** (difficulty b = 0): People with average ability have a 50% chance of being correct — it's a **medium-difficulty item**.
- **The rightmost curve** (difficulty b = +1.5): Only people with high ability can answer correctly — it's a **difficult item**.

For the same person (e.g., with ability θ = 0), the probability of answering correctly varies across the three items: high on the leftmost curve, exactly 50% on the middle curve, and low on the rightmost curve. **The same person, the same ability level, encountering items of different difficulty, leads to completely different outcomes** — this is exactly what the ICC is trying to show you.

## What Is This Useful For in Real Work

**Use Case 1: Determine if the item difficulty is appropriate.**
If you create a questionnaire and plot all the ICCs, and they all cluster on the left (very easy), what does that mean? This indicates that the test has little discrimination power for high-ability individuals — everyone scores perfectly, and you can't differentiate between them. In this case, you should add a few more difficult items to the right.

Conversely, if all items cluster on the right (very hard), low-ability individuals collectively score zero, and you can't differentiate between them either. In this case, you should add some easy items to the left.

**Ideal State**: The difficulty of the items (the position of the curve) should be like a ladder, evenly spread from left to right, covering the entire range of ability you are interested in. This way, regardless of the test-taker's ability level, there are always items of appropriate difficulty available.

**Use Case 2: Detect "Drifting" Items.**
If the same item, in different years or with different populations, shows an inexplicable shift in the ICC position (professionally known as "parameter drift"), you should be cautious — it might be due to item leakage, being guessed, or other reasons that make it unusually easy or hard for a particular group. Such items are potential issues in cross-year comparisons and should be identified and addressed.

## A Common Confusion: Is Difficulty "Better to the Left"?

No. **Difficulty has no good or bad; it only has appropriateness.**

A test needs a mix of item difficulties: it should have easy items to cover the low-ability range and difficult items to differentiate high-ability individuals. Having only difficult or only easy items is not ideal. So, if you see an item's ICC is on the left, don't rush to say "this item is bad." Instead, ask, "Does this test need an item at this difficulty level?"

(Discrimination is the one with clear good or bad — that's about the steepness of the curve, which we'll discuss in the next article.)

## One Final Sentence

> **The left-right movement of the ICC only concerns one thing: difficulty. A right shift means the item has become harder, and a left shift means it has become easier. A good test should have items with difficulty spread like a ladder from left to right, covering the entire population you want to differentiate.**
