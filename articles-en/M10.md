# 10 · The "Steepness" and "Flatness" of Curves: How Discrimination Looks on a Graph

> The previous article explained how curves shift left and right (difficulty). This one explores the other dimension — the steepness or flatness of the curve, which corresponds to discrimination, or a question's ability to "tell apart" people.

## Remember This One Sentence First

**The middle part of the ICC is steeper = higher discrimination (the question is good at distinguishing people); the middle part is flatter = lower discrimination (the question is like just going through the motions).**

## First, Look at Two Extreme Curves

Imagine two questions with the same difficulty (the point of 50% correct is at θ = 0), but with different shapes:

**The steep one**: Like a cliff. From -0.5 to +0.5, a very small range of ability, the probability of getting the question right jumps from 20% to 80%. That means a slight increase in ability leads to a huge difference in the probability of getting the question right.

**The flat one**: Like a gentle slope. From -2 to +2, a large range of ability, the probability of getting the question right slowly rises from 40% to 60%. Even with a big difference in ability, the probability of getting the question right is almost the same.

Now ask yourself: Which question is better at "showing who is stronger and who is weaker"?

Obviously, it's the steep one. On it, small differences in ability are amplified into large differences in the probability of getting the question right — it's like a high-magnification microscope that immediately separates people by ability. What about the flat one? The probability of getting the question right is almost the same for experts and novices, so it has almost no say in "who is stronger and who is weaker."

**Steep = high discrimination = strong ability to distinguish; flat = low discrimination = almost no ability to distinguish.**

## Why Steep Questions Are So Valuable

The core task of a test is to separate people by ability. And this "ability to distinguish," almost entirely depends on questions with high discrimination (steep).

Let's take an analogy. Suppose you want to sort a pile of fruits by sweetness:

- **A question with high discrimination** is like a precise sugar meter: it detects even slight differences in sweetness and helps you sort them accurately.
- **A question with low discrimination** is like someone with a dull sense of taste, who is also suffering from a cold and a stuffy nose: no matter how sweet or sour the fruit is, he just says "it's okay," and you can't sort them at all.

Low discrimination questions are not just "useless" — they are harmful. They take up space in the test, waste examinees' time and patience, but provide almost no useful information. Therefore, in question selection, **identifying and eliminating questions with low discrimination is the most cost-effective step** (see Chapters 40 and 41 for a step-by-step guide).

## What Happens When a Curve Is Extremely Steep or Extremely Flat

**Extremely steep**: The curve is almost like a straight vertical step — ability below a certain threshold is almost always wrong, and above it is almost always correct, with little transition in between. This is a nearly perfect "watershed" question, with extremely strong discrimination. In reality, such an ideal question is rare, but it is the direction we strive for.

**Extremely flat**: The curve is almost like a horizontal line — regardless of ability level, the probability of getting the question right is almost the same. This question has completely "given up," contributing nothing to distinguishing who is stronger and who is weaker. If the probability is also around 50%, it's basically like asking everyone to flip a coin — it should be eliminated.

**There is also the worst-case scenario**: the curve decreases as ability increases — the higher the ability, the lower the probability of getting the question right (negative discrimination!). This is usually due to a question error, reversed answer keys, or improper handling of reverse-scored items — it's a red flag (Chapter 46 will address this in detail).

## Steepness and Difficulty Are Two Different Things — Don't Confuse Them

This is the most common confusion for beginners, so be sure to distinguish:

- **Left-right position** → difficulty (how hard the question is, Chapter 9)
- **Steepness in the middle** → discrimination (how good the question is at distinguishing people, this chapter)

A question can be "both difficult and steep" (a good difficult question), "both difficult and flat" (a difficult but useless question), "both easy and steep" (a good easy question), or "both easy and flat" (an easy but useless question). **Difficulty and discrimination are two independent characteristics of a question — one determines the position, the other determines the steepness, and they do not interfere with each other.**

A useful mnemonic: **"Position determines difficulty, steepness determines sensitivity."**

## Summary for Practical Graph Reading

When you get a graph filled with ICC curves, you can now quickly scan it like this:

1. First, check whether the curve **spreads evenly from left to right** — this checks whether the difficulty coverage is sufficient (Chapter 9).
2. Then, look at how **steep each curve is** — identify the flat, flat, or even inverted curves, which are the questions that need to be addressed.
3. Ideally, you have a set of **steep curves with well-distributed positions** — they can clearly distinguish people and cover all ability levels.

## One-Sentence Conclusion

> **The steepness or flatness of a curve reflects discrimination: steep = a good question with strong ability to distinguish, flat = a useless question that's almost like just going through the motions, and inverted = a question that has an error and is a red flag. It is a separate concept from "left-right position = difficulty," so don't confuse them.**
