# 07 · Why Does Our Company Use IRT? —— A Business Perspective Answer

> The first six articles explain "What is IRT?" This article takes a different angle: as a data service company, why do we consider IRT as our core competence? Once you understand this article, you'll be able to explain to your clients "Why do we have the edge?"

## First, the conclusion: IRT is our "Professional Moat"

There are many people who can design questionnaires and run statistics. However, there are not many teams that can reliably handle large-scale, cross-year, cross-test, item bank construction, and ensure fairness in assessments. The technical foundation for such projects is precisely IRT.

In other words: IRT is not a gimmick we show off, but rather a **necessary component for many high-end assessment needs that cannot be avoided**. Whoever can solidly implement it stands on the side of supply scarcity.

## Scenario 1: Longitudinal Large-Scale Surveys

National-level large-scale surveys — such as monitoring scientific literacy, student scientific literacy, or digital literacy — often require continuous implementation over many years to assess whether the public or students' levels are improving year by year.

What's the challenge? **The questions must be changed every year** (old questions may leak or become outdated), yet the conclusions need to be compared across years. As previously explained, the ordinary total score is tied to the test paper, making it incomparable when the paper changes. To make the statement "this year is better than last year" technically valid, we must rely on IRT equating — using anchor items to align different years' tests on the same scale.

Such projects are looking for people who can make the yearly trend trustworthy. This is precisely where IRT excels.

## Scenario 2: Building and Operating an Item Bank

Many organizations want to build their own "item bank," using questions repeatedly, assembling tests as needed, and even implementing adaptive assessments (giving harder questions if correct, easier ones if incorrect).

The prerequisite for an item bank is that each item has stable "item parameters" (difficulty, discrimination, fairness) that are comparable across batches and populations. This set of capabilities is **only provided by IRT**. With ordinary scoring, the difficulty of an item changes with the population, making it impossible to build a sustainable asset.

What we can do for our clients is to transform a collection of scattered items into a structured, reusable, and maintainable item bank.

## Scenario 3: High-Stakes Judgments That Must Stand Up to Scrutiny

Some assessments require making "conclusions" — whether someone meets a standard, passes or fails, or is categorized into a level. These high-stakes judgments must be supported by evidence if questioned.

IRT has two strong tools here: first, the **information function**, which can prove that "we measure accurately around the passing score"; second, **DIF testing** (discussed in Article 23), which can prove that "this question is fair across different groups such as urban-rural and gender, without biasing any particular group." These are pieces of evidence that ordinary scoring cannot provide and that can be written into technical reports and withstand expert scrutiny.

## Scenario 4: "Data Never Leaves the Premises" Can Still Do Professional Analysis

This is a very practical business strength for us. Many survey data sets are highly sensitive and cannot be shared externally. However, clients often lack the professional analytical capabilities such as IRT.

Our approach is to **isolate the tools and data**: we deliver polished, trustworthy analysis tools and methods (such as our self-developed open-source IRT estimation engine **IRTC**, which is publicly available on GitHub), allowing analysis to be conducted in the client's own environment. This model works only if we truly have strong IRT engineering capabilities — which brings us back to IRT as the core.

## Scenario 5: Translating Complex Results into Plain Language

Just being able to calculate is not enough. Clients want conclusions they can understand and use for decision-making. Our value also lies in translating professional outputs such as θ, information functions, and DIF into plain language — for example, "your child's scientific literacy is roughly at what level among peers," "this questionnaire is most accurate for which population segment," or "which questions should be removed" (Articles 42 and 45 specifically discuss this).

**Many people understand technology, but few can explain it to those who don't.** This is also part of our moat.

## Will We "Use IRT Just Because We Can"?

No, we must be honest about this. IRT has costs and barriers, and not all projects are worth implementing (Article 31 specifically discusses when not to use it). Small-scale one-time surveys or internal assessments for a few dozen people can be efficiently handled with ordinary methods.

True professionalism is precisely reflected in **using it when appropriate and confidently saying "this isn't necessary" when it's not**. Saving clients money and choosing the right tools for the solution is itself a source of trust.

## Final Summary

> **IRT is the foundational requirement for many high-end assessment needs: cross-year comparability, item bank construction, fairness verification, and precision traceability. By mastering IRT, we stand on the side of supply scarcity — this is why our company chooses it as our core competence.**
