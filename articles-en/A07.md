# A07 · Reliability and Validity: From "Two Coefficients" to an Evidence System

> A deeper version accompanying Q04. Core long article for questionnaire and scale business line.

## First, debunk a popular simplification

Most textbook-style operations are: calculate a Cronbach's α > 0.7, perform a factor analysis with loadings > 0.4, and declare "good reliability and validity," then move on.

This simplification is so popular that people have forgotten it's a simplification. The modern measurement field (represented by *Standards for Educational and Psychological Testing*) holds that: **validity is not an attribute of the questionnaire, but an attribute of "score interpretation and use"; it is not an indicator, but an argument (validity argument).** You are not "testing validity," but collecting evidence for the claim that "using this score to make this judgment is reasonable."

This shift in perspective is not academic jargon—it directly changes what you do and what you report.

## Reliability: Stability of Scores

Reliability answers: Will the score remain stable if the same person is tested again, with half the questions, or with a different scorer? It corresponds to three types of coefficients:

**1. Internal Consistency** (most commonly reported):

- **Three usage norms for α**: Calculate by dimension (a multidimensional scale's overall calculation may overestimate or distort results); report confidence intervals rather than just point estimates; remember that α is a function of "item homogeneity + number of items" — a sufficiently large set of heterogeneous items can also yield α > 0.8, **a high α does not prove unidimensionality**;
- **ω series** (McDonald's omega): Based on factor models, with more relaxed assumptions, `psych::omega()` provides it directly, and it is becoming the new norm for reviewers. ω hierarchical can also answer "how much variance in the total score is attributable to the general factor" — whether to report the total score for a multidimensional scale depends on it.

**2. Test-Retest Reliability**: The correlation between two administrations separated by 2–4 weeks. It measures "stability across time," and is only meaningful for theoretically stable constructs (personality, ability), not for state-related constructs (mood), where low retest reliability is a characteristic of the construct, not a flaw in the scale.

**3. Inter-Rater Reliability**: For subjective scoring scenarios (essays, open-ended questions), use ICC or weighted Kappa; in large-scale assessments, inter-rater consistency is a mandatory reporting metric, and an arbitration mechanism must be designed.

**Standard for the use of reliability**: For group comparisons in research, ≥0.7 is acceptable; for high-stakes decisions about individuals (selection, diagnosis), ≥0.9 is required — because individual decision errors cannot be "averaged out."

## Validity: Five Types of Evidence

According to the *Standards*, validity evidence is divided into five sources, each with its practical implementation:

**1. Evidence Based on Content**: Does the item cover the entire construct? Operation: Construct blueprint → experts evaluate each item for relevance and representativeness (quantified as CVI) → cognitive interviews confirm that the target population understands the items without ambiguity. This is work done during the item development phase, **which cannot be supplemented after data collection**.

**2. Evidence Based on Internal Structure**: Does the data's dimensional structure align with theory? Operation: EFA (exploratory phase) → CFA (validation phase, independent sample) → report fit indices and loadings. Advanced: **Measurement Invariance Testing** — before comparing means across groups (gender, urban/rural, age), you must first prove that the scale measures the same construct and uses the same scale across groups (configural → metric → scalar, step-by-step testing), otherwise group differences may just be a false appearance of measurement bias. In the context of IRT, this corresponds to DIF testing, with the same logic.

**3. Evidence Based on Relationships with Other Variables**: Convergent validity (theoretical constructs are indeed related), discriminant validity (non-theoretical constructs are indeed differentiated, AVE square root > inter-dimensional correlation), and criterion-related validity (correlation with behavioral outcomes or existing gold standards, divided into concurrent validity and predictive validity).

**4. Evidence Based on Response Process** (most overlooked): Are respondents actually following the psychological process you intended? Cognitive interviews, response time analysis, and eye-tracking all count. If most people guess at a logic reasoning question, the score interpretation collapses.

**5. Evidence Based on Consequences**: Do the actual consequences of using the scores align with expectations? High-stakes assessments (admissions, certification) especially need to examine: whether differences in pass rates across groups are justifiable.

## Fit the System into Your Workflow

Depending on the seriousness of the project, take different subsets of this system:

| Scenario | Minimum Configuration |
| --- | --- |
| Course assignment / Preliminary research | α by dimension + EFA with clear structure |
| Thesis / Journal paper | ω/α + EFA → CFA across samples + convergent/discriminant validity + content validity process description |
| Formal assessment tool | All of the above + measurement invariance/DIF + criterion research + retest + technical manual |
| Large-scale monitoring | Add: inter-rater reliability system, equating across years, and validity evidence for standard setting |

## Three Practical Judgments

1. **Reliability is the ceiling, not the pass for validity**: Low reliability limits all correlations (attenuation formula), but a highly reliable scale can consistently measure the wrong thing — "our α is as high as 0.93" does not answer any validity question;
2. **Fit indices are not a pass-fail game**: CFI = 0.91 does not automatically qualify, and CFI = 0.89 does not automatically fail. Look at the source of the correction, the residual matrix, and whether each modification has a theoretical story — this is much more honest than the chi-square threshold;
3. **Validity argument is ongoing**: When the scale is used with a different population (adult scale used on adolescents), different language, or different administration method (paper-and-pencil to online), all existing evidence must be re-examined. "This is a mature scale" is not a pass mark.

Raising reliability and validity from a "passing ritual" to an "evidence system" will immediately widen the gap between your methods section and 80% of other submissions — reviewers can tell who is going through the motions and who is making an argument.
