# 41 · How to Identify and Remove Bad Items from Results

> The previous article provided a framework for judgment, and this one offers an actionable "Bad Item Checklist" along with the most important rule: deleting items cannot be based solely on numbers.

## First, the Most Important Rule

**Deleting items is not about "removing the one with the ugliest numbers," but a two-step process: "statistical suspicion detection + manual judgment for confirmation."**

Relying solely on machine-based deletion can mistakenly remove good items and leave hidden issues; relying solely on human judgment may fail to detect subtle bad items. **Statistics is responsible for "nominating suspects," while content experts are responsible for "conviction or release."** Remember this rule, and the checklist will not be misused.

## Bad Item Checklist (Six Common Suspects)

After obtaining results, go through each item one by one. If any of the following apply, the item is listed as a "suspect" for further review:

**Suspect 1: Low Discrimination (a is very small).**
Items that almost fail to differentiate between individuals (refer to posts 10 and 40). These items take up space but do little, and are the most common candidates for deletion.

**Suspect 2: Negative Discrimination (a < 0).**
A red flag (refer to post 46). The higher the ability, the less likely the item is to be answered correctly, which violates common sense and is almost certainly due to an error in the item—such as an incorrect answer key, a reverse-scored item that was not reversed, or a seriously ambiguous question. **This type must be checked; it is either deleted or revised.**

**Suspect 3: Poor Fit.**
The item's actual performance does not align with the model's predicted curve (refer to post 22). This often indicates that it is measuring something other than the intended construct (violating unidimensionality), is colluding with other items (violating local independence), or has inherent flaws.

**Suspect 4: Extremely Easy or Difficult and Unhelpful.**
The b value is either extremely high (almost no one gets it right) or extremely low (almost everyone gets it right), and this difficulty range is not needed for the test—its information contribution is nearly zero.

**Suspect 5: Abnormal Guessing Parameter (c is too high).**
A suspiciously high probability of guessing the correct answer (refer to post 11), which may indicate poor distractors or flaws in the item.

**Suspect 6: Large Standard Error.**
The parameter estimates are extremely unstable (refer to posts 21 and 30), indicating that the item is not well calibrated, and its "identity" is not clearly defined.

## Before Conviction, Content Experts Must Review These Points

Pass the suspect list to content experts (or, if you are reviewing it yourself, put on the "content review" hat), and ask the following questions for each item:

1. **Is there an error in the item itself?** Incorrect answer key, printing errors, ambiguous wording, or multiple correct answers? — These are "false positives," and the item is good but was recorded incorrectly. **Correcting the error often revives the item, so it should not be deleted.**
2. **Was a reverse-scored item not processed?** Especially for items with negative discrimination (refer to posts 37 and 46)—reversing the item and rerunning the analysis often resolves the issue.
3. **Does it have substantive fairness issues (DIF)?** Is it biased toward a particular group (refer to post 23)? If the DIF has a substantive cause, the item should be deleted or revised.
4. **Is it "well-placed but lacks discrimination"?** If the difficulty is exactly in the critical range, it may be worth the effort to revise it (change options, rephrase) rather than deleting it outright.
5. **Will there be other items to cover the same difficulty range if this one is deleted?** Avoid deleting all items in a particular difficulty range, which can lead to "information voids" in the test (refer to posts 13 and 15).

## An Often Overlooked Trade-off: The Ripple Effect of Deletion

Deleting an item is not an isolated action—it can have widespread effects:

- **After deletion, re-estimate the parameters** (refer to the iterative process in post 36): the item set has changed, and the parameters of the remaining items and the ability estimates of the individuals will be slightly adjusted.
- **Deleting too many can damage coverage**: deleting a batch of items at once may leave a difficulty range empty, reduce overall information, and make the test too short to be reliable.
- **Deletion should be documented**: record which items were deleted and why, and explain it in the report (just like data cleaning, refer to post 38). **Deleting items secretly to make results look better is a red line.**

Therefore, deletion should follow the principle of "**small steps and fast progress, deleting and reviewing continuously**": delete the most uncontroversial bad items, rerun the analysis, and review again, rather than making a large-scale deletion all at once.

## A Pragmatic Mindset: Bad Items Are Resources, Not a Shame

Do not feel discouraged when identifying bad items. Every item that is recognized as bad is:

- A **purification of the item bank** (removing defective items to make the bank cleaner);
- A **learning experience in item development** (understanding why this type of item is bad to avoid repeating the mistake);
- A **value-added contribution to the client** ("We have identified 5 problematic items for you," which is a real contribution).

**The ability to systematically identify and handle bad items is itself a demonstration of professional competence** (refer to posts 7 and 26). Everyone can create bad items, but few can identify them.

## Final Summary

> **Identify bad items through the two-step process of "statistical nomination + manual conviction": first list the items based on the six categories of suspicion (low or negative discrimination, poor fit, extremely useless difficulty, high guessing parameter, large standard error), then manually review whether the item was recorded incorrectly, whether it is a reverse-scored item, whether it has DIF, whether it can be revised, and confirm that deleting it does not damage the coverage of the difficulty range. Delete items in small steps, verify through reruns, and document the entire process. Bad items are resources, not a shame.**
