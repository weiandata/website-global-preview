# 18 · "Local Independence": Why Items Can't "Collude" in Cheating

> This is the second core assumption of IRT, and it is known as one of the "two major foundations" along with unidimensionality. The name sounds intimidating, but the concept is very relatable in everyday life.

## First, Translate the Name into Plain Language

"Local independence" (local independence) sounds like a legal clause. Its meaning is actually:

> **Given the same level (θ), whether a person answers a question correctly or not has no additional relationship with whether they answer another question correctly or not.**

Put even more simply: **The correctness of one question should only be determined by "your level" and "the question itself," and should not be influenced by "other questions."** Questions should be independent of each other and should not "collude."

Note that the premise is — "given the same level." For the same person, all questions are naturally influenced by their level (a person with a higher level tends to answer all questions correctly), which is normal and acceptable. What local independence aims to exclude is **any hidden connection between questions beyond the level.**

## What Does "Question Collusion" Mean?

Here are a few examples that are easy to understand:

**Example 1: Chain Questions.**
Question 8: "Based on the result from Question 7, continue to calculate..." If you got Question 7 wrong, you are almost certainly going to get Question 8 wrong — not because you don't know Question 8, but because you were dragged down by Question 7. The correctness of these two questions is **linked together**, which violates local independence.

**Example 2: A Series of Questions Based on the Same Passage.**
A reading comprehension passage is followed by five questions. If these five questions heavily depend on "whether you understood this passage," then those who understood the passage will answer all five correctly, while those who didn't will answer all five incorrectly. Their correctness is highly correlated, which is also a form of collusion (this is called the "item cluster effect," which is very common).

**Example 3: Questions That Leak Information to Each Other.**
The stem of Question 3 accidentally reveals the answer to Question 10. When you get to Question 10, a clever person can look back and get an easy point. The two questions are artificially linked.

**Commonality:** The relationship between the correctness of these questions goes beyond what "your level" can explain; they have an extra private connection.

## Why This Is a Problem

Because IRT assumes, when estimating parameters, that **each question is an independent piece of evidence**, and then aggregates these independent pieces of evidence to infer your level and the item parameters.

If a few questions are actually "colluding," it's like the same piece of evidence has been counted multiple times. The consequences are:

- **Overestimation of Information / Overestimation of Reliability:** The model assumes it has five independent pieces of evidence, but in reality, only one was copied five times. It becomes overly confident and reports an artificially high precision. This is the most common and subtle danger.
- **Bias in Parameter Estimation:** Colluding questions can distort the estimated difficulty and discrimination of each other.
- **Distorted Ability Estimation:** Bundled questions can disproportionately affect θ.

**In one sentence: When local independence is violated, the model will give you a false sense of accuracy.** This is more dangerous than an obvious error because it happens silently.

## How to Detect and Handle It

**Detection** (hand it over to professional analysis, just understand it): After running the model, look at the "residual correlations" — if two questions have an unusually correlated residual, it suggests they have an extra connection beyond the level, making them a suspect for collusion.

**Handling**, common approaches:

1. **Revise or Split the Questions:** Change chain-dependent questions into independent ones.
2. **Merge into a Single "Item Cluster" for Modeling:** Since questions based on the same passage naturally correlate, use a specialized "item cluster model" (testlet model) to treat them as a whole, rather than pretending they are independent.
3. **Remove Redundant Questions:** If two questions are almost asking the same thing (nearly repetitive), keep only one.

## Relationship with Unidimensionality

Unidimensionality (covered in the 17th article) refers to "all questions pointing to the same construct"; local independence refers to "questions shouldn't have any extra private connections beyond that common construct."

They are complementary: **If there is truly only one latent variable at play, and the questions are well-designed, then controlling for this latent variable should naturally make the questions independent.** So, violating local independence can sometimes be a signal that the dimension is not correctly identified (there may be a second latent variable secretly connecting some questions). These two assumptions are often checked together.

## One-Sentence Summary

> **Local independence = Given the same level, the correctness of questions should not have any extra connections. Chain questions, item clusters, and mutual information leakage all violate it, leading to the model's false sense of accuracy. Handling it involves revising questions, splitting them, modeling item clusters, or removing redundant ones. It and unidimensionality are the two major foundations of IRT, and they are often checked together.**
