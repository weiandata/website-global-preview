# A17 · Sample Size Planning and Statistical Power: Turning "How Many Questionnaires to Distribute" into an Arithmetic Problem

> A deeper version accompanying Q12. Research design line.

## Power: The Half That Is Often Overlooked

Statistical inference involves two types of errors: false positives (α, false alarm) and false negatives (β, missed detection). The academic community is meticulous about α (the 0.05 threshold), but β is often neglected. **Power = 1−β**, which is the probability that your study can detect a true effect when it exists.

Studies with insufficient power are threefold failures: true effects cannot be detected (a waste); detected "significant" effects are systematically overestimated (winner's curse — effect sizes that just barely cross the threshold under low power are necessarily inflated); and non-significant results cannot be interpreted (is there no effect, or was it not measured?). **The essence of sample size planning is to ensure sufficient power before starting the study.**

## Four-Element Equation

Power analysis involves four quantities: **effect size, α, power, and sample size** — solving for one when three are known. During planning, we solve for "sample size," and during post-hoc analysis, we solve for "power" (note: using observed effect size to calculate "post-hoc power" is circular reasoning; the correct approach is to use the **effect size set during planning** to evaluate the existing sample).

The challenge has always been in **honestly setting the effect size**, with three approaches:

1. **Meta-analysis value**: The aggregated effect size from meta-analyses in the same field. Note that publication bias can inflate this value, so consider using it at 80% of the reported value;
2. **Pilot data**: The estimated effect size from small-sample pilot studies is noisy and can only be used to estimate the order of magnitude, not as an exact value;
3. **Minimum Clinically Relevant Effect Size (SESOI)**: Reformulate the question — "What effect size is actually meaningful?" Planning based on this value ensures that when power is insufficient, you miss only "insignificant" effects. This is the most robust approach.

**Realistic Calibration for Social Sciences**: Large-scale replication studies in psychology show that the median effect size is around r ≈ 0.15–0.20 / d ≈ 0.35 — planning based on Cohen's "medium effect" (d = 0.5) results in most real studies having insufficient power. **It is better to plan based on a small effect.**

Tools: G*Power (graphical interface), R's `pwr` package; for complex models (SEM, multilevel, interaction effects), use **Monte Carlo simulation** — generate data based on expected parameters and repeatedly fit models, counting the proportion of significant results. Use packages like `simsem`/`simr`, or write your own loop (this is the most general and worth learning method).

**Special Reminder for Interaction Effects**: The required sample size for detecting an interaction is often more than four times that for the main effect. "Just checking the moderation effect" is a luxury in most sample sizes. During planning, you either pay for it or explicitly abandon it.

## Three Corrections for Survey Scenarios

Beyond the lab logic, survey sampling involves three additional considerations:

1. **Design Effect (deff)**: In cluster sampling, individuals within the same cluster are similar, reducing the effective information. For example, with a nominal n = 2000 and deff = 2, the effective sample size is only 1000. Planning formula: required n × deff;
2. **Response and Screening Rates**: If the expected response rate is 70% and screening rate is 8%, the distribution amount = target n ÷ (0.70 × 0.92);
3. **Minimum Reporting Unit Principle**: Sample size should be planned based on the "most detailed subgroup." A national sample of 3000 people is sufficient for national reporting, but if you need to report by 31 provinces, each province would have fewer than 100 people — the confidence interval for proportion estimates becomes too wide to be meaningful. Either expand the sample, reduce the reporting granularity, or use small domain estimation. **This decision must be made during the design phase.**

## Make It a Fixed Step in the Research Process

1. Include a section on "sample size justification" when writing the research plan: target analysis, set effect size and rationale, α/power, and calculation results (including tools and versions);
2. Set the effect size as small as possible; when there are multiple core hypotheses, plan based on the one that requires the largest sample;
3. When reporting the paper, clearly state the planning process — this is becoming a journal requirement (already standard in clinical fields, and rapidly catching up in psychology).

Sample size planning takes only ten minutes but can prevent the most expensive mistake — "realizing it was a waste after finishing." It is the highest value-to-time ratio step in research design.
