# 31 · When to Just Avoid IRT? —— A Honest List of Limitations

> The previous five articles discussed the strengths and prerequisites of IRT. This one flips the script, helping you use your expertise effectively: in which scenarios should you decisively put down IRT and use simpler methods? Only those who can say "this doesn't need IRT" are true experts.

## Why This Article Matters

Because a common pitfall is "having a hammer, seeing everything as a nail." Once you learn IRT, you might be tempted to use it everywhere, resulting in **high costs, high risks, and low value**.

True professionalism is **knowing when to act and when to stop**. Saving clients money and choosing the right tool for the project itself becomes a source of trust (as discussed in Article 7). Below are scenarios where simpler methods (usually traditional CTT, or even just calculating percentages) should be prioritized.

## Scenario 1: Sample Size is Too Small

Classroom tests with dozens of students, or small-scale internal questionnaires — the sample size is simply too small to feed IRT (as discussed in Article 30). Forcing IRT results in unstable parameters, large errors, and even failure to run.

**Use Instead**: Pass rates, item-total correlations, Cronbach's α. A sample of dozens is sufficient, and Excel can handle it.

## Scenario 2: One-Time Use, No Cross-Test Comparison

This test is only used once, and after scoring, you just produce a ranking or score and end it. No tracking, no test change, no item bank. In this case, IRT's biggest strength (cross-test comparability) is irrelevant, and you're just wasting cost.

**Use Instead**: Total scores, rankings, and basic statistics. Simple and direct.

## Scenario 3: Just Want a Quick Overview

In the early stages of a project, you just want to know "which items are clearly poor and what is the overall difficulty." This rough screening is fast and intuitive with traditional indicators.

**Use Instead**: Start with a quick CTT check, and remove clearly poor items first. Once data is sufficient and needs evolve, then move to IRT for fine-tuning (the "relay" approach discussed in Articles 2 and 5).

## Scenario 4: Items Seriously Violate Assumptions and Can't Be Fixed

If a set of items is definitely multidimensional and can't be split, or if there are complex dependencies that can't be changed, then IRT's foundation is already collapsed (as discussed in Article 29). Forcing IRT in such cases results in digital garbage disguised as professional results.

**Use Instead**: First, address the issues with the items (re-design them), or use a more suitable method (e.g., treat multidimensionality honestly). Don't use the wrong tool to "clean up" poor items.

## Scenario 5: The Client Wants "Simple and Understandable," Not "Technically Optimal"

Sometimes, the client just wants a percentage everyone can understand or an intuitive ranking for an internal decision that's not high-stakes. Presenting a θ, information function, or DIF report can actually increase communication costs and confuse people.

**Use Instead**: Give them the simple indicators they want. **Appropriateness > Advancedness.** If something can be explained in one sentence, don't use a whole model.

## Scenario 6: Data Quality is Too Poor

Lots of careless responses, severe missing data, and unclear sample sources — garbage in, garbage out (GIGO). No matter how advanced the IRT, it can't save bad data, and it might even give it a "looks professional" sheen, which is more misleading.

**First Do**: Data cleaning and quality control (as discussed in Article 38). Don't even consider modeling until the data is clean.

## The Other Side: When IRT is Almost Mandatory

To balance, here are the scenarios where IRT is almost unavoidable (as a complement to Articles 7 and 26):

- **Fair comparison across years and tests** (equating);
- **Building an item bank** for long-term operations;
- **Creating adaptive tests** (CAT);
- **High-stakes decisions**, needing to **prove accuracy near the cut score**;
- **Fairness checks** (DIF);
- **Large-scale literacy monitoring**, needing to accurately depict the full population distribution.

**The criterion is always "need," not "advancedness."** When needs align with these, IRT is not a choice; when they don't, simple methods are the optimal solution.

## A Decision Rule You Can Memorize

> **Small sample, one-time use, no cross-test, want simplicity, or data is dirty — put IRT aside.**  
> **Cross-year and cross-test, build an item bank, adaptive testing, prove accuracy, check fairness — please use IRT.**

## One Final Sentence

> **Use your expertise where it matters most. Small samples, one-time use, no cross-test, just rough screening or simple understanding, or dirty data — in these scenarios, decisively use simpler methods. The value of IRT lies in cross-test comparison, item banks, adaptive testing, accuracy proof, and fairness checks — these are the "must-have" areas. The criterion is need, not advancement. Only those who can say "this doesn't need IRT" are truly professional.**
