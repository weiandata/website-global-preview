# A24 · Calibration Weighting: Turning "Sample Like Population" into a Solvable Mathematical Problem

> A deep dive into survey statistics. General method explanation, not involving specific tools or products.

## Starting from the Nightmare of "Manual Weighting"

Many survey teams have experienced this scenario: after weighting, the gender matches, but age is off; pulling age back causes urban-rural discrepancies; the three dimensions are pulled back and forth, the weight table is revised to the eleventh version, and no one can say whether the current version is "good" or how far it is from "best."

The root of this nightmare is **treating an optimization problem as a manual task**. The contribution of calibration weighting (calibration weighting) is to formalize "making the sample structure match the population" into a mathematical problem with a clear objective, constraints, and a unique solution.

## The Mathematical Formulation of the Problem

Given: initial weights $ d_i $ (design weights × non-response adjustments), auxiliary variables $ x_i $ (gender, age group, urban-rural...), and population benchmarks $ T $ (total counts or proportions across dimensions from census data).

Find: new weights $ w_i $, satisfying:

1. **Calibration Constraint**: $ \sum w_i \cdot x_i = T $ (the weighted sample exactly matches the population benchmark);
2. **Minimum Distortion**: The "distance" between $ w_i $ and $ d_i $ is minimized (initial weights carry sampling design information, so minimal changes are preferred).

Different "distance functions" yield different members of the calibration family: linear distance corresponds to generalized regression estimation (GREG); exponential distance corresponds to raking (iterative proportional fitting); adding **bounds on $ w_i $** (to prevent extreme weights) gives bounded calibration. This unified framework was established by Deville & Särndal (1992), and it is now the standard tool for modern survey organizations.

## Why This Formalization Matters

**1. "Best" Now Has a Definition.** Manual weighting cannot answer "Can it be better?" In an optimization framework, the solution is "the set of weights that satisfies all constraints and is closest to the initial weights"—it exists, is unique (under convex problems), and is reproducible.

**2. Conflicts Become Diagnosable.** When constraints are too numerous or tight, the problem may be unsolvable—this is not a failure, but **information**: it precisely tells you which benchmarks are conflicting and where the sample is too thin to support the constraints. The response becomes systematic: relax some constraints to soft constraints (approximation rather than exact matching), merge sparse categories, or loosen weight bounds. The manual era's "back and forth" becomes here a single solution plus an infeasibility diagnosis.

**3. Quality Cost Is Measurable.** Calibration necessarily distorts the weight distribution, and the cost is monitored using effective sample size (ESS) = $ \frac{(\sum w)^2}{\sum w^2} $: the greater the drop in ESS, the higher the variance cost. **"Marginal full match but ESS cut in half" is not success—it's an alarm**—usually indicating that the constraint design exceeds the sample's capacity.

## Practical Workflow

1. **Select Auxiliary Variables**: Three standards—population benchmarks are reliable (census / authoritative statistics), measurement is accurate in the sample, and they are related to the core survey variables (correlation brings variance reduction benefits);
2. **Design Constraint Structure**: Full cross cells (gender × age × urban-rural) are often sparse, so typically only marginal constraints across dimensions (raking approach) or partial two-way cross are used;
3. **Set Weight Bounds**: For example, [0.3, 3] × initial weights, adjusted based on the trade-off between ESS and bias;
4. **Solve**: In R, use `calibrate()` from the `survey` package or `calib()` from the `sampling` package; for large datasets (hundreds of thousands of units, hundreds of constraints), construct the problem at the "population unit × value" aggregated level and solve it, reducing the scale by several orders of magnitude—this is a common engineering technique;
5. **Acceptance Checklist**: Residual table for all constraints, weight distribution diagnostics (extremes, CV, ESS), and pre- and post-calibration comparisons for key indicators.

## Two Advanced Topics

**Soft Constraints (Approximation Mode)**: Change "must exactly match" to "penalize deviation in the objective function," suitable for benchmarks with inherent error or when constraints are too numerous. Mathematically, this transforms from an equality-constrained optimization to a convex optimization with a penalty term (bounded quadratic programming), and the solution is equally mature. In practice, a mixed design of "exact matching for core dimensions + soft approximation for secondary dimensions" is very common.

**The Boundary of Calibration**: It corrects **structural bias in the auxiliary variable dimensions**. Calibration is powerless against selection bias unrelated to auxiliary variables (e.g., the sample of willing respondents is inherently different), which is the mathematical reason why "convenience samples cannot be whitewashed by weighting." Calibration is a fine-tuning for probability samples, not an exemption for non-probability samples.

## Conclusion

Translating business requirements ("provincial and gender-based pass rates must align with planning targets") into constraints and translating quality intuition ("don't change weights too much") into the objective function and bounds—**this "business → mathematics" translation ability is the most valuable skill in survey statistics**, and it is the core capability we repeatedly refine in large-scale survey data processing. The method itself is fully open in the literature. May this guide help you avoid the pitfalls of manual weighting.
