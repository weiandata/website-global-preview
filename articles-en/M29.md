# 29 · IRT Is Not All-Powerful: What Are Its Underlying Assumptions?

> Previously, we praised a lot. This article focuses on cooling things down — by laying out the few "foundations" that make IRT stand on solid ground. If the foundation is weak, the higher the building, the worse the collapse.

## Why We Need to Specifically Discuss Assumptions

Because IRT is a **statistical model**, and all models are "conditionally valid." It is not a machine that you press a button and it always gives you the correct answer. Rather, it is a set of "**methods that work only if reality matches my several assumptions**."

When reality does not match the assumptions, IRT will still solemnly spit out a bunch of parameters and draw a bunch of nice curves — but those are all **digital garbage** that can deceive you. Therefore, understanding assumptions is the **insurance against being deceived by your own analysis results**.

Below are the four most important foundations. The first three were discussed earlier, and here we summarize and deepen them; the last one is an addition.

## Foundation One: Unidimensionality — "These Questions Only Measure One Thing"

Classic IRT compresses a person into a θ, which is only reasonable when **all items point to the same latent variable** (Chapter 17).

- **What Happens If It Fails**: If the questions are actually measuring several things, forcing a single θ will make this θ a messy mix of things, leading to chaotic parameter estimation and unreliable subsequent judgments.
- **How to Maintain It**: Conduct a factor analysis to verify the dimensionality before modeling; if it can be split into unidimensional sub-scales, split them and measure separately.

## Foundation Two: Local Independence — "Questions Are Not Allowed to Collude"

Under the premise of equal ability levels, the correctness of questions should not be additionally correlated (Chapter 18). Linked questions, question groups, and mutual leakage will all break this assumption.

- **What Happens If It Fails**: The model treats "colluding questions" as independent evidence and counts them repeatedly, resulting in **inflated reliability and precision** — deceiving you with a false sense of security. This is the most hidden danger.
- **How to Maintain It**: Check residual correlations; revise questions, split questions, model question groups, or delete redundant items.

## Foundation Three: Choosing the Right Model Shape — "The S-Curve Must Match Reality"

IRT assumes that the probability of answering correctly follows a specific shape of S-curve as ability changes, and it also assumes that the model you choose (1PL/2PL/3PL/GRM...) matches your items (Chapters 6 and 22).

- **What Happens If It Fails**: For example, if multiple-choice questions have a clear guessing space but you use a model without a guessing parameter, or if the discrimination of the items is clearly uneven but you force the use of a 1PL model where everyone is the same — the fit will be poor, and the parameters will be biased.
- **How to Maintain It**: Conduct fit checks; choose the appropriate model based on item types and data, don't use one model for everything.

## Foundation Four: Sufficient and Representative Sample — "Data Must Support the Model"

Parameter estimation relies on data to "pin down" the parameters (Chapters 19 and 30). If the sample is too small or not representative, the estimates will be unstable and inaccurate.

- **What Happens If It Fails**: If the sample is insufficient, the parameters (especially the guessing parameter c in 3PL) will be unstable or even absurd, with large standard errors that make the results unusable; if the sample is not representative (e.g., only calibrated individuals at the middle level), the difficulty of items for the two extreme groups will not be accurately captured.
- **How to Maintain It**: Match the sample size to the model complexity (Rasch needs hundreds, 2PL/GPCM needs thousands, 3PL needs even more); ensure that the calibration sample covers the range of ability levels you care about.

## A "Foundation Checklist"

Before using IRT results, ask yourself the following questions one by one:

| Foundation | Self-Check Question | Signals of Problems |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Unidimensionality | Do these questions really measure only one thing? | Factor analysis reveals multiple strong factors |
| Local Independence | Are there linked questions, question groups, or leakage? | Abnormal residual correlations |
| Model Shape | Does the model match the item type and fit well? | Fit checks fail widely |
| Sample | Is the sample size sufficient and representative? | Large parameter standard errors, non-convergence |

If any of the four foundations is seriously not met, you should **stop and address the issue before interpreting the results**.

## An Important Mindset: Assumptions Are Not "Constraints," They Are "User Manuals"

Newcomers often see these assumptions as annoying constraints and want to avoid them. But from another perspective: **Assumptions are actually IRT honestly telling you "under what conditions it works and under what conditions you shouldn't trust it."**

A tool that clearly states its applicable boundaries is much more reliable than one that claims to "cure all diseases." The real danger is not "IRT with assumptions," but "IRT used without checking assumptions and immediately applying it."

**The dividing line between professionals and amateurs often lies in: amateurs only run models and get results, while professionals spend a lot of effort to confirm whether the foundations are stable before proceeding.**

## One Final Summary

> **The four foundations of IRT: unidimensionality (measuring only one thing), local independence (questions do not collude), choosing the right model shape (the curve matches reality), and a sufficient and representative sample. If any of these foundations collapse, the beautiful results are just digital garbage. Assumptions are not constraints, but the honest "user manual" of the tool — using results without checking the foundations is truly amateur.**
