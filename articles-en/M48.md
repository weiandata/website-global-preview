# 48 · When Results Clash with "Common Sense / Experience," Who Should We Trust?

> The third case in the emergency medicine section, and also the one that most tests your judgment. When analysis results don't align with your intuition or domain experience, should you trust the data or the common sense? This article provides you with an unbiased set of handling principles.

## First, Acknowledge: This Is a Real Issue, with No Lazy Answers

Both extreme attitudes are wrong:

- **"Data is supreme, and machine-calculated results are always correct"**: No. Machines only reflect the data they receive and the model you set up — if the data is dirty or the model is wrong, they will still output incorrect conclusions in a serious manner (this has been repeatedly discussed in posts 29 and 38).
- **"Experience is supreme, and if the results differ from what I expect, the model must be wrong"**: Also not. Experience has blind spots and biases, and many counterintuitive results are precisely what the data reveals as a truth you hadn't considered.

**The correct attitude is: treat the "clash" as a signal — it is reminding you that "this is worth digging into," rather than making you rush to take a side.** Conflict is not a problem; it is an entry point to discovering issues (or new knowledge).

## When Clashing, First Check the "Data / Model" Side

When results are abnormal, don't rush to question common sense or discard it — **first go back and check the technical side** — because the probability of technical errors is often higher than "common sense being overturned." Follow this order:

**1. Did the data go wrong?**
Data entry errors, coding errors, reverse-coded items not flipped (posts 37 and 46), unit errors, or treating missing values as 0 (post 38) — these are the most frequent "false abnormalities." A small coding error can completely change the results. **Check the data first — it's the most cost-effective approach.**

**2. Did the model go wrong?**
Wrong dimension selection, too simple a model, or violated assumptions (posts 29 and 47). "Abnormal results" calculated with the wrong model are the model's fault, not reality's.

**3. Who is the sample?**
What group of people does your sample represent? If the sample is biased (e.g., only recruited a specific type of person), then the results only reflect that group, and it's normal for them to differ from your common sense based on "the general population" — it's just that the common sense is applied to the wrong target, not the data being wrong.

**4. Is it an overinterpretation of small differences?**
Two numbers may look different, but when you calculate the standard error, the difference is lost in the error (post 21). The "abnormal difference" you think you see may not be statistically significant — it's just noise.

**After going through these four steps, many "clashes" will be found to be technical bugs, and after correction, the results will align with common sense.**

## If the Technical Side Is Clean, and the Results Still Clash

Congratulations, you may have encountered one of two more interesting situations:

**Situation A: The data reveals a real but counterintuitive phenomenon.**
Your "common sense" is actually a bias or outdated belief, and the data is correcting you. Many scientific advances in history have started with "counterintuitive but real" data. At this point, **you should humbly trust the data that has been rigorously tested**, and try to understand why it is counterintuitive.

**Situation B: Your "common sense" has captured something the model hasn't.**
Domain experts' intuition sometimes relies on information not included in the model (real-world context, historical background, or the uniqueness of the sample). At this point, common sense is a valuable supplement, indicating that the model may have missed an important factor, which is worth further modeling or investigation.

**How to distinguish between A and B?** Rely on further evidence: re-analyze from a different angle, cross-validate with other data sources, or consult more domain experts. **It's not about who has the loudest voice, but who can provide more evidence that stands up to scrutiny.**

## A Healthy Mindset: Data and Experience Are "Colleagues," Not "Opponents"

The most mature approach is to let data and experience **mutually validate** each other, rather than choosing between them:

- Use experience to **check the reasonableness of the data**: "Does this result align with domain knowledge? If not, first check whether the calculation is wrong."
- Use data to **illuminate the blind spots of experience**: "I always thought it was this way, but the data suggests it might not be — it's worth reconsidering."

**A good analyst neither blindly trusts data nor rigidly clings to experience, but lets the two dialogue and correct each other.** Data is a telescope, and experience is a map — the telescope sees far but may have distortions, the map has the big picture but may be outdated. Only by using both can you avoid getting lost.

## Putting It Into Action: A Checklist When Clashing

1. **Don't rush to draw conclusions or take sides.** Treat the conflict as a "signal for deeper exploration."
2. **First check the technical side**: data → model → sample → whether the small differences are overinterpreted.
3. **Once the technical side is clean**, determine whether it's "data correcting your bias" or "experience pointing out the model's omission," and distinguish them with more evidence.
4. **Regardless of the conclusion, truthfully record this conflict and your investigation process** — this is both professional documentation and valuable experience accumulation.

## One Final Word

> **When results clash with common sense, don't rush to take sides — treat it as a "signal for deeper exploration." First check the technical side (data errors, model errors, sample bias, overinterpretation of small differences — most false abnormalities are resolved here). Once the technical side is clean, it's either that the data is correcting your bias (trust the data), or that experience has captured the model's omission (you need to build more models), and distinguish them with more evidence. Data and experience are colleagues that correct each other, not opponents.**
