## Walk Through the Entire Process with a Fictional Project

Imagine a typical project (purely fictional teaching case): an organization wants to conduct a nationwide monitoring of a certain type of literacy among adolescents, with a sample size of tens of thousands, producing grade reports by province and urban-rural areas, and planning to track trends every two years. Below is a walkthrough of the entire journey by time axis — each station is marked with the corresponding detailed article in this series, and this article can serve as a map for the entire series.

**Station 0: Framework and Blueprint (12 Months Before Launch)**
Everything starts with the assessment framework: definition of literacy, dimension structure, and behavioral description (→ *Key Methodological Points for Literacy Assessments*). Only with a framework can there be item blueprint, test structure, and report structure. Simultaneously, sampling design is initiated: stratified multi-stage sampling, sample size calculation based on the smallest reporting unit (→ *Sample Size Planning* *Comprehensive Guide to Sampling Weights*).

**Station 1: Tool Development (First 9 Months)**
Item development, review, cognitive interviews, pilot testing, and psychometric screening (→ *Full Process of Scale Development* *Reliability and Validity*). Cognitive items undergo IRT calibration and DIF detection (→ *How to Choose a Polytomous Scoring Model* *DIF Detection*). Due to the large volume of content, a matrix-based test assembly is adopted — each participant only completes one booklet, with anchor items linking booklets (→ *Test Equating and Linking*). Simultaneously, the layout of anchor items for the next round is designed: **trend comparability is designed now, not calculated two years later**.

**Station 2: Data Collection and Monitoring (Month 0)**
Electronic data collection, with daily quality reports monitoring response structure and answer quality signals (→ *Survey Data Quality Control System*). Data processing plan is frozen and reviewed before data arrives (cleaning rules, exclusion criteria, weighting approach).

**Station 3: Data Cleaning (Month 1)**
Raw data is frozen for read-only access, with a six-step pipeline: structural validation → careless responding detection (duration + long sequences + logical contradictions, with two hits requiring revalidation) → missing data classification (skipped/missed separately coded) → coding derivation → external consistency → cleaning report (→ *Full Data Cleaning Process* *Careless Responding Detection* *Missing Data Handling*). Entire process is script-based + assertion-based (→ *Reproducible Research Workflow*).

**Station 4: Measurement Modeling (Month 2)**
IRT calibration: GPCM as the main model, joint estimation of item-level multidimensional structure (→ *What is IRT* *CTT vs. IRT* *Multidimensional IRT*). Computation for a million-sample size with a multidimensional model is handled using a streaming engine (→ *IRT Estimation for a Million-Sample Size*). After anchor item drift detection, cross-booklet equating is performed to place all booklets on the same scale (→ *Equating and Linking*). Background variables are incorporated into the model via latent regression, extracting plausible values (→ *Latent Regression* *EAP/MLE/WLE* — individual feedback uses WLE, group inference uses PV, with two distinct reporting standards clearly separated).

**Station 5: Weighting and Inference (Month 3)**
Design weights → non-response adjustment → calibration to population structure (→ *Comprehensive Guide to Sampling Weights* *Calibration Weighting*). Small sample units are merged according to predefined rules before producing grade proportions (→ *Merging Small Sample Units*). Variance estimation uses resampling + PV merging rules, with each published number carrying a complete uncertainty reporting standard.

**Station 6: Grading and Reporting (Month 4)**
Bookmark method for standard setting, drawing four grade lines, aligning grade descriptions with empirical performance (→ *Standard Setting*). All report numbers are generated by scripts, double-checked, and reported with clear reporting standards; sensitivity appendices proactively answer "Would the conclusions remain stable if we changed the cleaning or weighting rules?" Technical reports fully disclose the methodological chain — **the credibility of the published report equals the credibility of the weakest link in the entire chain**.

**Station 7: Archiving and the Next Round (Starting from Month 5)**
Data, code, environment, and documentation are packaged and frozen (can be replayed with one click three years later); anchor items are stored and managed confidentially; problematic items identified in this round are retired, and new items are added to the pretesting pipeline (→ *Item Bank Construction*). The trend report two years from now starts from the quality of today's archiving.

## What This Journey Illustrates

Three observations, which are also recurring themes in this series:

1. **Order Equals Quality**: Almost every accident at a station is rooted in "not doing the necessary work at the previous station" — analysis cannot save design, weighting cannot save sampling, and equating cannot save leaked anchor items;
2. **Chain Thinking**: While individual techniques (IRT, weighting, equating) have textbooks, what is truly scarce is the experience of assembling them into a **cohesive, interlocking pipeline** — the output specification of each station is the input prerequisite for the next;
3. **Honesty is the Technical Standard**: Each station has a "quick fix" option to cover up problems (data fabrication, silent downgrading, mixed reporting standards), as well as a "explicit reporting" professional option. Choosing the latter may be troublesome in the short term, but it is the foundation of long-term credibility.
