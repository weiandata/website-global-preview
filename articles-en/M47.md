# 47 · What to Do When the Model Doesn't Fit: Stay Calm and Follow This Checklist

> The second case in the emergency series, and also the one that most worries beginners. After running the model, you see "poor fit," and many people get nervous. This article gives you a calm checklist to turn panic into a structured diagnosis.

## First, Reassure Yourself: Poor Fit Isn't a "Failure"

In Article 22, we discussed that fit tests are like "mirrors that reveal the truth"—they are meant to show problems. **"Poor fit" doesn't mean you messed up; it's the model honestly telling you, "There's something here, check it out."**

The real danger lies in analyses that never perform fit tests and directly use the results—these aren't necessarily problem-free, but they hide the issues. So seeing "poor fit" means your process is responsible. Take a deep breath, and let's start checking things off the list.

## Checklist (Starting with the Most Common and Easiest to Check)

### First Check: Is It "Individual Items Not Fitting" or "Overall Poor Fit"

First, determine the scale of the problem, which leads to completely different approaches:

- **Only a few items show poor fit**: The issue is localized, and it's manageable. Treat these as "bad items" (Article 41)—review whether these items have issues, are reverse-scored, have incorrect answers, or are ambiguous. Fix or delete them, then rerun. **Most fit issues fall into this category.**
- **Widespread, systematic poor fit**: The issue lies in the "foundation." Continue checking (Second, Third, and Fourth Checks).

### Second Check: Did You Choose the Right Dimension (Unidimensional vs. Multidimensional)

The most common underlying cause of widespread poor fit is **forcing multidimensional data into a unidimensional model** (Articles 17 and 29).

You think this set of items measures the same construct, but in reality, they measure several different ones. The model tries to explain data that should be explained by multiple dimensions with a single axis, and it can't align.

**Solution**: Perform a factor analysis to confirm the dimensions. If it's truly multidimensional—split it into several unidimensional subscales and model them separately, or switch to a multidimensional model (Article 17). **Wrong dimension is the number one suspect for fit failure.**

### Third Check: Did You Use a Simple Model?

- If multiple-choice items have clear guessing space, but you used a model without a guessing parameter (1PL/2PL)—this model can't explain the phenomenon of low-level respondents guessing correctly, leading to poor fit. Try switching to a 3PL model (Articles 6 and 11).
- If the discrimination of items varies significantly, but you used a 1PL model that assumes all items have the same discrimination—those items with very high or very low discrimination will show poor fit. Try switching to a 2PL model.

**Solution**: Let the model's complexity match the true structure of the data. **But don't overdo it**—it's not better to be more complex. Complex models require more sample size (Article 30). Before switching models, confirm that the sample size is sufficient.

### Fourth Check: Are There Local Dependencies (Items Are Collaborating)

Linked items, item groups, or mutual leakage (Article 18) can destroy local independence and also manifest as fit issues (especially with abnormal residual correlations).

**Solution**: Check residual correlations to identify "collaborating" items; revise the items, split them, use a group-item model, or delete redundant items.

### Fifth Check: Data Quality / Sample

- **Dirty data with careless responses** (Article 38): Garbage data can make any model fit poorly. Go back and check if the data cleaning was thorough.
- **Too small a sample** (Article 30): When the sample is too small, fit statistics themselves are unstable, so don't overinterpret the results.

## A Key Judgment: Don't Be Scared by "Overly Sensitive" Tests

There's a subtle professional nuance here that you must understand:

**In large samples, fit tests become extremely sensitive—any minor deviation from the model will be flagged as "significant non-fit."** It's like an overly sensitive smoke alarm that goes off even when you're just baking bread.

So, when evaluating fit, don't only look at whether it's "significant" (p-value). Also consider the **actual size of the deviation** (effect size-like indicators, such as RMSEA) and whether it **has practical impact**. Sometimes, "statistically poor fit" with minimal deviation that doesn't affect conclusions is acceptable.

**Judging whether the fit is good enough is a combined judgment of statistics, experience, and purpose—not an automatic yes/no.** That's why this task requires professionals—machines provide numbers, but people provide judgment.

## An Emergency Flowchart

When the model doesn't fit, follow this order:

1. **Local or Global?** Local → Treat as a bad item (fix/delete/re-run), which usually solves the issue.
2. **Global** → Check dimensions (is it forcing multidimensional data into a unidimensional model? That's the biggest suspect).
3. → Check model selection (is it too simple? Should we add guessing or discrimination parameters?).
4. → Check for local dependencies (are items collaborating?).
5. → Check data quality and sample size.
6. Throughout the process, **don't be scared by overly sensitive tests in large samples—look at the actual size of the deviation and its impact.**

## One-Phrase Summary

> **Don't panic when the model doesn't fit—it's the mirror revealing the truth. First, determine whether it's local or global: local items can be fixed, deleted, or rerun (most common); global issues require checking dimensions (forcing multidimensional data into a unidimensional model is the top suspect), model selection, local dependencies, and data quality. Also remember that in large samples, tests become overly sensitive—don't only look at significance, but also the actual size of the deviation. Judging fit is a combined judgment, not an automatic switch.**
