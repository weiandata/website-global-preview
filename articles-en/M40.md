# 40 · Difficulty b and Discrimination a: How to Judge Whether a Question is "Good"

> The previous article taught you how to read the parameter table, and this one focuses on a single question: combining a and b, how to judge whether "this question is a good or bad one." It provides you with an actionable framework for judgment.

## First, Break a Misconception: There Is No Absolute "Good Question"

Whether a question is good or not **cannot be judged in isolation from the purpose of the test**. The same question can be a treasure in one project and a waste in another.

Therefore, judging whether a question is good or bad involves answering two levels of questions:

1. **Is the question itself "effective"?** —— This mainly depends on the discrimination a.
2. **Is the question used in the right context?** —— Check whether the difficulty b aligns with your target population.

The first is the "internal strength" of the question, and the second is "using the right tool for the right job." We will discuss them separately.

## Internal Strength: Discrimination a —— The Hard Indicator of Good or Bad

Discrimination is the **only parameter with a clear good or bad distinction** (Chapter 10). It measures the "discriminatory power" of the question; the higher, the better.

Here is a rough reference (experience range, not a rule):

| Discrimination a | Rough Evaluation |
| --- | --- |
| Negative | Alarm! The question is wrong, must be checked (Chapter 46) |
| Close to 0 ~ 0.4 | Too weak, almost no discriminatory power, consider eliminating |
| 0.4 ~ 0.8 | Average, usable but not impressive |
| 0.8 ~ 1.5 | Good, decent discriminatory power |
| Above 1.5 | Excellent, very strong discriminatory power |

**Remember**: a is a hard indicator. If it is too low, it is a serious flaw, regardless of what it is measuring or where it is used. **Questions with low discrimination should be suspected first and eliminated later.**

## Using the Right Tool: Difficulty b —— Appropriateness Matters More Than Level

Difficulty has no good or bad; it only has **appropriateness** (Chapter 9). The standard is: **Does it fall within the level range of your target population?**

- If you are measuring a group of people with moderate ability, then a question with b near 0 is appropriate; a question with b=3 is almost useless for them (everyone gets it wrong, no information is obtained).
- If you are making a judgment at the passing level (e.g., θ=0), you most need questions with b near 0 (the information peak is exactly on the passing line, Chapter 12).
- If you want to cover the entire population, you need a set of questions with b values ranging from low to high (Chapters 13, 15).

**Therefore, the question "Is a question with b=2 good?" is meaningless in itself.** You should ask, "Does my test need a question with difficulty b=2?" If it does, it is good; if not, it is redundant even if it is well-designed.

## Combining a and b: The Four-Quadrant Judgment Method

By crossing discrimination (is it effective) and difficulty appropriateness (is it used in the right context), a question falls into one of four quadrants:

| | Difficulty Appropriate (Within Target Range) | Difficulty Inappropriate (Deviation from Target Range) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **High Discrimination** | ⭐ Good question, keep for emphasis | Good topic, but used in the wrong context — keep it, use it in another project or a different difficulty range |
| **Low Discrimination** | Correct position but no discriminatory power — modify or eliminate | Not useful and not appropriate — prioritize elimination |

**Interpretation**:

- **Top-left (High Discrimination + Difficulty Appropriate)**: This is the ideal good question, the backbone of the test.
- **Top-right (High Discrimination + Difficulty Inappropriate)**: The question itself is good, but it is not currently applicable. Don't delete it! Store it in the item bank, as it can be used for testing other populations in the future (this is the value of an item bank, Chapter 44).
- **Bottom-left (Low Discrimination + Difficulty Appropriate)**: The position is correct, but it lacks discriminatory power. First, check if it can be modified (ambiguous question stem? Poor options?), and if not, eliminate it.
- **Bottom-right (Low Discrimination + Difficulty Inappropriate)**: Both useless and inappropriate — eliminate it first.

## Don't Forget to Discount the Judgment: Standard Error and Fit

The above judgments are based on the premise that the parameters are estimated accurately. Therefore, before making a conclusion, perform two actions (Chapters 21, 22):

- **Check the standard error**: If the standard errors of a and b are large, the "goodness" of the question is not accurately determined. Don't rush to make a judgment; it may be due to insufficient calibration.
- **Check the fit**: A poorly fitting question, even if the a and b values look good, indicates it "doesn't follow the rules." It should be closely examined to determine whether it measures something else or has dependencies.

## One-Sentence Summary

> **Judging whether a question is good involves two levels: discrimination a is the hard indicator (the higher, the better; low is a serious flaw), and difficulty b only matters in terms of appropriateness (as long as it falls within your target population). Use the four-quadrant method: high discrimination + appropriate difficulty is valuable, high discrimination + inappropriate difficulty should be stored in the item bank, and low discrimination questions should be modified or eliminated. Finally, don't forget to discount the judgment using standard error and fit.**
