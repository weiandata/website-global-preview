## Why "Literacy" Is Difficult to Measure

Scientific literacy, digital literacy, reading literacy, financial literacy, and more—over the past two decades, "literacy" (literacy/competency) has become a key term in large-scale assessments. The distinction between literacy and traditional "knowledge tests" is not just a name change: **literacy is "the ability to apply knowledge to solve problems in real-world contexts."** Each component of this definition poses challenges for measurement:

- "Real-world context" means that items must be contextualized, but context introduces construct-irrelevant variance (reading burden, differences in life experience);
- "Application" means that it is not just about memory; cognitive levels must be built into the scoring criteria;
- "Ability" means it is a latent variable—unobservable directly, and can only be inferred from response behavior.

Over the past two decades, projects like PISA and PIAAC have accumulated methodological insights essentially answering the question: **"How can literacy be measured effectively?"** Below, we extract key points by process.

## Key Point 1: Framework First—Define What Is Being Measured Before Discussing How to Measure It

Serious literacy assessments always begin with an **assessment framework**: the dimensional structure of the construct (e.g., "knowledge—ability—attitude" or a "content × process × context" matrix), behavioral descriptions for each dimension, and item composition blueprints. The framework is the constitution for everything that follows: item development follows it, models are set based on it, and reports are structured according to it.

**Without an open framework, a "literacy assessment" cannot be evaluated methodologically**—you even don't know what it claims to measure. Conversely, framework revisions (literacy conceptions change with the times, such as digital literacy changing every ten years) must be accompanied by bridging studies, otherwise trend data will be broken.

## Key Point 2: The Double-Edged Sword of Contextualized Item Development

Context makes measurement closer to "real-world application," but it also brings three risks that must be managed:

1. **Reading Ability Contamination**: Long contextual texts may make the assessment focus on reading rather than the target literacy. Control measures: text length standards, readability indicators;
2. **Contextual Fairness**: Rural students may not have seen a subway ticket machine, while urban students may not have encountered farming—context selection is a fairness decision. Defense: diverse item development teams, fairness expert review, and DIF checks as a safety net;
3. **Context-Dependent Local Dependence**: A set of items within the same context (testlet) violates the assumption of local independence. Models either model the testlet effect or control the proportion of item sets during test composition.

## Key Point 3: Multidimensional Reporting and Measurement Models

A literacy framework is inherently multidimensional, and the technical choices are discussed in the previous section (Multidimensional IRT): multidimensional item structures + joint modeling support + multidimensional reporting. The uniqueness of literacy assessments lies in the **mixing of attitude/behavioral dimensions with cognitive dimensions**—attitude scales (Likert) and cognitive items (right/wrong or partial scoring) belong to different model families (GPCM/GRM vs 2PL/GPCM), and the interpretation of reporting scales must be differentiated ("more positive attitude" vs "higher ability" are not the same "higher").

## Key Point 4: Matrix-Based Test Composition and Techniques for Group Inference

The content covered by a literacy framework far exceeds what an individual can handle, so matrix-based test composition (each person only completes one booklet) has become standard. This brings a full set of supporting techniques: cross-booklet equating (anchor item network), latent regression, plausible values (PV), and resampling variance estimation. Each link in this technical chain has been discussed in previous articles—literacy assessments are the final assembly of these techniques. **This chain also marks the technical dividing line between "professional assessments" and "large-scale questionnaires."**

## Key Point 5: Grade-Based Reporting and Standard Setting

Social communication of literacy results almost always relies on grades ("the proportion of students reaching the basic literacy level is X%"). Grade line setting requires a standard-setting procedure (Bookmark is mainstream, see the line marking article), and grade descriptions (proficiency level descriptors) must align with the framework's behavioral descriptions—"what people at level three can do" must be supported by empirical evidence (the actual pass rate of people at that level on corresponding items), not literary creation.

## Key Point 6: Trends Are the Most Valuable Asset and the Most Fragile

The core value of literacy monitoring lies in **trends** (whether it is improving year by year), and the requirements for methodological stability are almost stringent: anchor item confidentiality and drift detection, bridging design for framework revisions, consistency of sampling and weighting schemes across years, and even changes in testing modes (paper-and-pencil to computer-based) must undergo mode effect studies. **Every "small improvement" can create a false turning point on the trend line.** Change management (what can be changed, how to change, and how to bridge) is a core issue in long-term project governance.
