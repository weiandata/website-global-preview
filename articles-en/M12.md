# 12 · Information Function Curve: Where Is This Item Most Useful?

> The ICC tells you "how hard this item is and how well it distinguishes people." The information function answers a more practical question: where is this item most cost-effective to use?

## First, Build an Intuition: The "Sweet Spot" of an Item

A tennis racket has a "sweet spot" (sweet spot), where the ball hits with the least effort and maximum accuracy.

An item also has a sweet spot. **An item is not equally useful for all levels of ability; it has a specific range of ability where it is most effective at measuring.** The information function is the curve that maps out this sweet spot.

- **X-axis**: Still ability θ, ranging from -3 to +3.
- **Y-axis**: The "amount of information" this item provides at the corresponding ability level (which can be understood as "how accurate or useful the measurement is").

The curve typically looks like a **small hill**: high in the middle and low on both sides. The peak of the hill is the sweet spot of the item.

## Where Is the Peak? — Right Near the Item Difficulty

The amount of information an item provides reaches its maximum when the test-taker's ability is approximately equal to the item's difficulty.

Why? Recall the steepest part of the ICC — it is near the difficulty b (the point where half are correct and half are incorrect). At this point, small changes in ability lead to large changes in the probability of answering correctly. The item is most sensitive to the test-taker's actual ability level and has the strongest discrimination, naturally providing the most accurate and informative measurement.

In contrast, far from the difficulty (e.g., giving a group of top students an easy question), everyone answers correctly, and the probability of answering correctly remains at the ceiling. This item provides almost no new information — it's at the base of the hill.

**In one sentence**: **An item is most effective at measuring people whose ability is similar to its difficulty.**

Too difficult an item cannot accurately measure low-ability individuals (they all get it wrong, and there's no way to distinguish them), and too easy an item cannot accurately measure high-ability individuals (they all get it right, and there's no way to distinguish them).

## How High and Wide Is the Hill? — It Depends on Discrimination

- **High discrimination (steep ICC)**: The information hill is tall and narrow — it measures very accurately within the sweet spot, but the sweet spot is very narrow, and it quickly loses effectiveness once you move away. It's a "specialist expert."
- **Low discrimination (flat ICC)**: The information hill is short and flat — it measures poorly at all ability levels, but there's no clear sweet spot. It's a "jack-of-all-trades, master of none."

Thus, the information function incorporates both aspects of the ICC: **difficulty determines the location of the hill, and discrimination determines the height and width of the hill.**

## How Is This Used in Practice?

**Use Case 1: Assign Items to Key Ability Levels.**
Suppose your test aims to clearly differentiate people around the cut score (e.g., θ = 0) — passing the cut score gets you a certificate, failing means retaking. In that case, you should specifically select a set of items with **difficulty near 0 and high discrimination**, so that their information hills all peak around the cut score. This way, the area around the cut score is measured accurately and reliably, and the judgment is robust.

Conversely, if all your items' information hills peak elsewhere (e.g., all are difficult, with peaks at θ = 2), then the area around the cut score is actually measured poorly, and your "pass/fail" judgment is uncertain.

**Use Case 2: Eliminate Items That Contribute Little Information.**
If you have two items you want to keep, but the number of items is limited and test-takers' time is precious, how do you choose? Look at which item provides more information at the ability levels you care about. Items with short and off-target information hills should be prioritized for removal.

**Use Case 3: Select Items for Computerized Adaptive Testing.**
The core logic of computerized adaptive testing (covered in Chapter 28) is: based on your current estimated ability level, select an item whose information hill peaks exactly at your ability level — each item hits your sweet spot, so you can measure accurately with very few items. This mechanism is precisely built on top of the information function.

## An Often Overlooked Point: Information Can Be "Accumulated"

A single item's information hill may not be very high, but **information can be accumulated** — by summing the information hills of multiple items at each ability level, you get the total information of the entire test. This is what we'll discuss in the next chapter: the test information curve.

Because information can be accumulated, even if each item is not perfect, as long as their sweet spots collectively cover the ability range you care about, the entire test can still measure accurately and comprehensively. **The art of test construction is largely about combining information hills.**

## Final Summary in One Sentence

> **The information function is an item's "sweet spot map": the peak is near the item's difficulty (most accurate when ability ≈ difficulty), and the height and width of the hill are determined by discrimination. If you want to measure accurately in a certain group, select items whose information hills peak in that area.**
