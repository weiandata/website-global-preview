# 14 · What Does -3 to +3 on the Ability Scale Mean?

> We frequently use that scale from -3 to +3. This article will explain it thoroughly: what these numbers really mean, why there are negative numbers, and how to explain it to people who are not familiar with the concept.

## First, Let's Clear Up a Misconception: This Is Not "Scores"

When you see θ = -1.5, many people's first thought is "negative points? Deducted points?".

No, θ is a scale that **has no units, no maximum score, and no zero score meaning**. It's more like Celsius on a thermometer: 0°C doesn't mean "no temperature," but rather a reference point; -10°C doesn't mean "negative heat," but rather colder than the reference point.

θ is the same. **0 is not "zero ability," but rather a reference point.** Negative numbers don't mean "negative ability," but rather "below the reference point."

## How to Read These Numbers

θ usually falls between -3 and +3, and you can roughly interpret it as follows (remember, this is an approximation and varies depending on the item setting):

- **θ = 0**: Average level, standing in the middle of the population.
- **θ > 0**: Above the average level, the higher the number, the stronger the ability. +1 is "not bad," +2 is "quite excellent," and +3 is "rarely seen at the top."
- **θ < 0**: Below the average level, the smaller the number, the weaker the ability. -1 is "somewhat weak," -2 is "significantly behind," and -3 is "at the bottom."

Why is it specifically -3 to +3? Because this scale is designed so that the average ability level of the population is **set at 0, and most people fall within ±3** (statistically, this is equivalent to setting the mean at 0 and the standard deviation at 1, so over 99% of people fall within ±3). Very few people go beyond ±3, so the scale is usually drawn only up to -3 and +3.

## An Important Property: It Is "Equidistant"

This scale has an advantage that total scores do not: **the intervals are uniform**.

In a regular 100-point scale, the increase from 95 to 100 is not the same as from 55 to 60, even though both are "an increase of 5 points." The value of each point is not equal, especially at the extremes, where the scale is compressed.

The θ scale is designed so that the increase from 0.5 to 1.0 is the same as from 1.5 to 2.0 in terms of ability. This makes comparisons like "how much progress was made" or "how much difference is there between two groups" more meaningful. That's why large-scale monitoring reports often use the θ scale for trend analysis.

## Why Clients Still Don't Understand

Because ordinary people have no concept of "your scientific literacy θ is 0.83" — negative numbers, decimals, and no units are all off-putting.

Therefore, in practice, θ is almost never presented to clients as is. Instead, a **scale transformation** is usually done: a simple linear transformation (multiplying by a number and adding a number) is used to convert θ into a more understandable score. The most common is to convert it into a scale like "average 500, standard deviation 100" (as PISA and many large-scale assessments do):

- θ = 0 → Report score 500 (average)
- θ = 1 → Report score 600
- θ = -1 → Report score 400

After conversion, the numbers are all positive, whole hundreds, and have a sense of reference. Clients can understand "my child scores 560, which is above the average of 500." **θ is an internal working scale used for "production measurement," while the report score is an external communication scale used for "presentation."** The two are directly related, just in different forms (see Chapter 42 for details on how to convert and explain it).

## A Few Frequently Asked Questions

**Question: Can θ exceed 3 or be less than -3?**
Yes, but it's very rare. A few exceptional cases, such as extremely talented individuals or those with extreme deficiencies, may fall outside of ±3, but they are usually not shown on the scale.

**Question: Is θ = 1 the same for two different items?**
Not necessarily! The "where 0 is and what 1 represents" depends on the calibration settings of each item. θ values from different items cannot be directly compared unless they are equated to the same scale (see Chapter 44 for item comparability in item banks). This is often overlooked, so be careful.

**Question: Is θ accurate?**
It is an estimate with error (as discussed in Chapters 4 and 21). So strictly speaking, a person's ability is not "θ = 1.2" as a single point, but rather "a range around 1.2." When reporting, it's common to include the error range as well.

## Final Summary in One Sentence

> **The θ scale is like a thermometer: 0 is a reference point, not "zero ability," and negative numbers just mean "below the reference point." Its advantage is that the intervals are uniform and comparable; its disadvantage is that it's too abstract for external communication, so it's converted into report scores like 500 ± 100 for clients.**
