# 24 · A New Face of "Reliability" in IRT — It Changes with People

> Reliability is an old concept in measurement. Anyone who has done a questionnaire has heard of the "reliability coefficient α." However, in IRT, reliability takes on a new form. This article explains this upgrade clearly.

## First, a Review: What Is Reliability?

Reliability (reliability), in plain terms, means **"how stable and reliable this measurement is."**

A classic criterion for judgment: **Does the same group of people, using the same tool, produce consistent results when measured repeatedly?** High consistency = high reliability = reliable; measuring one day and getting a different result the next = low reliability = unreliable.

It's like a scale. If you weigh the same bag of rice three times and it's always 5.00 kg, the scale is reliable (high reliability); if it fluctuates between 4.8 and 5.3, the scale is not trustworthy (low reliability).

## The Traditional Face of Reliability: A Single Number

In traditional methods (CTT), reliability is **a single number**, the most famous being **Cronbach's α**, which typically ranges from 0 to 1, with higher values being better (empirically, values above 0.7 are considered acceptable, and above 0.8 are considered good).

This number is convenient — a questionnaire reports a single α, clean and straightforward. However, it has the same flaw as the "scale" mentioned in the first article — **it pretends to be equally reliable for everyone**. It gives you a "total reliability" for the entire test, as if measuring a top student and a struggling student is equally accurate. But reality doesn't work that way.

## IRT's New Face: Reliability Is No Longer a Single Number, but a Curve

IRT views reliability in a way that is consistent with how it views precision (as discussed in Articles 13 and 21). It says:

> **Reliability varies with ability — it is reliable for one group of people and unreliable for another.**

Therefore, in IRT, the complete answer to the question "How reliable is this test?" is not a single number, but rather **a curve that varies with ability** (which is another expression of the information curve):

- **High information levels** → small standard error → **reliable measurement for that group.**
- **Low information levels** → large standard error → **unreliable measurement for that group.**

This is the honest view of reliability: **different people are measured with different levels of reliability by the same test.** There is a lot of information near the cut score, so people near the cut score are measured reliably; there is little information at the extremes, so people at the extremes are measured inaccurately.

## Does IRT Still Provide a "Single Number" for Reliability?

Yes, to align with traditional practices and for convenience in reporting, IRT also has a "reliability" equivalent that is condensed into a single number:

- **Marginal reliability**: This condenses the ability-varying reliability into a single reliability coefficient by weighting it according to the population distribution, making it easier to compare with traditional α.
- **Empirical reliability / separation reliability (commonly used in the Rasch tradition)**: This follows a similar idea, measuring "how well this measurement separates people."

So you can understand it this way: **IRT can give you the honest "reliability curve" (full detail), and it can also condense it into a "single reliability number" when needed for reporting.** The former is its advancement, and the latter is a consideration for traditional habits.

## How to Use This "New Face" in Practice

**1. Don't be satisfied with "α = 0.85 is good."**
A high α for a test does not mean it is reliable for the group you care about. If you are making judgments near the cut score, you should specifically look at whether the **reliability/information near the cut score** is sufficient, rather than being misled by a nice overall mean. This is the most practical aspect of IRT's view of reliability.

**2. Use it to guide item development.**
If you find that reliability is insufficient for a certain ability level, add high-quality items for that segment (as discussed in Article 13). Reliability is no longer a one-time deal of "either meets the standard or not," but a fine-tuned process of "where it's lacking, add there."

**3. Explain clearly who the reliability applies to when reporting.**
Professional reports will state "This test has sufficient reliability for the middle ability range, but limited reliability for extreme ability ranges," rather than just providing a total coefficient. This is both honest and helps clients use the results correctly.

## A Common Follow-Up Question: Is Reliability the Same as Validity?

No, don't confuse them.

- **Reliability**: Is the measurement stable and reliable (does it give the same result when measured three times)?
- **Validity**: Is the measurement accurate and does it measure what you intend to measure (is this scale measuring weight, or have you mistakenly used it to measure length)?

A scale can be very stable (high reliability) but always overweigh by 2 kg (low validity) — it's consistently wrong. So high reliability **does not equal** accurate measurement. Validity is another major topic (Article A07 in the current content library focuses on this), and here you just need to remember: **Reliability concerns "stability," and validity concerns "accuracy." Both are needed, neither can be missing.**

## One-Sentence Conclusion

> **Reliability = whether the measurement is stable and reliable. Traditional methods give a single number (α), pretending to be equally reliable for everyone. IRT gives a curve that varies with ability, honestly stating "reliable for some groups, unreliable for others." When needed, it can also be condensed into a single number. The key to using it is — check whether the group you care about is reliable, don't be misled by the overall mean. Reliability concerns "stability," and validity concerns "accuracy," don't confuse them.**
