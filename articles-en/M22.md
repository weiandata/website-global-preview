# 22 · "Goodness of Fit": Does the Model and Data "Fit Well" Together?

> IRT is a model, a model is a simplification of reality. This article explains how to check whether "the model you chose fits well with real data" — if this check fails, all previous results are meaningless.

## First, Set a Rule: The Model Must "Match" the Data

IRT models (those S-shaped curves, that set of parameters) are a set of **assumptions about how people answer questions**. It assumes: the higher the ability, the more likely the answer is correct, the probability of correct answers follows a certain S-shaped curve, items are locally independent, and only one latent variable is at play...

The question is — **do real people really answer questions according to this script?**

Sometimes yes, sometimes no. **Goodness of fit** (goodness of fit) answers this question: how well does the model's "theoretical script" match people's actual answering behavior? If they match well, it's called "good fit"; if they don't match, it's called "poor fit."

## A Tailor's Analogy

A model is like a ready-made suit made according to standard measurements.

- Worn on someone with a standard body shape, it fits well — **good fit**.
- Worn on someone with an unusual body shape, it's tight here and loose there, sleeves are too long — **poor fit**.

Goodness of fit checks are like letting the "model suit" fit on the "data person" to see where it's tight or loose. If it's too tight or too loose, it means the suit (model) doesn't fit this data, and you need to change the size (change the model) or adjust the suit (modify the items or assumptions).

## Two Levels of "Fit"

Goodness of fit should be viewed from two levels, not mixed up:

**1. Item Fit (Does each item fit well).**
Check each item: does the actual performance match the S-shaped curve the model assigns to it?

- Items with poor fit are often "outliers": they might be measuring something else (violating unidimensionality), they might be colluding with other items (violating local independence), or they might be ambiguous, have errors, or have been leaked.
- Handling: items with poor fit should be closely examined, and deleted or modified if necessary (Chapter 41 will discuss how to identify bad items). **Item fit is an important probe for identifying problematic items.**

**2. Person Fit (Does each person fit the pattern).**
Check each person: does the person's answering pattern match the common sense that "higher ability people should answer difficult items correctly, lower ability people should answer easy items correctly"?

- People with poor fit are often "oddities": they might get all easy items wrong and all difficult items right (possibly guessing, cheating, or just randomly filling in), or their answers might be completely random.
- Handling: poor person fit is a clue for data cleaning and removing invalid responses (Chapter 49 will discuss this in detail). **Person fit is an important probe for identifying careless or cheating responses.**

## Some Terms You Might Hear (Just Recognize the Faces)

Goodness of fit has a lot of indicators and statistics, with names that vary widely. Just let professionals handle the analysis, and here's a quick overview to get you familiar with the terms and not be scared:

- **Chi-square type tests** (e.g., S-X², G²): compare the difference between the "actual correct/incorrect distribution" and the "model-predicted distribution." A significant difference indicates poor fit.
- **Infit / Outfit** (commonly used in the Rasch tradition): give each item and each person a "fit index," usually around 1 is considered normal, too high indicates "too much noise," and too low indicates "too much consistency" (possibly due to dependency or collusion).
- **Residuals**: the "difference" between actual performance and model prediction. If there's a pattern in the residuals (e.g., two items always have large residuals together), it means the model hasn't captured something.
- **Overall Fit Indexes** (e.g., M2, RMSEA, CFI, etc., also common in factor analysis): give the entire model a "general fit score."

You don't need to remember the algorithms of each, just know: **they all answer the same question — does the model fit the data?**

## A Key Mindset: Poor Fit Is Not "Failure," It's "Information"

Newcomers often panic when they see "poor fit." In fact, the value of goodness of fit checks lies precisely in **honestly telling you where things are wrong**:

- Are a few items not fitting well? → Review those items.
- Are many people answering randomly? → Check data quality and whether there are many careless responses.
- Is the entire model systematically not fitting? → Maybe the dimension was chosen incorrectly (trying to fit a multidimensional model to a unidimensional one), or maybe the model should be changed (using a 2PL model instead of a 1PL model).

**Goodness of fit checks are the "mirror that reveals the demon" for the model. Revealing problems is its core function.** An analysis that skips goodness of fit checks and directly uses parameters is truly dangerous (Chapter 47 will specifically discuss "what to do if the model doesn't fit").

## One Final Summary

> **Goodness of fit checks whether the "model suit" fits the "data person." It checks two levels: item fit (to identify problematic items) and person fit (to identify careless or cheating responses). Poor fit is not failure; it's the mirror revealing where to make adjustments. Skipping goodness of fit checks and directly using results is truly dangerous.**
